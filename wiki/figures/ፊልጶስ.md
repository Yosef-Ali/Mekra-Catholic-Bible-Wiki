## Biography

**ቅዱስ ፊልጶስ (Saint Philip)** was from **Bethsaida** in Galilee — the same town as Andrew and Peter (ዮሐ. 1:44). His name is Greek (*"lover of horses"*), suggesting Hellenistic influence. He was the **third apostle Christ called**, after Andrew and Peter:

> «ኢየሱስ ፊልጶስን አገኘውና "ተከተለኝ" አለው።»
> — ዮሐ. 1:43

Like Andrew, Philip immediately went to find another — his friend **Nathanael** (Bartholomew):

> «ሙሴ በኦሪት ነቢያትም ስለ እርሱ የጻፉትን አግኝተናል — ናዝሬታዊውን ኢየሱስን — የዮሴፍን ልጅ።»
> — ዮሐ. 1:45

When Nathanael objected — *"Can anything good come out of Nazareth?"* — Philip's answer is the catechist's perfect reply: *"Come and see."*

## Three Philip moments in John

### 1. The feeding of the 5,000 (ዮሐ. 6:5–7)
Christ tests Philip: *"Where shall we buy bread that these may eat?"* Philip's answer is the realist's: *"Two hundred denarii would not be enough for each to have a little."* He sees the impossibility — Christ uses it to show his power.

### 2. The Greeks who wished to see Jesus (ዮሐ. 12:20–22)
Greek-speakers approach Philip first — perhaps because his Greek name made him the natural bridge. He brings them through Andrew to Christ.

### 3. *"Show us the Father"* (ዮሐ. 14:8–9)
At the Last Supper, Philip asks plainly: *"Lord, show us the Father, and it will be enough for us."* Christ's reply is one of the deepest Trinitarian sayings in all Scripture:

> «ፊልጶስ — እኔን ያየ — አብን አየ።»
> — ዮሐ. 14:9

The Father is not other than what is revealed in the Son.

## Mission and martyrdom

Tradition places Philip's later ministry in **Phrygia (modern Turkey)**. He was martyred at **Hierapolis** — crucified upside down according to some accounts, stoned according to others. His tomb was reportedly discovered there in 2011.

He should not be confused with **Philip the Deacon / Evangelist** (የሐዋ. ሥራ 6:5, 8:5–40) — a different man, one of the seven deacons, who baptized **the Ethiopian eunuch** (የሐዋ. ሥራ 8:26–40). The Ethiopian eunuch's baptism — by Philip the Deacon, not Philip the Apostle — is one of the foundational moments of Ethiopian Christian identity.
