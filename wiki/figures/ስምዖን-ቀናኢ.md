## Biography

**ቅዱስ ስምዖን ቀናኢ (Saint Simon the Zealot)** is one of the **least mentioned** but most fascinating of the apostles. His epithets — *"the Zealot"* in Luke and Acts, *"the Cananaean"* in Matthew and Mark — both render the same Aramaic word *qana'ān*, meaning *"zealous"* or *"jealous for God."*

This identifies him with — or at least associates him with — the **Zealots**, the militant Jewish nationalist movement that resisted Roman occupation, sometimes by violence. They believed only God should be Israel's king and refused to pay tribute to Caesar.

The remarkable detail: **the same apostolic college contained Matthew the tax collector and Simon the Zealot.** A Jew who collaborated with Rome and a Jew who would have killed for resisting Rome — both at the same Last Supper, sharing the same cup. Christ's first miracle of reconciliation: he made them brothers.

## In Scripture

Simon is named in every list of the Twelve — but speaks no recorded word in the Gospels:

- ማቴ. 10:4 (Simon the Cananaean)
- ማር. 3:18 (Simon the Cananaean)
- ሉቃ. 6:15 (Simon called the Zealot)
- የሐዋ. ሥራ 1:13 (Simon the Zealot — present at the upper room before Pentecost)

Five mentions, no recorded sayings — yet listed always among the Twelve. The Christian who never preaches a famous sermon and yet is faithful to the end is the Simon model.

## Mission and martyrdom

Tradition pairs Simon with **Saint Jude Thaddaeus** in mission, preaching together in **Egypt, Mesopotamia, and Persia**. Both were martyred together in Persia — Simon traditionally by being **sawn in two** (his iconographic symbol is a saw).

The Latin Church celebrates their joint feast on **October 28**. The Ge'ez tradition similarly honors them together.

## What his life teaches

Simon's invisibility in the New Testament is, for the Christian, a **comfort and challenge**:

- **Comfort:** holiness does not require fame. Simon is a saint without a famous sermon, miracle, or epistle. His sanctity is in his fidelity, not his prominence.
- **Challenge:** the zeal that once turned to politics turned to Christ. Whatever fire we have — Simon's example asks: have we redirected it from the kingdoms of this world to the Kingdom of God?

## The lesson of mixed apostles

The presence of Simon **alongside** Matthew teaches the catechist that **the Catholic Church is not a political party**. She holds together those who would, in the world, be enemies. The unity is not in shared politics but in shared faith. This is the original miracle of ecumenism.
