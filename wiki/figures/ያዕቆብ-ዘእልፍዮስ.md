## Biography

**ቅዱስ ያዕቆብ ዘእልፍዮስ (Saint James, son of Alphaeus)** — called **"the Less"** to distinguish him from James the Greater (son of Zebedee) — is one of the **most debated identities** in Christian history. Three figures appear in the New Testament under the name James:

1. **James the Greater** — son of Zebedee, brother of John (already covered: [[figures/ያዕቆብ]]).
2. **James the Less** — son of Alphaeus, listed among the Twelve.
3. **James the Just** — *"the brother of the Lord"* (ገላ. 1:19), first bishop of Jerusalem, author of the Epistle of James.

Whether James the Less and James the Just are **the same person** or **two different people** has been debated for centuries. The dominant Western tradition identifies them; many Eastern traditions distinguish them.

## What we know either way

### As one of the Twelve

He appears in every apostolic list:

- ማቴ. 10:3 (*"James the son of Alphaeus"*)
- ማር. 3:18; ሉቃ. 6:15; የሐዋ. ሥራ 1:13

His mother **Mary of Clopas** stood at the Cross with the Mother of God (ማቴ. 27:56; ማር. 15:40; ዮሐ. 19:25) and was among the women who witnessed the empty tomb (ማር. 16:1).

### As bishop of Jerusalem (the Western identification)

In Acts and Galatians, **James** appears as the head of the Jerusalem Church — presiding at the **Council of Jerusalem** (የሐዋ. ሥራ 15:13–21) where the question of Gentile circumcision was decided. Saint Paul calls him a *"pillar"* of the Church alongside Peter and John (ገላ. 2:9).

He was a man of remarkable holiness — called *"the Just"* by his contemporaries. Tradition (preserved by Hegesippus through Eusebius) records that:

- He spent so much time on his knees in prayer that they grew calloused like a camel's.
- He was a Nazirite — drinking no wine, eating no meat, never cutting his hair.
- He alone of the apostles was permitted to enter the **Holy of Holies** of the Jewish Temple to pray for his people.

### As author of the Epistle of James

The **Letter of James** in the New Testament is attributed to him. It is the most concentrated **practical** book of the Bible — the book Martin Luther disliked because it insists faith without works is dead (ያዕ. 2:24). The Catholic tradition holds it as one of the great correctives to misunderstood Pauline teaching. See [[apologetics/sola-fide]].

## Martyrdom

Tradition records that James the Just was martyred in **Jerusalem around 62 AD** — thrown from the pinnacle of the Temple by the high priest Ananus, then clubbed to death when the fall did not kill him. As he died, he prayed for his executioners — like Stephen, like his Lord.

The Jewish historian **Josephus** records his death in his *Antiquities of the Jews* — one of the few cases where a Christian martyrdom is documented in non-Christian sources of the period.
