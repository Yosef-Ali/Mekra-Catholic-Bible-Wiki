## Biography

**ቅዱስ ማቴዎስ (Saint Matthew)** — also called **Levi** in Mark and Luke — was a **tax collector** at Capernaum, working for the Roman occupation. Tax collectors were despised in Jewish society as collaborators and extortionists; the term *"tax collectors and sinners"* became proverbial.

Christ called him with two words:

> «ተከተለኝ።»
> — ማቴ. 9:9

Matthew rose, left his tax booth, and followed. He then hosted Jesus at a great banquet in his home — inviting his fellow tax collectors and sinners. The Pharisees objected. Christ's response remains the most piercing summary of his mission:

> «ሐኪም የሚያስፈልጋቸው ጤነኞች አይደሉም — ሕሙማን ናቸው። …
> ምሕረትን እጠይቃለሁ — መሥዋዕትን አይደለም።»
> — ማቴ. 9:12–13

## The Gospel according to Matthew

Tradition unanimously attributes the **First Gospel** to him — written for a primarily **Jewish-Christian** audience, emphasizing:

- **Christ as the fulfillment of Old Testament prophecy** (over 60 explicit OT citations).
- **The genealogy** from Abraham to David to Christ (ማቴ. 1:1–17).
- **The Sermon on the Mount** — Christ as the new Moses giving the new Law (chapters 5–7).
- **The Kingdom of Heaven** — Matthew uses *"Kingdom of Heaven"* where Mark and Luke use *"Kingdom of God,"* honoring Jewish reluctance to pronounce the divine Name.
- **The Great Commission** — the Gospel's final words (28:16–20).

The Ethiopian tradition has venerated the Gospel of Matthew with special devotion since the very founding of Ethiopian Christianity — its themes of fulfillment and kingship resonating deeply with Ethiopian Christian identity.

## Mission and martyrdom

Tradition variously locates Matthew's later mission in **Ethiopia, Persia, or Parthia**. Some early sources — including the medieval Ethiopian *Synaxarium* — record that **Matthew preached in Ethiopia** and was martyred there. While this Ethiopian tradition is not independently confirmed by Western sources, it is held with reverence in the Ethiopian Christian heritage and adds a special bond between the Ethiopian Church and the Evangelist whose Gospel they so love.

His symbol in Christian iconography is **a winged man** — drawn from the four living creatures of Ezekiel and Revelation, signifying that Matthew's Gospel begins with Christ's human genealogy.

## A patron for the converted

Matthew is the patron of **bankers, accountants, tax collectors, and the converted** — those who left worldly success to follow Christ. His example teaches:

- **Christ calls the unworthy.** No past disqualifies a soul from grace.
- **Conversion is total.** Matthew did not negotiate — he rose and left.
- **The forgiven owe hospitality** — Matthew's first response to grace was to invite his old friends to meet his new Master.
