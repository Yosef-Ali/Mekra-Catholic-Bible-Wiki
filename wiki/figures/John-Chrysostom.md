## Biography

### Antioch and the desert

John was born in Antioch around 347 to a Christian widow, Anthusa, who refused remarriage to raise him. He studied rhetoric under the famous pagan teacher Libanius, who reportedly named John as the student he had wanted to succeed him "if the Christians had not stolen him." After baptism around age 20, John spent four years as a hermit in the mountains outside Antioch, living in a cave and memorizing the entire Bible. The ascetic regime broke his health permanently — he ate sparingly for the rest of his life and his stomach troubles began here.

### Priest of Antioch (386-397)

Ordained priest in Antioch in 386, John spent twelve years preaching there. His sermons drew massive crowds and were transcribed by stenographers — over 800 homilies survive, the largest body of patristic preaching from any single Father. His running commentaries on **Genesis, the Psalms, Matthew, John, Acts, and the Pauline Epistles** became the standard Greek Christian biblical exegesis for the next millennium. The byname **Χρυσόστομος ("Golden-Mouth", አፈወርቅ)** was attached to him posthumously by acclamation; in his lifetime he was simply "the preacher of Antioch."

### Patriarch of Constantinople (398-403)

In 397, Emperor Arcadius had John kidnapped from Antioch (the people would never have let him leave voluntarily) and consecrated him Patriarch of Constantinople. As patriarch, John attacked clerical luxury, sold off the patriarchal silverware to feed the poor, and preached repeatedly against the empress **Eudoxia** for what he considered her vanity and her appropriation of Church land. The conflict was inevitable.

### Exile and death

In 403, a synod packed with John's enemies (the "Synod of the Oak", chaired by Theophilus of Alexandria) deposed him on trumped-up charges. After a brief recall, he was exiled again in 404 to Cucusus in Armenia, where he continued writing and corresponding (his **Letters from Exile** are a major spiritual classic). When pilgrims continued to visit him, the emperor ordered him moved further — to Pityus on the eastern Black Sea coast. He died on the forced march, at Comana in Pontus, on September 14, 407. His last words: *"Δόξα τῷ Θεῷ πάντων ἕνεκεν"* ("Glory to God for all things").

### The Liturgy of Saint John Chrysostom

The **Divine Liturgy of Saint John Chrysostom** is the principal eucharistic liturgy of the Byzantine rite — used throughout the Eastern Catholic and Eastern Orthodox world to this day. The attribution is partly traditional (Chrysostom shaped the liturgy of Constantinople; the text crystallized over centuries), but the prayers reflect his theology and Antiochene exegetical sensibility. The **Anaphora of Saint John Chrysostom** is one of the principal anaphoras of the Ethiopian Orthodox and Ethiopian Catholic Qeddase, alongside the older Anaphora of the Apostles.

## Theological contributions

### 1. The preacher's vocation

Chrysostom defined Christian preaching as a sacred vocation parallel to the priesthood itself. His treatise *On the Priesthood* (*De Sacerdotio*) is still read in seminaries Catholic, Orthodox, and Protestant. The argument: the preacher must rise to the dignity of the Word he preaches, or the Word judges him.

### 2. Social justice and almsgiving

Chrysostom's homilies on the Rich Man and Lazarus, on Acts 4 (the apostolic community of goods), and on the parable of the Last Judgment (Matt 25) form the most sustained patristic teaching on the Church's obligation to the poor. He coined the principle later picked up by Catholic Social Teaching: *"Not to share one's goods with the poor is to rob them."*

### 3. Eucharistic realism

Against any merely symbolic reading: Christ in the Eucharist is the same Christ who walked Galilee. The Anaphora that bears his name expresses this in some of the strongest realist language in the Christian East — language Ethiopian Catholic theology of the Eucharist directly inherits.

## Why he matters for Ethiopian Catholics

1. **The Anaphora of Saint John Chrysostom is one of the canonical anaphoras of the Ethiopian Qeddase.** When an Ethiopian Catholic priest celebrates this anaphora, he prays words shaped by Chrysostom.
2. **Ethiopian preaching tradition (ድጓዊ ስብከት) is heir to the Antiochene tradition** that Chrysostom perfected — extended, exegetical, applied to daily life.
3. **His teaching on almsgiving** undergirds the Ethiopian Catholic emphasis on *ምሕረት* (mercy) as constitutive of Christian life, not optional.
4. **He is the patron of preachers** — the Ethiopian Catholic clergy invoke him in homiletic preparation.
