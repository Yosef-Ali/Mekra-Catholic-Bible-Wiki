# ተክለ ሃይማኖት (Tekle Haymanot)

**Type:** figure

**Variants:** Takla Haymanot, "Plant of the Faith"
**English:** Saint Tekle Haymanot

**Died:** 1313 (traditional) — Debre Libanos

## Biography

### From hunter's son to priest

Tekle Haymanot was born around 1215 in the region of Zorare in Shewa (central Ethiopia), the son of a priest named Tsega Zeab. Tradition holds that as a young man he was a skilled hunter; the conversion narrative in his *gadl* tells of an angel appearing to him in the forest and turning him from the hunt to the religious life. He was ordained priest by the Egyptian-born Metropolitan Abba Gerlos.

### The pilgrimage to Debre Damo and Jerusalem

Tekle Haymanot traveled north to Tigray to join the famous monastery of **Debre Damo**, founded in the 6th century by [[figures/Augustine]]'s contemporary Abuna Aregawi (one of the Nine Saints). At Debre Damo he received the monastic habit. He is said to have made the pilgrimage to Jerusalem, where he prayed at the Holy Sepulchre.

### Founding Debre Libanos (~1284)

Returning to Shewa, Tekle Haymanot established the monastic community that would become **ደብረ ሊባኖስ (Debre Libanos)** — for centuries the most influential monastery in Ethiopian Christianity. Debre Libanos became the seat of the *Echege* (the highest indigenous ecclesiastical office), second only to the Egyptian-appointed Metropolitan. Every Ethiopian Echege from the 13th century until 1959 came from Debre Libanos.

### The political role: the Solomonic restoration

Tekle Haymanot's life coincided with the **Solomonic dynasty's restoration** in 1270. Tradition credits him with mediating between **Yikuno Amlak** (the new Solomonic king) and the displaced Zagwe rulers, securing a peaceful transition and a grant of one-third of the kingdom's land to the Church. Whether the historical detail is exactly as the *gadl* records, Tekle Haymanot's spiritual authority shaped the Solomonic settlement that endured (with interruptions) until 1974.

### The standing penance

The most famous story of Tekle Haymanot: in his old age he undertook a vow of standing prayer, never sitting or lying down. After seven years of standing, **one of his legs broke off and fell to the ground.** He continued to pray standing on the remaining leg for another twenty-two years. The fallen leg is preserved as a relic in Debre Libanos. Ethiopian iconography always depicts ተክለ ሃይማኖት standing on one leg with wings, a pilgrim's staff, and the broken leg visible at his feet.

The story is hagiographic, not biographical in the modern sense — but it conveys the *theological* truth of his life: monastic prayer as a costly, embodied offering. The Compendium's teaching on Christian asceticism (Q565-571) finds its Ethiopian face in this image.

## Why he matters for Ethiopian Catholics

1. **He is the most popular saint in Ethiopian Christianity** after Mary. Every region has a parish named after him; every Ethiopian Christian can recognize his iconography.
2. **He shaped the institutional Ethiopian Church** through Debre Libanos and the Echege office for 700 years.
3. **The Ethiopian Catholic Church inherits his monastic tradition** — Ethiopian Catholic religious life draws directly from the Tekle Haymanot inheritance, even where it has been adapted to the post-Vatican II environment.
4. **He embodies the monastic vocation** that Christ named in Matt 19:21 — selling all to follow.

## In Scripture (the monastic call)

> «ኢየሱስም «ፍጹም መሆን ከፈለግህ፥ ሂድና ያለህን ሽጠህ ለድሆች ስጥ፤ በሰማያት መዝገብ ታገኛለህ፤ መጥተህም ተከተለኝ» አለው።»
> — የማቴዎስ ወንጌል 19:21

This verse is the foundation of Christian monasticism — quoted by Anthony of Egypt at his conversion, by Basil the Great in his Rule, and by Tekle Haymanot's *gadl* as the call he answered.
