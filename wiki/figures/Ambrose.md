## Biography

### Roman governor

Ambrose was born around 340 at Trier in Gaul, where his father served as Praetorian Prefect — among the highest civil offices in the Roman Empire. After his father's death, the family returned to Rome, where Ambrose received a thorough classical and legal education in Greek and Latin. By his early thirties he had risen to **governor (consularis) of Liguria and Aemilia**, with his seat at **Milan** — at that time the western imperial capital, more important than Rome itself.

### Acclaimed bishop (374)

In 374 the Arian bishop of Milan, Auxentius, died. The election to succeed him risked riots between the Nicene and Arian factions. Ambrose, as governor, came to the cathedral to keep the peace. According to the famous story, a child's voice cried out *"Ambrosius episcopus!"* ("Ambrose for bishop!") — and the cry was taken up by both factions at once. Ambrose was at that moment a **catechumen, not yet baptized**. Within eight days he was baptized, ordained through every minor and major order, and consecrated bishop on **December 7, 374**.

He gave away his vast personal fortune to the poor and the Church and never again held private property.

### Bishop of Milan (374-397)

Ambrose's twenty-three years as bishop reshaped Latin Christianity:

1. He **studied Greek theology systematically** (Origen, Basil, the Cappadocians, Athanasius) and translated their categories into Latin — making him the first major Latin Father whose theology is fully informed by the Greek tradition.
2. He **fought Arianism in the imperial court itself**, refusing to surrender Milanese basilicas to the Arian empress Justina (the famous siege of 386, when Ambrose and his people occupied a basilica and sang the night through — the origin of antiphonal singing in the Latin West).
3. He **wrote the first major Latin hymns**, the **Ambrosian hymns**, including *Aeterne rerum conditor*, *Deus Creator omnium*, *Veni redemptor gentium* (the Advent hymn still sung in Latin and Ethiopian Catholic Advent), and (by tradition) the *Te Deum* with Augustine. These hymns founded Christian hymnody as a genre.
4. He **wrote on the sacraments** — *De Mysteriis* and *De Sacramentis*, the earliest extended Latin treatises on baptism, confirmation, and the Eucharist; sources for nearly everything later Catholic sacramental theology will say.

### The Ambrosian rite

Ambrose reorganized the Milanese liturgy, producing what became (and remains) the **Ambrosian rite** — a distinctive Western liturgical tradition still celebrated in the Archdiocese of Milan and surrounding dioceses today. It is the only Western non-Roman rite in continuous use since antiquity. The Ambrosian rite has its own calendar, lectionary, eucharistic prayer, and hymnody — a sister rite to the Roman, the Mozarabic, and the various Eastern Catholic rites including the **Ge'ez rite of the Ethiopian Catholic Church**.

### Confronting Theodosius (390)

The most famous episode of Ambrose's episcopate. In 390, Emperor **Theodosius I**, in a fit of imperial rage over the murder of a military commander in **Thessalonica**, ordered the population summoned to the circus and **massacred — approximately 7,000 men, women, and children killed**. When Theodosius next attempted to enter the cathedral of Milan for the Eucharist, **Ambrose barred him at the door**, refused him communion, and demanded **public penance**. Theodosius — emperor of the Roman world — submitted. He removed his imperial robes, prostrated himself in the cathedral porch in sackcloth, and did penance publicly for **eight months** before being readmitted to communion at Christmas 390.

This is the founding episode of the Catholic principle that **the moral law binds rulers as it binds subjects** — that even the emperor cannot receive the Eucharist while soaked in innocent blood. Every later confrontation between bishop and ruler in Christian history (Becket and Henry II, Hildebrand and Henry IV, More and Henry VIII, Romero and the Salvadoran junta) descends from this moment.

### Baptizing Augustine (Easter Vigil 387)

In 384 a young North African rhetorician arrived in Milan as professor of imperial rhetoric: **Augustine**. Augustine came to hear Ambrose preach as a student of style; he stayed because Ambrose's allegorical readings of the Old Testament resolved his Manichaean objections. Over three years Augustine moved from skeptic to catechumen to candidate for baptism. **At the Easter Vigil of 387 (April 24-25, 387), Ambrose baptized Augustine in the Milanese baptistery** along with Augustine's son Adeodatus and friend Alypius.

The baptismal font of Milan, where this happened, has been excavated and is preserved beneath the modern Milan Cathedral. **The single most consequential baptism in Latin Christian history after the apostolic generation.** Through Augustine, Ambrose's theology shapes the entire Western Christian tradition — and through Augustine reaches Aquinas, Anselm, Aquinas, Luther, Trent, Newman, Vatican II.

### Death

Ambrose died at Milan on April 4, 397 — Holy Saturday — at about 57. His relics rest in the **Basilica of Sant'Ambrogio** in Milan, beside those of Saints Gervasius and Protasius (the martyrs whose relics he had discovered in 386).

## Theological contributions

### 1. Latin sacramental theology

Ambrose's *De Mysteriis* and *De Sacramentis* — based on his actual catechetical lectures to the newly baptized in Milan — are the earliest detailed Latin description of the Christian initiation rites: the scrutinies, the renunciation of Satan, the threefold immersion, the chrismation, the white garment, the lighted candle, the first Eucharist. The structure described there is the structure of every Catholic baptism today, including in Ethiopian Catholic parishes.

### 2. Christian hymnody

Ambrose effectively invented Latin Christian hymnody. The metrical pattern he used (iambic dimeter quatrains) became the standard Latin hymn meter for the next 1,500 years. *Veni redemptor gentium* is the founding Advent hymn of the Latin tradition; through Luther's German paraphrase it shaped Bach's *Nun komm der Heiden Heiland* and through that the entire Lutheran chorale tradition. **The Ethiopian Catholic Advent liturgy preserves Ambrosian hymnic material in Ge'ez and Amharic translation.**

### 3. Ethics of public office

Ambrose's *De Officiis Ministrorum* ("On the Duties of Ministers") is the founding Christian treatise on public ethics — modeled formally on Cicero's *De Officiis* but reorienting the four cardinal virtues toward Christian ends. Through Aquinas's reception of this text, Ambrose's ethics enter the Catholic moral tradition and shape Catholic Social Teaching's account of the obligations of public officials.

### 4. Allegorical exegesis of the Old Testament

Ambrose's homilies on the Patriarchs (*De Abraham*, *De Iacob*, *De Ioseph*) read the Old Testament Christologically and morally — the method that resolved Augustine's Manichaean revulsion toward the OT and made him a candidate for baptism. The Catholic principle of the **typological reading of the Old Testament** (Adam → Christ, Isaac → Christ, Joseph → Christ) descends through Ambrose into all later Latin exegesis.

## Why he matters for Ethiopian Catholics

1. **The Ge'ez liturgical tradition is structurally a sister rite** to the Ambrosian, the Roman, and the Mozarabic — all of them ancient apostolic Western and African rites that developed before the Roman rite assumed Western dominance. Ambrose's establishment of a regional Latin rite alongside Rome is the precedent for the Ge'ez rite's preservation alongside the Roman rite within Catholic communion.
2. **Ambrose's hymns** entered the Ge'ez Advent and Christmas offices through medieval translation channels (probably via the early Catholic missions from Portugal in the 16th-17th c. and re-confirmed in the modern Ethiopian Catholic missal).
3. **The confrontation with Theodosius** is invoked in Ethiopian Catholic teaching on the priesthood and on the sacrament of penance — the bishop's authority to bind and loose binds even the powerful.
4. **The baptism of Augustine at Easter 387** is the model invoked at every Ethiopian Catholic adult Easter Vigil baptism. Catechumens preparing for baptism at the Easter Vigil are walking in the footsteps of Augustine; their priest stands in Ambrose's place.

## Open questions

- Are Ambrose's hymns preserved in Ge'ez in their original metrical structure, or have they been re-set to Yaredic chant patterns?
- The Ambrosian rite's relationship to the Ge'ez rite at the level of liturgical structure: are there shared archaic elements that point to a common pre-Constantinian Western/African substrate?
