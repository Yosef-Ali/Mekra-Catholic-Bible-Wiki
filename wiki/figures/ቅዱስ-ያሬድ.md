# ቅዱስ ያሬድ (Saint Yared)

**Type:** figure
**Amharic:** ቅዱስ ያሬድ
**Variants:** Yared, Yared the Melodist, Yared Liqu
**English:** Saint Yared
**Born:** c. 505 — Aksum
**Died:** c. 571 — Semien Mountains (in seclusion, by tradition)

**Titles:** Father of Ethiopian Sacred Music, the Ethiopian Melodist, Liqu (Master)

## Biography

### A late beginning

Tradition holds that ቅዱስ ያሬድ was a slow learner as a child. After repeated frustrations in his early studies under his uncle Gedeon, he ran away into the wilderness and sat beneath a tree. There he watched a small caterpillar try repeatedly to climb the trunk, falling and trying again, until at last it reached the top. Yared took the lesson to heart, returned to his studies, and learned what was placed before him with a discipline that became proverbial. The story is preserved in his *gadl* (life) and is taught to Ethiopian children to this day.

### The three birds

Yared's gift for sacred music came, by tradition, through a vision. While praying, three birds appeared to him and carried him into the heavenly Jerusalem, where he heard the angels singing. He returned with the gift of sacred song — and with the foundational structure of all Ethiopian liturgical music: **three modes**, corresponding to the three birds.

| Mode | Amharic | Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Ge'ez** | ግዕዝ | Daily / weekday liturgy; the foundational mode |
| **Ezel** | እዝል | Lent, fasting seasons, funerals; sober and grave |
| **Araray** | አራራይ | Major feasts (ገና, ጥምቀት, ፋሲካ); festive and ornate |

Every chant in the Ethiopian liturgical tradition is composed in one of these three modes. The Ethiopian Catholic Church and the EOTC share the entire Yaredic musical inheritance.

### The king and the spear

The most famous Yared story: while Yared was chanting before King Gabra Masqal at court, he became so absorbed in the singing that he did not notice the king, leaning forward to listen, had inadvertently driven the tip of his spear into Yared's foot. Blood pooled on the floor; Yared sang on without flinching. When the chant ended and the king saw what had happened, he wept and offered Yared whatever he asked. Yared asked only to be released from court life to live as a hermit. He withdrew to the Semien Mountains and was never seen again, taken (tradition says) directly to heaven without dying.

### What Yared composed

Yared is credited with composing the *Digua*, *Mawase'et*, *Me'eraf*, and *Zimmare* — the four major chant-books that organize Ethiopian liturgical singing across the year. The *Digua* alone covers the entire liturgical calendar with proper chants for Sundays, feasts, and ordinary days. No other early-Christian liturgical tradition has so thoroughly indigenous a musical corpus by a single named composer.

## Why he matters for Ethiopian Catholics

1. **He is the founder of the indigenous Ethiopian musical tradition** that the Ethiopian Catholic Qeddase still uses. When an Ethiopian Catholic priest chants the Anaphora of the Apostles, the music is Yaredic.
2. **His three modes (ግዕዝ, እዝል, አራራይ) structure the entire Ethiopian Catholic liturgical year.** The catechetical instruction "this season uses Ezel" is a direct inheritance from Yared.
3. **He stands as a witness that liturgy is both word and song.** The Catholic principle "*qui bene cantat bis orat*" (who sings well prays twice — Augustine) finds its African expression in Yared.
4. **His feast day (ግንቦት 11) is shared between Ethiopian Catholics and the EOTC** — one of the most ecumenical days in the Ethiopian liturgical calendar.

## In Scripture (the praise tradition Yared inherited)

> «ሃሌ ሉያ። ጌታን በመቅደሱ አመስግኑት፤ በኃይሉ ጠፈር አመስግኑት።»
> — መዝሙረ ዳዊት 150:1

> «በመለከት ድምፅ አመስግኑት፤ በበገናና በመሰንቆ አመስግኑት።»
> — መዝሙረ ዳዊት 150:3

> «እስትንፋስ ያለው ሁሉ ጌታን ያመስግን። ሃሌ ሉያ።»
> — መዝሙረ ዳዊት 150:6

Psalm 150 is the textual root of all Yaredic chant — every instrument named in the psalm finds its parallel in the Ethiopian liturgical tradition (the kebero drum, the sistrum/ጸናጽል, the begena harp).
