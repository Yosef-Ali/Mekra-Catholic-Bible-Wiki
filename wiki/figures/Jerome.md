## Biography

### Education and conversion

Jerome was born in the Dalmatian town of Stridon (the exact site is lost) sometime in the 340s. He studied rhetoric and grammar in Rome under the famous teacher Donatus, was baptized as a young man, and traveled through Gaul before settling for a time in Aquileia (northern Italy) in an ascetic community. From there he went east — to Antioch, where he learned Greek seriously, and to the **desert of Chalcis** (Syria), where he lived as a hermit for several years and learned **Hebrew** from a Jewish convert. Jerome is the only Latin Father with serious working knowledge of Hebrew.

### The famous dream

In Antioch, Jerome had the dream that became the founding moment of Christian humanism. In the dream, he is dragged before the divine tribunal and accused: *"Ciceronianus es, non Christianus"* — "You are a Ciceronian, not a Christian." The accusation shook him: he had loved pagan classics more than Scripture. He vowed never again to read pagan literature. He kept the vow — for a few years — then quietly resumed reading Cicero and Virgil, but the experience permanently shaped his integration of classical learning into biblical scholarship.

### Rome and the patrician women (382-385)

In 382 Jerome went to Rome as secretary to **Pope Damasus I**, who commissioned him to revise the existing Latin translations of the Gospels. He also became spiritual director to a circle of devout patrician women on the Aventine — most notably **Saint Paula** and her daughter **Saint Eustochium**, who later joined him in the Holy Land. After Damasus's death in 384 the Roman clergy turned against Jerome (his attacks on clerical laxity had earned him many enemies), and he left Rome forever.

### Bethlehem (386-420): the Vulgate

Jerome settled in **Bethlehem** in 386, founding a double monastery (men's and women's communities) near the Church of the Nativity. Paula funded the foundation; Eustochium succeeded her as abbess. Jerome lived in a cave near the grotto of the Nativity (still shown to pilgrims today as **Saint Jerome's Cave**) for the remaining 34 years of his life.

These were the years of the **Vulgate** — Jerome's revolutionary new Latin translation of the entire Bible, made (for the Old Testament) directly from the Hebrew text rather than from the Septuagint. The work occupied him from approximately **382 (Gospels, in Rome) through ~405 (the bulk of the OT, in Bethlehem)**. The major stages:

| Stage | Dates | Content |
|---|---|---|
| Gospels | 382-384 (Rome) | Revision of Old Latin Gospels against Greek |
| Psalms (first revision) | 384 (Rome) | The "Roman Psalter" |
| Psalms (second revision) | ~387 (Bethlehem) | The "Gallican Psalter" — the Psalter used in Latin liturgy until 1979 |
| Hebrew OT | ~390-405 | The historic core of the Vulgate — direct from Hebrew |
| Tobit, Judith | ~405 | Translated from Aramaic |

### The dispute with Augustine over the canon

When Jerome translated directly from the Hebrew, he discovered that several books in the Septuagint (and therefore in Christian use) had no Hebrew original. He proposed treating these **deuterocanonical books** (Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, 1-2 Maccabees, parts of Daniel and Esther) as separate from the canon proper. **Augustine** disagreed, defending the longer canon as received by the Church. The Catholic Church has followed Augustine; the Reformers later followed Jerome's instinct, producing the shorter Protestant canon. **The Ethiopian Catholic Church holds the broader Catholic canon plus several books venerated in the Ethiopian tradition** (such as Enoch and Jubilees, distinctively Ethiopian), making the Ethiopian biblical canon the largest of any Catholic-communion Church.

### Death

Jerome died at Bethlehem on September 30, 420. His tomb is in the Church of the Nativity. The relics were later translated to the Basilica of Saint Mary Major in Rome, where they are venerated today.

## Theological contributions

### 1. The Vulgate

The **Vulgata Editio** ("the common edition") became the standard Latin Bible of the Western Church for **over 1,500 years**. The Council of Trent (1546) declared it the official Latin text of the Catholic Church. Every Catholic theologian from Gregory the Great through Aquinas through Newman read Scripture in Jerome's words. The translations of Genesis 1:1 (*"In principio creavit Deus caelum et terram"*), the Psalms (*"Dominus regit me et nihil mihi deerit"*), and the Magnificat were Jerome's. The Council of Trent's preference for the Vulgate did not (as sometimes alleged) freeze translation work — Pope Pius XII's *Divino Afflante Spiritu* (1943) explicitly authorized translations from the original Hebrew and Greek for liturgical and study use, recovering Jerome's own preference for the *hebraica veritas*.

### 2. Biblical philology

Jerome inaugurated Christian biblical scholarship as a textual-philological discipline. His *De Viris Illustribus* (a catalog of Christian writers), his commentaries on Isaiah, Jeremiah, Ezekiel, Daniel, the Minor Prophets, Matthew, and most of the Pauline epistles, and his vast correspondence (~150 letters, many of them mini-treatises) constitute the largest single-author patristic corpus after Augustine. The Catholic principle that Scripture must be read in its **original languages and historical context** is Jerome's principle — confirmed by Vatican II's *Dei Verbum* (1965).

### 3. The threefold sense of Scripture

While more associated with Origen, the **literal-historical, moral, and spiritual** senses of Scripture are operative throughout Jerome's commentaries. He insists on the literal sense as foundational ("the literal sense is the foundation; without it the spiritual senses collapse") — a principle that runs from him through Aquinas (*Summa Theologiae* I.1.10) into Catholic exegesis to the present.

## Why he matters for Ethiopian Catholics

1. **The principle of the Vulgate** — Scripture in the Church's working language, made from the best available originals — is the principle that produced the **Ge'ez Bible** (translated from Greek and Hebrew sources by Frumentius's successors) and later the **Amharic Emmaus Bible** used by Ethiopian Catholics today. Every translation project in Ethiopian Christian history is in Jerome's lineage.
2. **His debate with Augustine on the canon** is directly relevant: the Ethiopian Catholic canon (preserving deuterocanonicals + the Ethiopian additions) follows Augustine's principle of received tradition, while the textual-critical work to produce reliable editions follows Jerome's principle of *hebraica veritas*.
3. **His commentaries** are the prototype of the Ethiopian *andemta* tradition (running biblical commentary in the vernacular) — though the andemta developed independently in the Ethiopian monastic schools, the form parallels Jerome's.
4. **His patronage of translators** is invoked by every Catholic Bible Society project in Africa, including the ongoing work to refine the Amharic Emmaus and to produce vernacular translations into Tigrinya, Oromo, and other Ethiopian languages.

## In Scripture (Jerome's foundational text)

> «ቅዱስ መጽሐፍ በሙሉ በእግዚአብሔር መንፈስ አነሣሽነት የተጻፈ ነው፤ እንዲሁም ለማስተማር፤ ለመገሠጽ፤ ስሕተትንም ለማረም፤ በጽድቅም መንገድ ለማሠልጠን ያገለግላል፤»
> — 2ኛ ወደ ጢሞቴዎስ 3:16

> «በዚህም የእግዚአብሔር ሰው በችሎታው ፍጹም እንዲሆንና ለመልካም ሥራ ሁሉ ብቁ ሆኖ እንዲገኝ ይጠቅማል።»
> — 2ኛ ወደ ጢሞቴዎስ 3:17

Jerome's most-quoted sentence — *"Ignorantia Scripturarum ignorantia Christi est"* ("Ignorance of the Scriptures is ignorance of Christ", from his Prologue to Isaiah) — is essentially a paraphrase of 2 Timothy 3:16-17. If Scripture *makes the man of God complete*, then to be ignorant of Scripture is to be incomplete in Christ.

## Open questions

- The relationship between the Ge'ez Bible and Jerome's Vulgate: did the Ge'ez translators of the 4th-6th centuries ever consult the Vulgate, or are the Ge'ez OT and NT independently translated from Greek (LXX and the Greek NT) without Latin influence?
- Is there a critical edition of Jerome's letters in Amharic translation? Letters 22 (to Eustochium on virginity) and 53 (to Paulinus on biblical study) would be of high catechetical value.
- The Ethiopian Catholic seminary curriculum on Scripture: how much of Jerome's principle of *hebraica veritas* has been received through *Divino Afflante Spiritu* and *Dei Verbum* in Ge'ez and Amharic Bible work?
