## Overview

ትንቢተ ኢሳይያስ (The Book of Isaiah) is the first and greatest of the Major Prophets, spanning the prophetic ministry of Isaiah ben Amoz from the death of King Uzziah (c. 740 BC) through the Assyrian crisis, the Babylonian exile, and the eschatological vision of new heavens and a new earth. The book's 66 chapters mirror the 66 books of the Christian canon and divide broadly into three sections: Proto-Isaiah (chs. 1–39), focused on judgment against Judah and the nations during the Assyrian period; Deutero-Isaiah (chs. 40–55), the "Book of Consolation" addressed to the exiles in Babylon with the promise of a new exodus; and Trito-Isaiah (chs. 56–66), the vision of the restored community and new creation. Isaiah is called the "Fifth Gospel" because of its density of messianic prophecy — no Old Testament book more thoroughly prefigures the person, mission, and passion of Jesus Christ. The Church reads Isaiah especially during Advent and Holy Week: the Immanuel prophecy (7:14), the royal child of peace (9:6–7), the Spirit-anointed servant (61:1–2), and above all the Fourth Servant Song (52:13–53:12) are the scriptural spine of the Church's Christology.

## Key Themes

**Immanuel: God With Us and the Messianic King.** The Immanuel prophecy (7:14), given to King Ahaz in the context of the Syro-Ephraimite war, is the theological anchor of Isaiah's messianism: "Behold, a virgin shall conceive and bear a son, and shall call his name Immanuel." Matthew 1:23 cites this text as fulfilled in the virginal conception of Jesus — the ultimate "God with us." The promise is expanded in chapters 9 and 11: the child born to us is "Wonderful Counselor, Mighty God, Everlasting Father, Prince of Peace" (9:6), who sits on David's throne eternally. The shoot from the stump of Jesse (11:1) upon whom the Spirit of the LORD rests with sevenfold gifts (11:2) is the messianic king who judges with righteousness and inaugurates a peaceable kingdom. These royal prophecies are essential to [[teaching/jesus-christ-incarnation]] and [[teaching/mary]], as the Church sees in the virginal conception and in the divine titles of the child the revelation of Christ's two natures.

**The Suffering Servant and Vicarious Atonement.** The four Servant Songs (42:1–9, 49:1–7, 50:4–11, and 52:13–53:12) present the figure of the Servant of the LORD — called from the womb, gentle yet unyielding, rejected and suffering, yet through his suffering bringing salvation to the nations. The Fourth Servant Song (Isaiah 53) is the theological summit of the Old Testament's understanding of redemptive suffering: the Servant bears our griefs, is wounded for our transgressions, crushed for our iniquities, and by his stripes we are healed (53:4–5). He is led like a lamb to the slaughter (53:7), makes himself an offering for sin (53:10), and bears the sin of many (53:12). The New Testament applies this song directly to Christ's passion: Matthew 8:17, Acts 8:32–35, 1 Peter 2:24–25, and the entire Passion narrative draw from Isaiah 53. This Servant theology is the primary Old Testament foundation for [[teaching/passion-death-resurrection]] and the Catholic understanding of redemptive suffering.

**New Exodus, New Creation, and the Spirit-Anointed Herald.** Deutero-Isaiah announces a new exodus — not from Egypt but from Babylon, through the wilderness transformed into a highway for the redeemed (40:3–5). This "comfort" (40:1) is grounded in the incomparable sovereignty of the Creator God, who alone declares the end from the beginning (chs. 40–48). The figure of the Spirit-anointed herald (61:1) — who brings good news to the poor, binds the brokenhearted, and proclaims liberty to captives — is claimed by Jesus in the Nazareth synagogue as His mission statement (Luke 4:18–21). The final vision of Trito-Isaiah — new heavens and a new earth (65:17, 66:22) where Jerusalem is a joy and weeping is no more — is taken up by Revelation 21. This eschatological hope and the Spirit-anointed mission support [[teaching/holy-spirit]], [[teaching/baptism]], and [[teaching/forgiveness-resurrection-eternal-life]].

The Spirit-anointed herald of good news — claimed by Jesus in the Nazareth synagogue (Luke 4:18–19) as the program of His public ministry.

## In the Compendium

Isaiah is arguably the most-quoted Old Testament book in the Catechism and Compendium, providing the scriptural foundation for Christology, Mariology, and sacramental theology:

- **Immanuel and the Virginal Conception (Isa 7:14):** The prophecy of the virgin birth is the cornerstone of [[teaching/jesus-christ-incarnation]] and [[teaching/mary]] — God has definitively visited His people in the flesh.
- **The Divine Titles of the Messianic Child (Isa 9:6–7):** The royal titles (Mighty God, Everlasting Father) confess the divinity of Christ, supporting [[teaching/jesus-christ-incarnation]] and the Creed's confession of Christ as "God from God."
- **The Suffering Servant (Isa 52:13–53:12):** The prophecies of vicarious, redemptive suffering are thoroughly integrated into [[teaching/passion-death-resurrection]] — Christ died for our sins according to the Scriptures (1 Cor 15:3).
- **The Spirit-Anointed Mission (Isa 61:1–2):** Jesus' self-interpretation of His ministry supports [[teaching/holy-spirit]] and the theology of the Spirit's work in the Church.
- **The New Exodus and the Highway in the Desert (Isa 40:3–5):** John the Baptist is the voice in the wilderness, prefiguring [[teaching/baptism]] as the passage through water into new life.
- **New Heavens and New Earth (Isa 65:17, 66:22):** The eschatological vision of cosmic renewal is foundational to [[teaching/forgiveness-resurrection-eternal-life]].

- The identity of the Servant in the Servant Songs — does the Emmaus edition reflect the Christian conviction that the Servant is ultimately Christ, or does it present the Servant as a fluid figure (Israel, the prophet, the remnant, the Messiah)?
