## Overview
2 John is the New Testament's shortest book (13 verses), written by "the elder" (ὁ πρεσβύτερος) to "the elect lady and her children" — likely a local church personified as a woman. The letter distills the theology of 1 John into a crisp warning: traveling teachers who deny that "Jesus Christ has come in the flesh" are deceivers and antichrists (v. 7). The faithful must not receive such teachers into their homes or even greet them, lest they share in their wicked works. The letter also reiterates the central commandment: "love one another... that we walk according to his commandments" (vv. 5–6).

## Key Themes
**Truth and Love — Walking in the Commandment.** "The elder" rejoices that some of the elect lady's children are "walking in the truth, just as we were commanded by the Father" (v. 4). Truth and love are inseparable in the Johannine vision: "this is love, that we walk according to his commandments" (v. 6). The commandment is not new — they had it "from the beginning" — but must be continually lived. Love is not sentiment but obedience.

**Refusal of Hospitality to False Teachers.** The letter's most striking instruction concerns "deceivers" who "do not confess the coming of Jesus Christ in the flesh" (v. 7). The elder commands: "do not receive him into your house or give him any greeting, for whoever greets him takes part in his wicked works" (vv. 10–11). In a culture where hospitality signaled acceptance, this refusal is a boundary drawn around the truth of the incarnation. It reflects the Catholic principle that charity without truth is not charity, and that the Church must guard the deposit of faith even at the cost of offense.

## Key Passages
- 2 John 4 — Walking in the truth according to the Father's commandment
- 2 John 6 — This is love: that we walk according to his commandments
- 2 John 7 — Many deceivers have gone out who do not confess the coming of Jesus Christ in the flesh
- 2 John 10–11 — Do not receive false teachers into your house or give them greeting
