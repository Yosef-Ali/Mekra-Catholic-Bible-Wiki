## Overview

መጽሐፈ ባሮክ (The Book of Baruch) is a deuterocanonical work attributed to Baruch ben Neriah, the scribe and companion of the prophet Jeremiah. It is set in Babylon shortly after the fall of Jerusalem (587 BC) and comprises four distinct sections: a penitential prayer confessing Israel's sins (1:1–3:8), a wisdom poem praising the Torah (3:9–4:4), a prophetic poem of consolation to Jerusalem (4:5–5:9), and the appended Letter of Jeremiah (ch. 6) warning against idolatry. The book was almost certainly composed later than the exilic period (probably 2nd century BC) but is read by the Church as an authentic expression of exilic spirituality: the prayer of a people who have lost everything and turn in repentance, seeking wisdom in the Law.

Baruch is read in the Catholic liturgy during Holy Week and Advent. The wisdom poem (ch. 3) — "She is the book of the commandments of God, the law that endures forever" — is one of the most explicit identifications of Wisdom with Torah. The consolation poem's image of Jerusalem seeing her children return "carried in glory, as on a royal throne" (5:6) is applied to the Church's vision of the heavenly Jerusalem.

## Key Themes

**Penitential Prayer.** Baruch 1:15–3:8 is a profound confession of sin modeled on Daniel 9 and Ezra 9. The exiles confess that the disaster of 587 BC was not divine unfaithfulness but Israel's own rebellion: "We have sinned before the Lord. We have disobeyed Him" (1:17-18). The prayer acknowledges the justice of God's judgment while appealing to His mercy. This pattern — confession of sin followed by plea for mercy — is the biblical model of penitential prayer that grounds [[teaching/penance]] and [[teaching/sin]].

**Wisdom as the Law.** Baruch 3:9–4:4 asks why Israel has abandoned the fountain of wisdom and answers: wisdom is found in the Torah. "She is the book of the commandments of God, the law that endures forever. All who hold her fast will live, but those who forsake her will die" (4:1). This explicit identification of Wisdom with the Mosaic Law is unique in the Old Testament and supports [[teaching/moral-law-and-grace]] — the Law as gift, not burden.

**Hope of Restoration.** The consolation poem (4:5–5:9) speaks to Jerusalem as a bereaved mother whose children have been taken but will return. "Take off the garment of your sorrow and affliction, O Jerusalem, and put on forever the beauty of the glory from God" (5:1). The Church reads this eschatologically — the restoration of Jerusalem prefigures the heavenly Jerusalem and the final gathering of God's scattered children. Baruch 3:36-38 ("He appeared on earth and lived among men") is read Christologically as an Advent prophecy of the Incarnation, supporting [[teaching/jesus-christ-incarnation]].

## Key Passages

> «እኛስ እስከ ዛሬ ድረስ በአባቶቻችን አሻራ ተከትለን ለአምላካችን ለእግዚአብሔር ባለመታዘዝ ኃጢአት ሠርተናል።»
> — ባሮክ 1:17-18

The confession of sin — acknowledgment that exile is consequence, not accident, grounding the theology of penance.

> «እርሷም የእግዚአብሔር ትእዛዛት መጽሐፍ ናት፤ ለዘለዓለምም የምትኖር ሕግ።»
> — ባሮክ 4:1

Wisdom identified with Torah — the Law as the path of life and the dwelling place of divine wisdom.

> «ከዚያም ወዲያ በምድር ላይ ታየ፤ ከሰዎችም ጋር ኖረ።»
> — ባሮክ 3:38

The Incarnation prophecy — read during Advent as foretelling the Word dwelling among us (John 1:14).
