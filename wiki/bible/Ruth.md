## Overview

መጽሐፈ ሩት (The Book of Ruth) is a short narrative set "in the days when the judges ruled" (1:1), during a famine that drives Elimelech's family from Bethlehem to Moab. After the deaths of her husband and sons, Naomi returns to Bethlehem with her Moabite daughter-in-law Ruth, who famously refuses to abandon her: "Where you go I will go... your people shall be my people, and your God my God" (1:16). In Bethlehem, Ruth gleans in the fields of Boaz, a wealthy relative who redeems her through the levirate marriage custom. The book ends with the genealogy tracing from Perez to David — placing Ruth, a Moabite convert, in the direct lineage of Israel's greatest king and, ultimately, the Messiah. In the Catholic canon, Ruth is read as a book of covenant loyalty (hesed) and divine providence. The genealogy of Matthew 1 explicitly includes Ruth, one of only four women named, highlighting God's inclusion of the foreigner in salvation history.

## Key Themes

**Covenant Loyalty (Hesed).** Ruth is the Bible's greatest narrative illustration of hesed — steadfast, covenantal love that goes beyond duty. Ruth's loyalty to Naomi is not based on blood obligation or expected return; she pledges herself permanently to Naomi, her people, and her God. Boaz likewise acts with hesed in going beyond the letter of the law to redeem Ruth. This mutual loyalty models the self-giving love that is the heart of [[teaching/marriage]] and the Church's understanding of covenant fidelity.

**The Foreigner Welcomed.** Ruth is a Moabite — a people specifically excluded from the assembly of the Lord by Deuteronomy 23:3. Yet the book narrates her full inclusion: she becomes an Israelite by confession of faith, marries an Israelite, and becomes the great-grandmother of David. The book challenges any narrow ethnic understanding of election and prepares for the universal mission of the Church. This theme strongly supports [[teaching/human-dignity]] and [[teaching/the-church]] — the Church as the home of all nations.

**Providence Through Ordinary Means.** God is never named as an actor in Ruth; there are no miracles, visions, or prophetic oracles. Yet His providence operates through every detail: the famine that drives the family to Moab, Ruth "happening" to glean in Boaz's field (2:3), Boaz's availability as redeemer. The book teaches that God works through ordinary human decisions — migration, work, love, legal transactions — to accomplish His extraordinary purposes. This "hidden providence" theology supports [[teaching/faith-and-revelation]].

## Key Passages

> «ወደምትሄጂበት እሄዳለሁ፤ በምታድሪበትም አድራለሁ፤ ወገኖችሽ ወገኖቼ ናቸው፤ አምላክሽም አምላኬ ነው።»
> — ሩት 1:16

Ruth's confession of faith — the paradigm of gentile conversion and covenant loyalty, echoing in the marriage rite to this day.

> «ርስቱ ያለ እንዳይቋረጥ ለሙቱ ሚስት እንደ እንግዲህ ሩትን አገባት።»
> — ሩት 4:10

Boaz's redemption of Ruth — the legal act of the go'el (kinsman-redeemer) that prefigures Christ's redemption of the Church.

## In the Compendium

- **Gentile Inclusion (Ruth 1:16):** Ruth's confession supports [[teaching/the-church]] — the universality of salvation and the Church's catholicity.
- **Levirate Marriage (Ruth 4):** The legal institution of the go'el supports [[teaching/marriage]] — marriage as covenant with communal dimension.
- **The Davidic Lineage (Ruth 4:18-22):** Ruth's place in David's genealogy anchors [[teaching/jesus-christ-incarnation]] — God draws the outsider into the messianic line.
