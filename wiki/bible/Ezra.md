## Overview
Ezra recounts the return of the Jewish exiles from Babylon under Persian decree (c. 538 BC) and the rebuilding of the Temple in Jerusalem. The book is named after Ezra the scribe-priest who leads a second wave of returnees and spearheads a spiritual reform centered on the Torah. Historically it covers approximately 80 years and is continued in the book of Nehemiah. Catholic tradition reads Ezra as a model of fidelity to God's law and the renewal of liturgical worship after exile — a type of the Church's own mission of restoration.

## Key Themes
**Return and Restoration.** The book opens with Cyrus of Persia issuing a decree allowing the Jews to return and rebuild the Temple (Ezra 1:1-4). This fulfills Jeremiah's prophecy of 70 years of exile. The returnees rebuild the altar first, then lay the Temple foundation — liturgical worship precedes all else. The opposition from surrounding peoples tests their resolve but does not stop the work.

**The Scribe and the Law.** Ezra arrives as "a scribe skilled in the law of Moses" (7:6) with authority from King Artaxerxes to teach and enforce the Torah. His mission is not merely architectural but spiritual — to restore the covenant community around the Word of God. This anticipates the Church's teaching office (Magisterium) and the centrality of Scripture in Catholic life.

**Covenant Purity and Reform.** The crisis of mixed marriages (chapters 9-10) threatens the distinct identity of the post-exilic community. Ezra's prayer of confession (9:6-15) acknowledges corporate sin and appeals to God's mercy. The reform, while severe to modern readers, reflects the existential concern for preserving covenant faithfulness in a fragile restored community.

## Key Passages
- Ezra 1:1-3 — Cyrus's decree to rebuild the Temple
- Ezra 3:11 — Joy at laying the Temple foundation
- Ezra 7:10 — Ezra devoted himself to study and teach the Law
- Ezra 9:6 — Ezra's prayer of confession for the people's sin
