## Overview

ወደ ሮሜ ሰዎች (The Letter of Paul to the Romans) is the most systematic and theologically profound of Paul's letters and arguably the single most influential document in Christian history. Written around AD 56–57 from Corinth (or its port Cenchreae, via the deaconess Phoebe in Romans 16:1–2), Paul addresses a mixed Jewish-Gentile Christian community in Rome — a church he did not found and had not yet visited. His purpose is dual: to present his mature understanding of the Gospel (the "good news" of God's righteousness revealed in Christ) and to secure the Roman church's support for his planned mission to Spain (15:24–28).

Romans unfolds as a carefully structured theological argument. After the greeting and thesis statement (1:16–17), Paul demonstrates the universal reign of sin over both Gentiles (1:18–32) and Jews (2:1–3:20). He then proclaims justification by faith apart from works of the Law (3:21–5:21), explores the new life in the Spirit and freedom from sin (6:1–8:39), wrestles with the mystery of Israel's unbelief (9:1–11:36), and concludes with a sweeping exhortation to the transformed Christian life of worship, love, and service (12:1–15:13). The letter is the closest Paul comes to a "summa" of his theology — the gospel of God's righteousness revealed in Christ and received by faith.

## Key Themes

**Justification by Faith and the Righteousness of God (የእግዚአብሔር ጽድቅ).** The central theme of Romans is the revelation of God's saving righteousness (δικαιοσύνη θεοῦ / የእግዚአብሔር ጽድቅ) — not a punitive justice but a gift that sets humanity right with God. Paul argues that all, both Jew and Gentile, stand under the power of sin and fall short of God's glory (3:23). The Law cannot save; it diagnoses sin but cannot cure it. Justification comes "freely by his grace through the redemption that is in Christ Jesus" (3:24), received through faith, not works. Abraham becomes the model — he believed God and it was reckoned to him as righteousness (ch. 4). This doctrine is the cornerstone of [[teaching/faith-and-revelation]] and the Catholic understanding of grace, sharply distinguishing Christian faith from both legalism and moralism.

**Baptism, Death, and New Life in the Spirit.** Romans 6 is the New Testament's most concentrated passage on the theology of Baptism. Paul confronts the objection that grace licenses sin: "Are we to continue in sin that grace may abound? By no means!" (6:1–2). Baptism is union with Christ's death and resurrection — we are buried with Christ in baptismal death so that we may walk in newness of life (6:3–4). This sacramental realism grounds [[teaching/baptism]]: Baptism is not a mere symbol but a real participation in the Paschal Mystery. Chapter 8 then unfolds the life of the baptized: the indwelling Spirit frees from condemnation, confirms us as adopted children who cry "Abba, Father," and guarantees that nothing can separate us from the love of God in Christ (8:31–39).

**The Universal Call and the Mystery of Israel.** Romans 9–11 is Paul's anguished meditation on the mystery of Israel's unbelief. He insists that God's word has not failed and that there remains a remnant chosen by grace (11:5). The "hardening" of part of Israel has served the ingathering of the Gentiles, but God's ultimate purpose is to "graft them in again" (11:23) so that "all Israel will be saved" (11:26). These chapters are essential for Catholic-Jewish relations and guard against supersessionism — the Church does not replace Israel but is joined to the root of the olive tree. Paul's doxology (11:33–36) — "O the depth of the riches and wisdom and knowledge of God!" — captures the apostle's ultimate posture before the mystery of divine election: worship.

## Key Passages

> «በወንጌል አላፍርምና፤ በመጀመሪያ ለአይሁድ እንዲሁም ለግሪካውያን፥ ለሚያምኑ ሁሉ የእግዚአብሔር ኃይል ለማዳን ነው።»
> — ሮሜ 1:16

The thesis statement of the entire letter — the Gospel is the power of God for salvation to everyone who believes, Jew first and also Greek.

> «ሁሉም ኃጢአትን ሠርተዋልና፤ የእግዚአብሔርም ክብር ጐድሎአቸዋል፤ በኢየሱስ ክርስቶስ በሆነው ቤዛነት በኩል እንዲያው በጸጋው ይጸድቃሉ።»
> — ሮሜ 3:23–24

The universal diagnosis and remedy: all have sinned and all are justified freely by grace through the redemption in Christ Jesus.

> «ስለዚህ በክርስቶስ ከሙታን እንደ ተነሣ እንዲሁ እኛም በአዲስ ሕይወት እንድንኖር፥ በጥምቀት አማካኝነት በሞት ከእርሱ ጋር ተቀብረናል።»
> — ሮሜ 6:4

The core of baptismal theology — through Baptism we are buried with Christ into death so that, as He was raised, we too walk in newness of life.

> «እግዚአብሔርንም ለሚወዱት እንደ ዓላማውም ለተጠሩት ነገር ሁሉ ለበጎ እንዲደረግ እናውቃለን።»
> — ሮሜ 8:28

The assurance of divine providence — God works all things for the good of those who love Him and are called according to His purpose.

> «መልካም፣ ደስ የሚያሰኝና ፍጹም የሆነውን የእግዚአብሔር ፈቃድ ምን እንደሆነ ለማወቅ በልባችሁ መታደስ ተለወጡ እንጂ ይህን ዓለም አትምሰሉ።»
> — ሮሜ 12:2

The hinge from theology to ethics — the transformed life flows from the mercies of God. Christian moral life is renewal of the mind, not conformity to the world.

## In the Compendium

Romans is the doctrinal backbone of Catholic catechesis on grace, justification, baptism, and moral life:

- **Justification by Faith (Romans 3:21–28):** Central to [[teaching/faith-and-revelation]] (Q1–25) and [[teaching/moral-law-and-grace]] (Q420–432) — the gratuitousness of salvation is the heart of the Gospel.
- **Baptism into Christ's Death and Resurrection (Romans 6:3–4):** The primary biblical text for [[teaching/baptism]] (Q252–264). Baptism effects a real ontological union with the Paschal Mystery.
- **Universal Sinfulness (Romans 1:18–3:20):** Foundational for [[teaching/sin]] and [[teaching/human-dignity]] — the doctrine of original sin and the universality of the need for redemption.
- **Life in the Spirit and Moral Transformation (Romans 8; 12–15):** Essential for [[teaching/virtues]] (Q377–390) — the Spirit enables the moral life that the Law could only command.
- **Resurrection Hope (Romans 8:18–39):** Supports [[teaching/forgiveness-resurrection-eternal-life]] (Q105–131) — nothing can separate us from the love of God in Christ Jesus our Lord.
- **The Body of Christ (Romans 12:4–8):** The diversity of gifts and unity of the Church underpins [[teaching/the-church]].
