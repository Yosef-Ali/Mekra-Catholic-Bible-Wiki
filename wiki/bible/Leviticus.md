## Overview

ኦሪተ ዘሌዋውያን (Leviticus, "pertaining to the Levites") is the third book of the Torah, situated at the foot of Mount Sinai. While Exodus ends with the construction of the Tabernacle, Leviticus provides the instruction manual for worship within it. The book is primarily legal and ritual: it prescribes the sacrificial system (chs. 1–7), the consecration of the Aaronic priesthood (chs. 8–10), purity laws (chs. 11–16), and the Holiness Code (chs. 17–27). Though often considered the most challenging book for modern readers, Leviticus is indispensable for understanding Christ's sacrifice on the Cross and His priesthood. The Letter to the Hebrews cannot be understood without Leviticus. Every sacrifice — burnt offering, grain offering, peace offering, sin offering, guilt offering — finds its fulfillment in the one perfect sacrifice of Christ. The Day of Atonement (ch. 16) with its two goats (one sacrificed, one sent into the wilderness bearing sin) is the most vivid Old Testament type of redemption.

Leviticus also contains the great commandment to love: "You shall love your neighbor as yourself" (19:18), which Jesus identifies as the second greatest commandment (Matt 22:39). The book's central theme is holiness — God is holy, and His people are called to be holy. The separation between clean and unclean, sacred and profane, is not arbitrary but pedagogical, forming Israel in the consciousness of God's transcendent otherness.

## Key Themes

**Sacrifice and Atonement.** The first seven chapters detail five types of sacrifice. The whole burnt offering (olah) is wholly consumed, representing total self-gift to God. The sin and guilt offerings make expiation for specific transgressions. The peace offering is a communion meal shared between the altar, priest, and worshiper. All point to Christ: the perfect holocaust who gives Himself entirely, the sin offering who becomes sin for us (2 Cor 5:21), and the peace offering who reconciles us to God and each other. The blood manipulation — sprinkling on the altar, smearing on the horns — signifies that life belongs to God and that atonement comes through life given. This theology underpins [[teaching/eucharist]] (the Mass as sacrifice) and [[teaching/sin]] (the gravity of sin requiring expiation).

**The Day of Atonement (Yom Kippur).** Leviticus 16 is the liturgical and theological center of the book. The high priest enters the Holy of Holies only on this day, bearing blood for himself and the people. The two goats ritual is the Bible's most powerful image of substitutionary atonement: one goat is sacrificed for sin, while the other (the scapegoat, Azazel) is sent into the wilderness bearing the sins of the people. The Letter to the Hebrews reads Christ as both high priest and sacrifice, entering not a man-made sanctuary but heaven itself with His own blood (Heb 9:11-14).

**Holiness as Vocation.** "You shall be holy, for I the Lord your God am holy" (19:2). The Holiness Code (chs. 17–27) applies this to every sphere of life: worship, sexuality, economics, and social justice. The separation of clean from unclean animals (ch. 11) is a daily physical reminder of Israel's election. The sabbath and jubilee laws (ch. 25) restore the original order of creation by returning land and liberating slaves. Leviticus insists that liturgy (how we worship) and ethics (how we treat our neighbor) are inseparable — an insight fundamental to [[teaching/moral-law-and-grace]].

## Key Passages

> «በዚህም ቀን ካህኑ ስለ እናንተ ኃጢአት ይስረይ ዘንድ ቤዛ ያደርግላችኋል፤ በእግዚአብሔርም ፊት ንጹሖች ትሆናላችሁ።»
> — ዘሌዋውያን 16:30

The Day of Atonement promise: cleansing from sin through priestly mediation — the Old Testament type of Christ's paschal sacrifice.

> «ባልንጀራህን እንደ ራስህ አድርገህ ውደደው፤ እኔ እግዚአብሔር ነኝ።»
> — ዘሌዋውያን 19:18

The command to love neighbor — the second great commandment, cited by Jesus and Paul (Rom 13:9, Gal 5:14).

> «ተቀደሱ እኔም ቅዱስ ነኝና፤ እኔ እግዚአብሔር አምላክህ ነኝ።»
> — ዘሌዋውያን 19:2

The universal call to holiness — the foundation of the Church's teaching on the universal call to sanctity.
