## Overview
The Letter of James is a practical, wisdom-infused exhortation written by "James, a servant of God and of the Lord Jesus Christ" — traditionally identified as James the Just, "brother of the Lord" and first bishop of Jerusalem. Addressed to "the twelve tribes in the dispersion," the letter reads like New Testament wisdom literature, echoing the teaching of Jesus (especially the Sermon on the Mount) and the Old Testament Proverbs. James is deeply concerned with the integrity of faith: faith without works is dead. He addresses the trials of the poor, the danger of the tongue, partiality toward the rich, and the power of prayer — including the sacrament of the sick (5:14–15). The letter has been controversial since Luther called it "an epistle of straw," but the Catholic Church has always affirmed its canonicity and its vital balancing of Pauline teaching on justification.

## Key Themes
**Faith and Works — An Organic Unity.** James 2:14–26 is the letter's most famous (and misunderstood) passage: "faith by itself, if it does not have works, is dead." James does not contradict Paul's teaching on justification by faith (Rom 3:28); rather, he addresses a different problem — a faith that is mere intellectual assent without transformative effect. Abraham was justified by works when he offered Isaac (2:21), and Rahab by works when she received the spies (2:25). Paul and James use "justification" in complementary senses: Paul speaks of the initial gift of righteousness, James of the visible demonstration that faith is alive. The Catholic tradition holds both together: we are justified by grace through faith working through love (Gal 5:6). This passage directly informs [[teaching/faith-and-revelation]] and [[teaching/moral-law-and-grace]].

**The Tongue, Wealth, and Practical Wisdom.** James is relentlessly concrete. The tongue is "a fire, a world of unrighteousness" that can set a whole forest ablaze (3:5–6) — "with it we bless the Lord and Father, and with it we curse people made in God's likeness" (3:9). The rich who oppress the poor will face judgment (5:1–6). Instead of making plans for profit, believers should say "if the Lord wills" (4:15). Chapter 5 commands patience in suffering, prayer in all circumstances, and the anointing of the sick by the elders with prayer — "the prayer of faith will save the sick, and the Lord will raise him up; and if he has committed sins, he will be forgiven" (5:15). This is the biblical foundation for the Sacrament of the Anointing of the Sick.
