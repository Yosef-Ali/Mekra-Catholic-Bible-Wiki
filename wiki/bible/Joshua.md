## Overview

መጽሐፈ ኢያሱ (Joshua, from the Hebrew יְהוֹשֻׁעַ meaning "YHWH is salvation") is the sixth book of the Bible and the first book of the Deuteronomistic History (Joshua through Kings). The book narrates the entry of Israel into the Promised Land under Joshua's leadership after the death of Moses. Structurally, it divides into three major movements: the crossing of the Jordan and the conquest of the land (chs. 1–12), the distribution of tribal inheritances (chs. 13–21), and the covenant renewal at Shechem (chs. 22–24). Joshua is a book about the fulfillment of God's promises — the land sworn to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob (Genesis 12:7) is now possessed. But possession is not automatic; it requires Israel's faithful obedience. The book's central command — "Be strong and courageous" (1:6–9) — rings throughout as Joshua leads a new generation out of the wilderness and into their inheritance. The name "Joshua" in Hebrew (Yehoshua) is the same as "Jesus" (Yeshua) in its later form, making the book a profound type of Christ: just as Joshua led Israel into the earthly promised land, so Jesus leads the Church into the heavenly promised land. The conquest narratives raise difficult moral questions but must be read in their theological context as the execution of divine judgment against deeply corrupt Canaanite cultures (Deuteronomy 9:4–5), not as a model for human warfare.

## Key Themes

**The Conquest of the Land and the Faithfulness of God.** The book of Joshua is, above all, a testimony to God's covenant faithfulness. After centuries of waiting — from Abraham through Egyptian slavery and wilderness wandering — the LORD fulfills His oath: "not one of all the good promises that the LORD had made to the house of Israel failed; all came to pass" (21:45). The conquest is presented as God's war, not Israel's military achievement: Jericho's walls fall not by siege engines but by liturgical procession and trumpet blasts (ch. 6), and the sun stands still at Gibeon at Joshua's prayer (ch. 10). This divine agency is crucial — the land is gift, not conquest-by-merit. Yet the book includes the tension of incomplete possession: territories remain unconquered (13:1–7), pointing forward to a final and complete inheritance that only Christ the new Joshua can secure. This faithfulness of God to His promises undergirds [[teaching/faith-and-revelation]] — faith is trust in the God who keeps His word across generations.

**Holy War and the Ban (Ḥerem): Judgment and Purity.** The command to devote certain Canaanite cities to destruction (the ḥerem or "ban") is among the most morally challenging aspects of the Old Testament. In its canonical context, the ban is not gratuitous violence but divine judgment executed against cultures whose iniquity had reached its full measure (Genesis 15:16; Deuteronomy 9:4–5). The narrative insists that this is God's war, not Israel's — the real danger is not military failure but idolatrous contamination. The story of Achan (ch. 7), who takes devoted spoils and brings disaster on Israel, illustrates that the ban was fundamentally about purity and exclusive devotion to the LORD. The Church reads these texts typologically: the spiritual warfare of the Christian is not against flesh and blood but against sin — the "Canaanites" within the heart must be devoted to destruction through repentance and grace. This theme supports [[teaching/moral-law-and-grace]], which teaches that the moral life is a battle against sin that requires divine assistance.

**Covenant Renewal: Choose This Day Whom You Will Serve.** The climax of Joshua is not military victory but liturgical assembly. In chapter 24, Joshua gathers all Israel at Shechem — the very place where God first promised the land to Abraham (Genesis 12:6–7) — and leads a covenant renewal ceremony. He recites the history of salvation (24:2–13), issues the famous challenge: "choose this day whom you will serve... as for me and my house, we will serve the LORD" (24:15), and the people respond with a threefold commitment (24:16–24). Joshua sets up a stone of witness and writes the covenant in "the book of the law of God" (24:26). This moment is the theological anchor connecting the exodus generation to the settled life of Israel. The Shechem covenant is a perpetual call to decision — the faith must be personally appropriated by each generation. This covenant-renewal pattern shapes [[teaching/the-church]] and grounds the liturgical understanding that every generation of Christians must ratify the baptismal covenant for itself.

The great decision at Shechem — Joshua's challenge to exclusive covenant loyalty, a call to personal decision that resonates through all of salvation history.

The people's triple ratification of the covenant — a liturgical act that binds Israel to the LORD at the very place where the promise was first given to Abraham.

## In the Compendium

Joshua provides the biblical paradigm for covenant fidelity, divine faithfulness, and the typology of Jesus:

- **The Commission to Joshua (Josh 1:6–9):** The command to meditate on the law and be strong and courageous supports [[teaching/moral-law-and-grace]] and [[teaching/faith-and-revelation]] — the Word of God is the source of strength and direction for the life of faith.
- **The Conquest as Gift (Josh 6, 21:43–45):** The land is given by God, not earned, undergirding [[teaching/god-the-father-creator]] and the theology of grace — all good things come as divine gift.
- **The Covenant at Shechem (Josh 24:14–28):** The choice between the LORD and other gods grounds [[teaching/first-three-commandments]] — the exclusive worship of God and the personal decision of faith.
- **Joshua as Type of Jesus:** The name Yehoshua/Yeshua (salvation) and the role of leading God's people into their inheritance supports [[teaching/the-church]] — the Church is the new Israel led by Christ into the heavenly promised land.
