## Overview
Philippians is Paul's letter of joy, written from prison (probably Rome or Ephesus, c. AD 62) to his beloved community in Philippi — the first church he planted on European soil. Despite his chains, the letter radiates joy (χαρά, used 16 times) and calls the Philippians to unity, humility, and steadfastness. The centerpiece is the Christ Hymn (2:5–11), which traces the arc from pre-existence through kenosis (self-emptying) to exaltation, declaring that "at the name of Jesus every knee should bow." Paul also warns against Judaizers ("dogs," 3:2), defines his own life as "knowing Christ and the power of his resurrection," and promises that "the Lord is near" (4:5).

## Key Themes
**The Christ Hymn — Kenosis and Exaltation.** Philippians 2:5–11 is the most theologically concentrated passage on the incarnation in Paul's letters. Christ, "though he was in the form of God, did not count equality with God a thing to be grasped, but emptied himself, taking the form of a servant." This self-emptying (kenosis) is the pattern for Christian humility — "have this mind among yourselves, which is yours in Christ Jesus." The hymn then soars to the universal confession that "Jesus Christ is Lord, to the glory of God the Father." The Catholic tradition reads this passage as foundational for the theology of the incarnation and the moral call to humility and obedience.

**Citizenship in Heaven and Joy in Suffering.** "Our πολίτευμα (citizenship) is in heaven, and from it we await a Savior, the Lord Jesus Christ" (3:20). Paul reframes the Philippians' pride in their Roman colonial status: their true homeland is heaven. From this eschatological anchor flows the command to "rejoice in the Lord always" (4:4), to be anxious for nothing but bring everything to God in prayer (4:6), and to find contentment in every circumstance — "I can do all things through him who strengthens me" (4:13). The letter models how Christian joy coexists with suffering and chains.
