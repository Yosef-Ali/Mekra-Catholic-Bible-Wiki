## Overview

1 Maccabees narrates the Jewish revolt against Seleucid oppression (167–134 BC), beginning with the persecution under Antiochus IV Epiphanes, who outlawed Jewish religious practice, desecrated the Temple (the "abomination of desolation"), and imposed Hellenistic worship. The priest Mattathias and his five sons — Judas Maccabeus, Jonathan, Simon, John, and Eleazar — lead the resistance. Judas recaptures and rededicates the Temple (164 BC), an event commemorated by the feast of Hanukkah (the "Dedication," John 10:22). The book continues through the deaths of Judas and Jonathan to the establishment of the Hasmonean dynasty under Simon. Written in Hebrew (now lost; preserved in Greek), 1 Maccabees is the primary historical source for the intertestamental period. It is deuterocanonical: accepted by the Catholic and Orthodox Churches, rejected by Protestants and Jews.

The book's theology is pragmatic and Deuteronomic: fidelity to the Law brings deliverance; apostasy brings disaster. Judas Maccabeus is portrayed as a new Joshua and David, fighting holy war under divine sanction. The book's historical value is immense — it narrates the crisis that formed the Jewish sects (Pharisees, Sadducees, Essenes) and the political context into which Jesus was born.

## Key Themes

**Religious Liberty and Martyrdom.** Antiochus IV's persecution was the first attempt in history to eradicate a religion entirely. The decree outlawed circumcision, sabbath observance, dietary laws, and Torah reading; copies of the Law were burned; idolatrous altars were erected throughout Judea. The Maccabean response — armed resistance in defense of religious freedom — has influenced Catholic social teaching on the right to religious liberty and the duty to resist tyranny, supporting [[teaching/society-and-justice]].

**Zeal for the Law.** Mattathias's opening speech and act — killing a Jew who stepped forward to offer pagan sacrifice, then killing the king's officer (2:23-26) — models the zeal of Phinehas (Num 25). The Maccabees are not innovators but restorers: they fight to return to the Torah. The rededication of the Temple (ch. 4) is the book's liturgical climax: the altar is rebuilt, new vessels are made, the lampstand is lit, and eight days of celebration are inaugurated. This restorationist zeal supports [[teaching/faith-and-revelation]] and the Catholic understanding of sacred Tradition.

**Diplomacy and Realpolitik.** Unlike the idealized narratives of earlier Scripture, 1 Maccabees is remarkably realistic about political maneuverings. Judas seeks alliance with Rome (ch. 8), Jonathan plays Seleucid claimants against each other, and Simon consolidates power through diplomacy. This political dimension raises permanent questions about the relationship between faith and earthly power that inform [[teaching/the-church]] and the Church's relationship with the state.

## Key Passages

> «የማቲያስም ልጆች ስለ ወንድሞቻቸው ተሰብስበው ወደ እርሱ መጡ፤ በዙሪያውም ያሉትን የአይሁድን ጉባኤዎች ሁሉ አጠናከሩ።»
> — 1ኛ መቃብያን 3:2

Judas Maccabeus rallies the faithful — the formation of the resistance community, the remnant that preserves faith under persecution.

> «ከዚያም ይሁዳና ወንድሞቹ በቀጥታ ወደ ኢየሩሳሌም ከተማ ሄዱ፤ መቅደሱንም አነጹና የተበረከሰውን ምስዋዕ አፈረሱ፤ አዲስም ምስዋዕ ሠሩ።»
> — 1ኛ መቃብያን 4:36-59 (summary)

The rededication of the Temple — the origin of Hanukkah, the feast Jesus Himself observed (John 10:22).
