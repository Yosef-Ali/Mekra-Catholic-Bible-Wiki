## Overview

1ኛ ሳሙኤል (1 Samuel) is the ninth book of the Bible and the third book of the Deuteronomistic History. The book spans the critical transition from the period of the judges to the establishment of the monarchy in Israel, structured around three towering figures: Samuel the prophet-judge (chs. 1–7), Saul the first king (chs. 8–15), and David the rising anointed one (chs. 16–31). The book opens with Hannah's barrenness and her miraculous conception of Samuel — a story that echoes the matriarchs of Genesis and prefigures the Annunciation — and closes with the tragic death of Saul on Mount Gilboa. Theologically, 1 Samuel wrestles with the tension between human kingship and divine kingship: when Israel demands a king "like all the nations" (8:5), the LORD declares, "they have rejected me from being king over them" (8:7). Yet God does not abandon His people to their choice — He raises up David, a man after His own heart (13:14), and through David the monarchy becomes the vehicle of messianic promise. 1 Samuel is the book of the anointed one (מָשִׁיחַ, mashiach): Saul the failed messiah and David the true messiah, both pointing forward to Christ the Anointed who perfectly fulfills the offices of prophet, priest, and king.

## Key Themes

**The Rejection of Divine Kingship and the Demand for a Human King.** The crisis of 1 Samuel 8 is a theological turning point in the Old Testament. The elders of Israel approach Samuel at Ramah and demand a king "to judge us like all the nations" (8:5). The request is not for leadership per se — Israel always had judges — but for a monarchy that mirrors the surrounding nations, a visible, permanent, dynastic ruler with a standing army. The LORD's diagnosis cuts to the heart: "they have not rejected you, but they have rejected me from being king over them" (8:7). Yet God accommodates the request: He gives detailed instructions for the king's conduct (8:11–18) and ultimately directs Samuel to anoint both Saul and David. This dialectic — rejection of divine kingship met by divine accommodation that will ultimately fulfill the deeper longing — is the grammar of salvation history: God works through even sinful human choices to bring about His purposes. This supports [[teaching/the-church]] — the Church recognizes that all earthly authority is subordinate to the kingship of Christ — and [[teaching/faith-and-revelation]] — God's revelation unfolds through the complexities of human history.

**The Anointing of David and the Heart of God.** The hinge of 1 Samuel is chapter 16: God rejects Saul and sends Samuel to Bethlehem to anoint one of Jesse's sons. Samuel, like Israel, looks on outward appearance — the tall, impressive Eliab — but the LORD intervenes: "man looks on the outward appearance, but the LORD looks on the heart" (16:7). David, the youngest, the shepherd boy, is chosen and anointed. From that moment "the Spirit of the LORD rushed upon David" (16:13), even as the Spirit departed from Saul. The anointing of David establishes the pattern of divine election: God chooses the unlikely, the overlooked, the least — a theology that runs from Abel through Joseph to Mary of Nazareth. David's anointing as shepherd-king prefigures Christ the Good Shepherd, anointed not with oil but with the Holy Spirit, who rules not by outward splendor but by the love that lays down its life. This theology of election and the heart grounds [[teaching/human-dignity]] — every person's true worth is seen by God alone — and [[teaching/jesus-christ-incarnation]] — the Messiah comes from Bethlehem, the least of the clans of Judah.

**David and Goliath: The Battle Belongs to the LORD.** The David and Goliath narrative (ch. 17) is the most iconic story in 1 Samuel and one of the most well-known biblical narratives. Goliath, the Philistine champion, embodies human power — massive, armed, experienced, intimidating. David approaches with a sling, five smooth stones, and a confession: "I come to you in the name of the LORD of hosts, the God of the armies of Israel" (17:45). The theological point is explicit: "the LORD saves not with sword and spear; for the battle is the LORD's" (17:47). The story is not about underdog triumph but about divine deliverance through the weakness of human instrumentality — a theme that reaches its climax at the cross, where the seemingly defeated Christ defeats the ultimate enemy (sin and death) through the "weakness" of self-giving love. The Goliath narrative also establishes David's public identity and begins Saul's jealousy, setting in motion the long narrative of Saul's pursuit of David (chs. 18–31), which becomes a schooling in patient trust and refusal to seize the kingdom by force — a profound type of Christ who refused Satan's offer of the kingdoms of the world (Matthew 4:8–10).

David's confession before Goliath — the battle belongs to the LORD, not to human armament, the paradigm of spiritual warfare by faith.

## In the Compendium

1 Samuel establishes the pattern of prophetic kingship, divine election, and the messianic hope:

- **The Call of Samuel (1 Sam 3:1–21):** The prophetic office as attentiveness to God's word supports [[teaching/faith-and-revelation]] — God speaks, and the human response is obedient listening.
- **The Demand for a King (1 Sam 8):** The tension between divine and human kingship grounds [[teaching/the-church]] — all earthly authority is judged by the kingship of Christ, the only King who is also Servant.
- **David's Anointing (1 Sam 16:1–13):** The election of the unlikely, the youngest, the shepherd prefigures [[teaching/jesus-christ-incarnation]] — the Messiah comes from humble origins, and [[teaching/mary]] — Mary's Magnificat echoes Hannah's song.
- **David and Goliath (1 Sam 17):** Victory through weakness and faith supports [[teaching/moral-law-and-grace]] — the battle against sin is won by grace, not human effort.
- **David's Refusal to Kill Saul (1 Sam 24, 26):** David's restraint toward the LORD's anointed supports the Compendium's teaching on respect for legitimate authority and refusal of vengeance.
