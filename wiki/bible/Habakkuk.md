## Overview
Habakkuk is unique among the prophets: instead of delivering God's word to the people, he brings the people's questions to God. "How long shall I cry for help, and you will not hear?" (1:2). God answers by revealing that He is raising up the Chaldeans as an instrument of judgment — an answer that only deepens the prophet's struggle. The book ends with a psalm of trust sung in the face of total devastation.

## Key Themes
**The Problem of Evil.** Habakkuk's bold questioning models honest prayer. He sees violence and injustice and demands that God account for His silence. God's answer — that He works through even pagan empires — reframes the question. Catholic tradition reads Habakkuk as teaching faith amid unresolved suffering.

**The Just Shall Live by Faith.** Habakkuk 2:4 — "the righteous shall live by his faith" — is quoted three times in the New Testament (Romans 1:17, Galatians 3:11, Hebrews 10:38). It is a cornerstone of Pauline theology and, through Luther, the Reformation. Catholic teaching integrates faith and works, but never diminishes the primacy of faith as the foundation of justification.
