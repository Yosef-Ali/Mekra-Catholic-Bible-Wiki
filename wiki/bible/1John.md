## Overview
1 John, attributed to the same Johannine circle that produced the Fourth Gospel, is not a letter in the conventional sense but a theological treatise and pastoral exhortation directed at a community fractured by schism. Certain former members ("antichrists") had denied that Jesus is the Christ come in the flesh (2:22; 4:2–3), separating the divine Christ from the human Jesus in a proto-docetic heresy. John responds with an integrated theology of love, truth, and obedience: God is light (1:5) and God is love (4:8, 16). The letter oscillates between tests of authentic faith — confessing the incarnation, keeping the commandments, loving the brother — and assurances of salvation for those who abide in Christ. Its purpose is clear: "I write these things to you who believe in the name of the Son of God, that you may know that you have eternal life" (5:13).

## Key Themes
**God Is Love — The Inseparable Bond of Love for God and Neighbor.** 1 John 4:7–21 is the New Testament's most concentrated theology of love: "God is love, and whoever abides in love abides in God, and God abides in him" (4:16). This love is not an abstract attribute but a person — made visible in the sending of God's only Son as the expiation for our sins (4:9–10). John draws the ethical corollary: "if anyone says, 'I love God,' and hates his brother, he is a liar" (4:20). Love for God and love for neighbor are inseparable; one cannot have the vertical without the horizontal. This teaching directly grounds [[teaching/moral-law-and-grace]] and the twofold commandment of love.

**Assurance, Sin, and the Advocate.** 1 John holds tension between two truths: Christians are called to sinlessness ("I am writing these things so that you may not sin," 2:1), yet "if we say we have no sin, we deceive ourselves" (1:8). The resolution lies in the advocacy of Christ: "if anyone does sin, we have an advocate (παράκλητος) with the Father, Jesus Christ the righteous, and he is the expiation (ἱλασμός) for our sins" (2:1–2). This is the biblical foundation for the Sacrament of Penance — the Church continues Christ's ministry of forgiveness for the baptized who fall into sin. John also warns about "antichrists" who deny the Father and the Son (2:22) and calls believers to "test the spirits" (4:1), grounding discernment in the confession of the incarnate Christ.
