## Overview
2 Peter, likely the last New Testament book written (c. AD 100–130), is a "farewell testament" of the apostle Peter written in the face of his imminent death (1:14). The letter confronts a crisis: false teachers who deny Christ's return, mock eschatological hope, and use Christian freedom as license for immorality. Peter responds by affirming the certainty of the apostolic witness (including his own at the Transfiguration, 1:16–18) and the "more sure prophetic word" of Scripture. The letter's most distinctive contribution is its teaching on the "day of the Lord" — not a failure of Christ's promise but the patience of God desiring all to reach repentance, and the ultimate dissolution and renewal of the cosmos in fire.

## Key Themes
**False Teachers — Knowledge without Virtue.** Peter condemns false teachers who "secretly bring in destructive heresies, even denying the Master who bought them" (2:1). Their error is not merely intellectual but moral: they follow the way of Balaam, have eyes full of adultery, and entice unsteady souls with promises of freedom while being themselves slaves of corruption (2:14–19). Against this, Peter calls believers to "make every effort to supplement your faith with virtue, and virtue with knowledge, and knowledge with self-control..." (1:5–7) — a chain of virtues that leads to effective and fruitful knowledge of Christ. Knowledge (γνῶσις) is a key term in 2 Peter, but it is knowledge shaped by virtue, not the speculative gnosis of the false teachers.

**The Day of the Lord — Fire, Patience, and New Creation.** Chapter 3 addresses the scoffers' question: "Where is the promise of his coming?" Peter answers that God's delay is not slowness but patience — "the Lord is not slow to fulfill his promise as some count slowness, but is patient toward you, not wishing that any should perish, but that all should reach repentance" (3:9). The day of the Lord will come like a thief, and the heavens and earth will be dissolved by fire. But Christians await "new heavens and a new earth in which righteousness dwells" (3:13). This eschatological hope grounds [[teaching/forgiveness-resurrection-eternal-life]].
