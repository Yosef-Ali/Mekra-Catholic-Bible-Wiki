## Overview
1 Thessalonians is probably the earliest surviving Christian document, written by Paul from Corinth around AD 50–51, just months after he founded the church in Thessalonica. The letter addresses a young, persecuted community anxious about believers who had died before Christ's return (the parousia). Paul offers comfort with the earliest New Testament teaching on the resurrection of the dead: the Lord himself will descend from heaven with a cry of command, the archangel's call, and the trumpet of God, and the dead in Christ will rise first. The letter also models Paul's pastoral heart — his longing to see them, his delight in their faith, and his exhortation to holiness.

## Key Themes
**The Parousia and the Resurrection of the Dead.** Chapters 4–5 contain the earliest Christian teaching on the end times. Paul assures the Thessalonians that those who have "fallen asleep" in Christ will not be left behind — they will rise first, and then the living will be caught up together with them to meet the Lord. This is not a secret rapture but a public, triumphant arrival of the risen Lord. The day of the Lord will come "like a thief in the night" (5:2), so believers must stay awake and sober, putting on the breastplate of faith and love and the helmet of the hope of salvation (5:8). The passage grounds [[teaching/forgiveness-resurrection-eternal-life]] on the bodily resurrection.

**A Model Church and Pastoral Care.** Paul praises the Thessalonians as a model for believers throughout Macedonia and Achaia — their faith has gone forth everywhere (1:7–8). His pastoral strategy is outlined in chapter 2: he came not with flattery or greed but with gentleness, "like a nursing mother taking care of her own children," sharing not only the gospel but his very self. Chapter 3 recounts Timothy's mission to strengthen them and Paul's overflowing joy at the good report. The letter shows that Christian ministry is not merely doctrinal instruction but the sharing of life.
