## Overview
Tobit is a deuterocanonical book set in the Assyrian exile (8th century BC). It tells the story of Tobit, a righteous Israelite blinded by bird droppings, and his son Tobias, who journeys to Media guided by the angel Raphael. The book weaves together themes of prayer, almsgiving, marriage, and divine providence. It has always been part of the Catholic canon (affirmed at Trent) and holds special significance in Ethiopian tradition where devotion to angels — particularly Raphael — is strong.

## Key Themes
**Faithful Suffering and Prayer.** Tobit's blindness comes while performing a work of mercy (burying the dead). His prayer and that of Sarah (a woman tormented by a demon) rise simultaneously to God, who sends Raphael as the answer. The book teaches that prayer offered in suffering is heard, even when the answer seems delayed. The Rosary's pattern of petition and trust echoes Tobit's spirituality.

**The Angel Raphael and Divine Guidance.** Raphael accompanies Tobias in disguise, arranging his marriage to Sarah and healing Tobit's blindness. "I am Raphael, one of the seven angels who stand before the glory of the Lord" (12:15). The book is foundational for Catholic angelology — angels as protectors, guides, and intercessors. Ethiopian Catholic devotion to the archangels (Michael, Gabriel, Raphael, Uriel) finds rich support here.

**Marriage as Sacred Vocation.** Tobias and Sarah's wedding night is sanctified by prayer before consummation (8:4-8). They rise from the bed to pray together, asking not for lust but for a blessed union. This passage is read in the Catholic marriage liturgy and models the sacramental understanding of matrimony as covenant, not mere contract. Almsgiving, burial of the dead, and honoring parents round out the book's corporal works of mercy.

## Key Passages

- Tobit 8:5-7 — Tobias and Sarah's wedding prayer
- Tobit 12:8 — "Prayer with fasting and almsgiving is good"
- Tobit 12:15 — Raphael reveals his angelic identity
