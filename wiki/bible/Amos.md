## Overview

ትንቢተ አሞጽ (The Book of Amos) is the earliest of the writing prophets and the great prophetic voice for social justice. Amos was not a professional prophet but "a herdsman and a dresser of sycamore figs" (7:14), called by God from Tekoa in Judah to prophesy against the northern kingdom of Israel during the prosperous but corrupt reign of Jeroboam II (c. 760–750 BC). The book's structure is a masterpiece of rhetorical strategy: it opens with oracles against the surrounding nations (chs. 1–2), drawing Israel into applause — until the judgment turns on Israel itself. The core of the book (chs. 3–6) delivers fierce denunciations of social injustice: the rich who trample the poor, the merchants who cheat with false scales, the judges who sell the righteous for silver, and the religious who observe feast days while neglecting justice. Five visions of judgment (chs. 7–9) culminate in the promise of restoration — the fallen booth of David will be raised up. Amos is the prophet of social justice, but his justice is theological: the oppression of the poor is an offense against the God who made heaven and earth and who hears the cry of the afflicted.

## Key Themes

**Let Justice Roll Down — Social Justice as Worship.** Amos' central message is that worship without justice is not merely incomplete but odious to God. "I hate, I despise your feasts... take away from me the noise of your songs" (5:21–23). What God desires instead: "Let justice roll down like waters, and righteousness like an ever-flowing stream" (5:24). Justice (ፍርድ, mishpat) and righteousness (ጽድቅ, tsedaqah) are not abstract ideals but concrete care for the poor, the vulnerable, and the oppressed. This is the prophetic foundation of Catholic social teaching: the option for the poor is not political preference but divine mandate. Amos directly supports [[teaching/moral-law-and-grace]] and the Church's social doctrine.

**The Famine of the Word of God.** Amos 8:11–12 contains one of Scripture's most haunting prophecies: a famine — not of bread or water, but of hearing the words of the LORD. People will wander from sea to sea seeking God's word and will not find it. This prophecy of spiritual famine is both judgment and warning: when a people persistently reject God's word, the withdrawal of that word is itself the judgment. The passage is a profound meditation on [[teaching/faith-and-revelation]] — the word of God is not a possession but a gift that can be withdrawn.

**The Day of the LORD — Unexpected and Demanding.** Amos inverts the popular expectation of "the Day of the LORD" as a day of triumph over enemies: "Woe to you who desire the day of the LORD! ... It is darkness and not light" (5:18–20). The Day of the LORD brings judgment first on Israel, not on its enemies, because covenant privilege brings covenant responsibility. This theology of judgment beginning with the household of God supports [[teaching/ascension-and-judgment]].

**The Restoration of David's Fallen Booth.** The book ends not with judgment but with hope: "In that day I will raise up the booth of David that is fallen" (9:11). James cites this passage at the Council of Jerusalem (Acts 15:16–17) as proof that God always intended to include the Gentiles in the people of God — the rebuilding of David's tent is fulfilled in the Church, where Jews and Gentiles worship together. This promise supports [[teaching/the-church]] and [[teaching/jesus-christ-incarnation]].

## Key Passages

> «ነገር ግን ፍርድ እንደ ውኃ፥ ጽድቅም እንደማይደርቅ ምንጭ ይፍሰስ።»
> — አሞጽ 5:24

The prophet's central demand — let justice roll down like waters and righteousness like an ever-flowing stream. The core Old Testament text for Catholic social teaching.

> «የጌታን ቃላት ከመስማት እንጂ የእንጀራ ራብ ወይም የውኃ ጥማት አይደለም ... የጌታን ቃል ለመፈለግ ይሮጣሉ፤ ነገር ግን አያገኙትም።»
> — አሞጽ 8:11–12

The famine of hearing the word of God — the most terrible judgment is not physical deprivation but the withdrawal of God's word.

> «መልካሙን እንጂ ክፉውን አትፈልጉ ... ክፉውን ጥሉ፥ መልካሙንም ውደዱ፥ በአደባባይም ፍርድን አጽኑ።»
> — አሞጽ 5:14–15

Seek good and not evil — the ethical core of Amos: authentic religion requires concrete commitment to justice in the public square.

> «በዚያ ቀን የወደቀችውን የዳዊትን ድንኳን አነሣለሁ፤ የተነደለውንም ቅጥሩን እጠግናለሁ።»
> — አሞጽ 9:11

The restoration of David's fallen booth — cited by James at the Council of Jerusalem as fulfilled in the Church's inclusion of the Gentiles.

## In the Compendium

Amos is the Old Testament's primary resource for social justice and the integrity of worship:

- **Justice and Righteousness (Amos 5:24):** The demand that justice flow like water is foundational for [[teaching/moral-law-and-grace]] and the Church's social doctrine — the moral law requires justice toward the poor.
- **The Famine of the Word (Amos 8:11–12):** Supports [[teaching/faith-and-revelation]] — the word of God is gift, not possession, and can be withdrawn as judgment.
- **The Day of the LORD (Amos 5:18–20):** Judgment beginning with God's own people supports [[teaching/ascension-and-judgment]].
- **David's Fallen Booth (Amos 9:11–12):** James' citation at the Council of Jerusalem (Acts 15) directly supports [[teaching/the-church]] — the Church is the restored dwelling of David.
- **God as Creator and Judge (Amos 4:13, 5:8–9):** The doxologies embedded in the judgment oracles confess God as Creator, supporting [[teaching/god-the-father-creator]].

## Open questions

- Amos 5:24 — the Emmaus Amharic rendering of "justice" (ፍርድ) and "righteousness" (ጽድቅ): does it distinguish between God's justice and human justice, or use the same vocabulary?
- Amos 9:11–12 in the Septuagint vs. the Hebrew: the LXX reads "that the rest of mankind may seek the Lord" (cited in Acts 15:17), while the MT has "that they may possess the remnant of Edom." Which reading does the Emmaus Amharic follow?
