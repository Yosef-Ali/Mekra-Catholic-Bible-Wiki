## Overview

መዝሙረ ዳዊት (The Book of Psalms, literally "the Psalms of David") is the prayer book and hymnal of Israel, adopted by the Church as the heart of her liturgy — the Divine Office (Liturgy of the Hours) and the responsorial psalm at every Mass. The 150 psalms span centuries of composition, from Moses (Ps 90) to the post-exilic period, traditionally gathered under David's name because he is the archetypal psalmist and the royal ancestor of the Messiah. The Psalter is divided into five "books" (1–41, 42–72, 73–89, 90–106, 107–150), each ending with a doxology, mirroring the five books of the Torah. The psalms cover the full range of human experience — praise, lament, thanksgiving, royal enthronement, wisdom, and imprecation — teaching that no emotion is too raw to bring before God.

The Church reads the Psalms Christologically: Christ is the true David, the one who prayed the psalms in His earthly life and fulfills them in His Paschal Mystery. The messianic psalms (especially 2, 22, 45, 72, 89, 110, 118, and 132) find their definitive meaning in Jesus' incarnation, passion, resurrection, and eternal priesthood. The Psalter is the only book of Scripture whose entirety is prayed liturgically in the Church's cycle — this constant use makes it the most-quoted Old Testament book in the New Testament and the most intimate meeting place between the praying Christ, the praying Church, and the individual believer.

## Key Themes

**Prayer as the Total Response of the Human Person.** The Psalms teach that prayer is not merely intellectual but involves the whole person — body, soul, emotions, memory, and will. The range of forms — hymns of praise (e.g., Ps 150), laments (e.g., Ps 22, 13), thanksgivings (e.g., Ps 30, 116), royal psalms (e.g., Ps 2, 110), and wisdom psalms (e.g., Ps 1, 119) — demonstrates that every human situation is a school of prayer. The psalms teach that praise is the primary language of the creature before the Creator and that lament and even protest (e.g., Ps 44, 88, 137) are legitimate forms of faith — they are not doubts about God's existence but cries to a God believed to be just and powerful. This comprehensive vision of prayer supports [[teaching/prayer-in-christian-life]] and [[teaching/prayer-combat]], which teaches that prayer is a battle against distraction, dryness, and the Enemy.

**Messianic Expectation and Fulfillment in Christ.** The royal psalms paint a portrait of the coming Messiah: the Anointed One enthroned on Zion (Ps 2), the eternal priest according to the order of Melchizedek (Ps 110), the Bridegroom-King (Ps 45), the suffering righteous one whose hands and feet are pierced and whose garments are divided (Ps 22), the stone rejected by the builders that becomes the cornerstone (Ps 118). Jesus Himself uses the psalms to interpret His identity and mission — He quotes Psalm 22 from the cross ("My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?") and Psalm 110 to confound His opponents about the identity of the Messiah (Matthew 22:41–45). The New Testament applies these psalms to Christ's divinity, passion, resurrection, ascension, and eternal reign. These messianic psalms are essential to [[teaching/jesus-christ-incarnation]] and [[teaching/passion-death-resurrection]].

**Liturgical Use: The Voice of Christ and the Church.** The Psalter is the great school of liturgical prayer. The Church prays the psalms not merely as ancient poetry but as the prayer of Christ the Head, with whom the Body joins its voice. The Church has always recognized in the "I" of the psalms the voice of Christ, the voice of the Church, and the voice of the individual Christian simultaneously. Psalms 95 (the invitatory psalm at Matins), the penitential psalms (especially 51, the Miserere during Lent), the Hallel psalms (113–118 at Passover), and the canticles within the psalter structure the Church's daily and festal worship. The psalms are the essential prayer book of [[teaching/the-church]], [[teaching/the-lords-prayer]], and the entire liturgical life.

## Key Passages

> «ጌታ እረኛዬ ነው፥ የሚያሳጣኝም የለም።»
> — መዝሙረ ዳዊት 23:1

The Shepherd Psalm — the most beloved of the psalter, expressing total trust in God's providential care, read Christologically as the Good Shepherd (John 10).

> «አቤቱ፥ እንደ ቸርነትህ መጠን ማረኝ፤ እንደ ምሕረትህም ብዛት መተላለፌን ደምስስ። ከበደሌ ፈጽሞ እጠበኝ፤ ከኃጢአቴም አንጻኝ፤»
> — መዝሙረ ዳዊት 51:3–4

The Miserere — David's prayer of repentance after his sin with Bathsheba, the Church's preeminent penitential psalm, prayed daily in the Liturgy of the Hours.

> «ጌታ ጌታዬን፦ "ጠላቶችህን ለእግርህ መረገጫ እስካደርግልህ ድረስ በቀኜ ተቀመጥ" አለው።»
> — መዝሙረ ዳዊት 110:1

The most-quoted Old Testament verse in the New Testament — the Messiah's enthronement and eternal priesthood, applied to Christ's ascension and session at the Father's right hand.

> «አምላኬ፥ አምላኬ፥ ለምን ተውኸኝ?»
> — መዝሙረ ዳዊት 22:2

The cry of dereliction prayed by Jesus from the cross — the passion psalm that moves from abandonment to universal praise, the pattern of the Paschal Mystery.

> «ኑ፤ በጌታ ደስ ይበለን፤ ለመዳኛችን ዓለት እልል እንበል። በምስጋና ወደ ፊቱ እንድረስ፤ በዝማሬም ለእርሱ እልል እንበል፤»
> — መዝሙረ ዳዊት 95:1–2

The invitatory psalm — the Church's daily call to worship at Matins, celebrating God as the rock of salvation and summoning the faithful to joyful praise.

## In the Compendium

The Psalms pervade the Catechism and the Compendium's teaching on prayer and Christology:

- **Christian Prayer (Psalms as a whole):** The Psalter is the primary biblical school of [[teaching/prayer-in-christian-life]] (Q544–568) — every form of prayer is modeled and taught in the psalms.
- **The Battle of Prayer (Ps 42, Ps 63, Ps 130):** The psalms of longing, thirst, and lament support [[teaching/prayer-combat]] — prayer is a struggle that requires vigilance and perseverance.
- **Christ's Passion and Resurrection (Ps 22, Ps 16, Ps 110):** The messianic psalms are directly quoted in [[teaching/passion-death-resurrection]] (Q112–124) — Jesus Himself prays the psalms in His suffering.
- **The Our Father (Ps 103, Ps 145):** The psalms of praise and blessing form the background of [[teaching/the-lords-prayer]] — the language of divine fatherhood and kingdom is shaped by the Psalter.
- **The Holy Spirit (Ps 104):** The Spirit as the Lord and giver of life echoes the creation psalm, supporting [[teaching/holy-spirit]].
- **Resurrection Hope (Ps 16, Ps 49, Ps 73):** The psalms that express confidence beyond death support [[teaching/forgiveness-resurrection-eternal-life]] (Q105–131).
