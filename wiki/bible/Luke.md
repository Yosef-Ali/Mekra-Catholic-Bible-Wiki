## Overview

የሉቃስ ወንጌል (The Gospel according to Luke) is the longest Gospel and the first volume of a two-part work (Luke–Acts) that traces the movement of salvation from Nazareth to Jerusalem to the ends of the earth. The author, Luke the beloved physician (Colossians 4:14) and companion of Paul, addresses his work to "most excellent Theophilus" (1:3) — likely a Gentile Christian of high standing, though the name (meaning "lover of God") may also address every reader. Composed around AD 80–85, Luke explicitly states his method: he has carefully investigated everything from the beginning, drawing on eyewitness testimony, to write an orderly account (1:1–4). This is the most self-consciously literary of the Gospels, written in polished Greek and structured with a historian's care.

Luke is the Gospel of mercy, of the marginalized, of women, and of the Holy Spirit. It alone contains the parables of the Good Samaritan (10:25–37) and the Prodigal Son (15:11–32), the Magnificat of Mary (1:46–55), the infancy narratives with their intimate detail (1:5–2:52), and the Emmaus road encounter (24:13–35). Luke's Jesus is the Savior of all — Jew and Gentile, rich and poor, righteous and sinner — whose table fellowship with outcasts scandalizes the religious establishment and reveals the radical inclusivity of the Kingdom. The Gospel is punctuated by joy and praise: angels sing at Jesus' birth, the seventy-two return rejoicing, and the Gospel ends with the disciples in the Temple "continually blessing God" (24:53).

## Key Themes

**Universal Salvation and Divine Mercy.** Luke's genealogy traces Jesus back not just to Abraham but to Adam — the universal father of all humanity (3:38) — signaling that Christ's saving work transcends Israel. The great parables unique to Luke (Good Samaritan, Prodigal Son, Rich Man and Lazarus, Pharisee and Tax Collector) all dramatize the reversal of expectations: the outsider is justified, the lost is found, the merciful one is the neighbor. The theme of divine mercy (ἔλεος) runs through the infancy canticles (the Magnificat, Benedictus) and climaxes in Jesus' words from the Cross: "Father, forgive them" (23:34). Luke's vision of salvation is comprehensive — it heals bodies, restores communities, forgives sins, and lifts up the lowly.

**The Holy Spirit and Prayer.** Luke is the Gospel of the Holy Spirit: the Spirit fills John the Baptist from the womb (1:15), overshadows Mary (1:35), descends on Jesus at His baptism (3:22), drives Him into the wilderness (4:1), and anoints Him for His messianic mission (4:18). This same Spirit will be poured out on the Church at Pentecost (Acts 2), making Luke–Acts the great narrative of the Spirit's work in salvation history. Prayer is likewise central: Jesus prays at every decisive moment — at His baptism (3:21), before choosing the Twelve (6:12), at the Transfiguration (9:29), and on the Cross (23:34, 46). The parables of the persistent widow (18:1–8) and the Pharisee and tax collector (18:9–14) are Luke's distinctive teaching on prayer. This emphasis makes Luke especially important for [[teaching/prayer-in-christian-life]].

**The Reversal of the Lowly and the Mighty.** Mary's Magnificat (1:46–55) sets the program for the entire Gospel: God scatters the proud, casts down the mighty, lifts up the lowly, and fills the hungry with good things. This theme plays out in Luke's special attention to the poor, women, Samaritans, tax collectors, and sinners — all those on the margins of respectable society. The rich man who ignores Lazarus at his gate (16:19–31) and the rich fool who builds bigger barns (12:13–21) stand as warnings, while Zacchaeus the tax collector (19:1–10) and the sinful woman who anoints Jesus' feet (7:36–50) embody the transformative power of encountering Christ. This social dimension of the Gospel links Luke directly to Catholic social teaching and [[teaching/society-and-justice]].

## Key Passages

> «እጅግ የተከበርክ ቴዎፍሎስ ሆይ፥ በብዙዎች በእኛ መካከል ስለ ተፈጸሙት ክስተቶች በጽሑፍ ለማዘጋጀት እንደሞከሩት ሁሉ፥ ከመጀመሪያው አንሥቶ የዐይን ምስክሮችና የቃሉ አገልጋዮች የሆኑት ለእኛ ባስተላለፉልን መሠረት፥ ከመጀመሪያው አንሥቶ ስለሆኑት ነገሮች ሁሉ በጥንቃቄ በመመርመር፥ ለአንተ ቅደም ተከተሉን የጠበቀ ታሪክ ልጽፍልህ መልካም ሆኖ ታየኝ።»
> — ሉቃስ 1:1–3

Luke's literary prologue — the most formal in the New Testament — grounding his account in careful investigation and eyewitness testimony.

> «ማርያምም እንዲህ አለች፦ "ነፍሴ ጌታን ታከብረዋለች፤»
> — ሉቃስ 1:46

The opening of the Magnificat — Mary's great hymn of praise, which the Church prays daily at Vespers and which encapsulates Luke's theology of reversal and mercy.

> «እንግዲህ ከነዚህ ከሦስቱ በወንበዴዎች እጅ ለወደቀው ሰው ባልንጀራ የሆነው ማንኛው ይመስልሃል?»»
> — ሉቃስ 10:36

The conclusion of the Good Samaritan — Jesus redefines "neighbor" to include even the despised outsider, making mercy the measure of discipleship.

> «ተነሥቼ ወደ አባቴ እሄዳለሁና እንዲህ እለዋለሁ፤ "አባቴ ሆይ፤ በሰማይና በፊትህ በድያለሁ።»
> — ሉቃስ 15:18

The turning point of the Prodigal Son — the sinner's resolution to return, and the first movement of every authentic conversion.

> «ኅብስትንም አንሥቶ፤ አመስግኖ፤ ቆረሰና እንዲህ ሲል ሰጣቸው፦ "ይህ ስለ እናንተ የሚሰጠው ሥጋዬ ነው፤ ይህን ለመታሰቢያዬ አድርጉት።"»
> — ሉቃስ 22:19

The institution of the Eucharist — the memorial command (ተውስቶ/anamnesis) that the Church fulfills in every Mass. Luke's account uniquely includes "given for you" — emphasizing the personal, sacrificial dimension.

## In the Compendium

Luke's distinctive material enriches Catholic catechesis across multiple domains:

- **Mary and the Magnificat (Luke 1:46–55):** Central to [[teaching/mary]] (Q194–199) — the Magnificat is the Church's great hymn of praise and the model of Marian devotion.
- **The Eucharist (Luke 22:19–20):** Luke's institution narrative, with its "do this in remembrance of me," is foundational for [[teaching/eucharist]] (Q271–294) and the theology of the Mass as memorial sacrifice.
- **The Holy Spirit (Luke 4:18; Acts 2):** Luke–Acts provides the narrative backbone for [[teaching/holy-spirit]] (Q136–146), from the annunciation to Pentecost.
- **Forgiveness and Mercy (Luke 15, 7:36–50):** The parables of mercy and the forgiveness of the sinful woman directly support [[teaching/forgiveness-resurrection-eternal-life]] and the theology of [[teaching/penance]].
- **Prayer (Luke 11:1–13, 18:1–14):** Luke's teaching on persistent prayer and the model of the Lord's Prayer enriches [[teaching/the-lords-prayer]] and [[teaching/prayer-in-christian-life]].
- **Social Justice (Luke 4:18–19, 16:19–31):** Jesus' preferential option for the poor and the parable of the Rich Man and Lazarus are essential for [[teaching/society-and-justice]] and Catholic social teaching.
