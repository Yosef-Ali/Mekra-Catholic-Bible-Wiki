## Overview

ኦሪት ዘፀአት (Exodus, meaning "departure" or "going out") is the second book of the Torah and the great narrative of Israel's liberation from Egypt. The book moves from oppressive slavery (ch. 1) through the call of Moses at the burning bush (chs. 3–4), the ten plagues and the Passover (chs. 7–12), the crossing of the Red Sea (chs. 13–15), the journey to Mount Sinai (chs. 16–18), the giving of the Law and the covenant ratification (chs. 19–24), and the construction of the Tabernacle (chs. 25–40). Exodus is the foundational salvation event of the Old Testament: just as Genesis answers "who created the world?", Exodus answers "who redeemed Israel, and how?" The exodus pattern — God sees, hears, descends, delivers, and binds — becomes the paradigm for every subsequent act of divine redemption, culminating in the Paschal Mystery of Christ.

Catholic tradition reads Exodus typologically and sacramentally. The Passover lamb whose blood saves from the destroyer prefigures Christ, the Lamb of God whose blood delivers from sin and death. The crossing of the Red Sea prefigures Baptism. The manna in the wilderness prefigures the Eucharist. The Tabernacle prefigures both the Incarnation (the Word "tabernacled" among us, John 1:14) and the Church as the dwelling place of God. Exodus is not merely Israel's story — it is the Church's story, the great catechism of liberation, covenant, and worship.

## Key Themes

**The Passover and the Blood of the Lamb.** The tenth plague — the death of the firstborn — and the institution of the Passover (ፋሲካ) are the theological heart of Exodus. The Israelites are commanded to slaughter a year-old unblemished lamb, apply its blood to the doorposts, and eat its flesh with unleavened bread (12:1–14). The blood is the sign: "when I see the blood, I will pass over you" (12:13). The Passover is established as a perpetual memorial (መታሰቢያ), a liturgical feast that makes the saving event present to every generation. The New Testament and the Church read this as directly fulfilled in the Eucharist: Christ our Passover (1 Cor 5:7) — the unblemished Lamb whose blood saves, whose flesh is eaten, and whose sacrifice is made perpetually present in the Mass. Exodus 12 is thus fundamental to [[teaching/eucharist]] and [[teaching/passion-death-resurrection]].

**The Covenant at Sinai and the Gift of the Law.** Chapters 19–24 narrate the great theophany at Mount Sinai, where God descends in fire, smoke, and earthquake to establish a covenant with Israel. The Ten Commandments (20:1–17) are not arbitrary rules but the terms of covenant relationship — the way a redeemed people lives in freedom with God and neighbor. The preamble is not "obey and be saved" but "I am the LORD your God who brought you out of Egypt" (20:2) — grace precedes law. The covenant is ratified with blood: Moses sprinkles the people, saying "Behold the blood of the covenant" (24:8), words Jesus deliberately echoes at the Last Supper (Matthew 26:28). This covenant theology is foundational to [[teaching/moral-law-and-grace]] — the Decalogue remains binding on Christians, not as a means of justification but as the path of the redeemed life.

**The Tabernacle: God Dwells Among His People.** Nearly one-third of Exodus (chs. 25–31, 35–40) is devoted to the instructions for and construction of the Tabernacle (ማደሪያ / የመገናኛው ድንኳን). The structure is not a human invention but built according to the pattern shown on the mountain (25:9, 40). At its completion, the glory cloud (የጌታ ክብር) fills the Tabernacle so powerfully that even Moses cannot enter (40:34–35). The Tabernacle is the original "God with us" — a portable Eden, a microcosm of creation, where heaven and earth meet. The Church reads this as prefiguring the Incarnation (the Word tabernacled among us), the Church (the living temple of God, cf. 1 Peter 2:5), and the individual Christian (the temple of the Holy Spirit, 1 Cor 6:19). The Tabernacle theology supports [[teaching/the-church]], [[teaching/jesus-christ-incarnation]], and [[teaching/holy-spirit]].

## Key Passages

> «እግዚአብሔርም ሙሴን፦ "የምኖር እኔ ነኝ" አለው፤ "ስለዚህ ለእስራኤል ልጆች፦ '"የምኖር" ወደ እናንተ ላከኝ በላቸው'" አለው።»
> — ዘፀአት 3:14

The revelation of the divine name YHWH (I AM WHO I AM / የምኖር) — the foundation of Israel's knowledge of God and the source of Jesus' "I AM" declarations in John's Gospel.

> «ደሙም በምትኖሩባቸው ቤቶች ምልክት ይሆንላችኋል፤ ደሙንም ባየሁ ጊዜ ከእናንተ እልፋለሁ፤ ... ይህም ቀን መታሰቢያ ይሁናችሁ፤ በዓል አድርጋችሁ ጠብቁት፤ ... ለትውልዳችሁ ለዘላለም ሥርዓት አድርጋችሁ ትጠብቁታላችሁ።»
> — ዘፀአት 12:13–14

The institution of the Passover — the blood of the lamb as the sign of salvation, the perpetual memorial that prefigures the Eucharistic sacrifice.

> «"ከግብፅ ምድር ከባርነት ቤት ያወጣሁህ ጌታ አምላክህ እኔ ነኝ።" "ከእኔ በቀር ሌሎች አማልክት አይኑሩህ።"»
> — ዘፀአት 20:2–3

The preamble and first commandment of the Decalogue — the covenant law given at Sinai, grace before demand, the charter of the moral life.

> «ሙሴም ደሙን ወስዶ በሕዝቡ ላይ ረጨው፦ "በእነዚህ ቃሎች ሁሉ ጌታ ከእናንተ ጋር ያደረገው የኪዳኑ ደም እነሆ" አለ።»
> — ዘፀአት 24:8

The ratification of the Sinai covenant in blood — echoed by Christ at the Last Supper ("This is the blood of the new covenant"), connecting the old and new covenants.

> «ከዚህ በኋላ ደመናው የመገናኛውን ድንኳን ሸፈነ፤ የጌታም ክብር ማደሪያውን ሞላ።»
> — ዘፀአት 40:34

The glory of God fills the Tabernacle — the climax of Exodus, God dwelling in the midst of His people, the prototype of the Incarnation and the Church.

## In the Compendium

Exodus provides the biblical foundation for the sacraments and the moral law:

- **The Divine Name (Exodus 3:14):** The revelation of YHWH as "I AM" supports [[teaching/god-the-father-creator]] and Christ's "I AM" declarations in [[teaching/jesus-christ-incarnation]].
- **The Passover (Exodus 12):** The primary Old Testament type of the Eucharist — [[teaching/eucharist]] (Q271–294) draws directly on the Paschal lamb and the perpetual memorial.
- **The Crossing of the Red Sea (Exodus 14–15):** The patristic and liturgical reading of this event as a type of Baptism supports [[teaching/baptism]] (Q252–264).
- **The Decalogue (Exodus 20:1–17):** The moral law is permanently binding; the first three commandments are treated in [[teaching/first-three-commandments]], the remainder in the subsequent commandment pages.
- **The Covenant Blood (Exodus 24:8):** The ratification of the old covenant prefigures the Eucharistic blood of the new, directly underpinning [[teaching/eucharist]] and [[teaching/passion-death-resurrection]].
- **The Tabernacle (Exodus 25–40):** The theology of sacred space and the indwelling presence of God supports [[teaching/the-church]] as the temple of the Holy Spirit.
- **Manna in the Wilderness (Exodus 16):** Prefigures the Eucharist as the bread from heaven, supporting [[teaching/eucharist]].

## Open questions

- The Amharic rendering of the divine name in Exodus 3:14 as "የምኖር" (the one who is/exists) — does this adequately capture the Hebrew אֶהְיֶה אֲשֶׁר אֶהְיֶה (I AM WHO I AM), and is it consistent with how the Septuagint's ὁ ὤν is rendered elsewhere?
- Exodus 12 — the Emmaus edition's rendering of "memorial" (መታሰቢያ) in the Passover institution: does it carry the strong sense of anamnesis (making-present) that the Hebrew זִכָּרוֹן and the Greek ἀνάμνησις carry, or does it lean toward mere remembrance?
- The relationship between the Tabernacle instructions and the heavenly liturgy (cf. Hebrews 8–9) — a dedicated thematic page on "The Tabernacle and the Temple in Salvation History" would serve as a bridge to Hebrews and Revelation.
- The Golden Calf incident (Exodus 32) and Moses' intercession — a profound type of Christ the mediator and Mary's intercessory role, deserving a thematic page.
