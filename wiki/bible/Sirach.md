## Overview

Sirach (also called Ecclesiasticus, "the Church's Book") is the longest wisdom book in the Catholic canon, composed in Hebrew around 180 BC by Jesus ben Sirach (Yeshua ben Sira) and translated into Greek by his grandson around 132 BC. Unlike Proverbs, which is a collection of short sayings, Sirach is a unified work of ethical instruction covering nearly every aspect of life: honor of parents, friendship, wealth and poverty, speech and silence, women and marriage, the priesthood, sacrifice, and the history of Israel. Its tone is conservative and priestly — deeply invested in the Temple, the Law, and the wisdom tradition. The book was widely used in the early Church (hence its Latin name, Ecclesiasticus, "the Church's book") for catechesis and moral instruction. It remains a treasury of practical wisdom for Catholic life.

Sirach 24 is the theological summit: personified Wisdom speaks of her origin from God, her dwelling in Israel, and her identification with the Torah. This passage is read in the Marian liturgy because the Church applies Wisdom's self-description to the Blessed Virgin, the "Seat of Wisdom."

## Key Themes

**Wisdom as the Fear of the Lord.** Sirach opens with the classic wisdom thesis: "The fear of the Lord is the beginning of wisdom" (1:14). But Sirach deepens this by identifying Wisdom with the Torah given at Sinai (ch. 24). Wisdom is not abstract philosophy but the concrete way of life revealed in the Law. This integration of wisdom and law supports [[teaching/moral-law-and-grace]] and the Catholic understanding that moral teaching is not arbitrary rule but the path of life.

**Practical Ethics and Virtue.** Sirach covers everyday morality with extraordinary concreteness: on friendship (6:5-17, 22:19-26), on speech and the tongue (28:13-26), on humility (3:17-29), on wealth and almsgiving (29:8-13), on physicians and medicine (38:1-15), on mourning (38:16-23), and on honoring parents (3:1-16). It is the Bible's most comprehensive ethical manual, deeply formative for [[teaching/virtues]], [[teaching/human-dignity]], and [[teaching/society-and-justice]].

**Honor and Shame.** Sirach reflects a Mediterranean culture of honor and shame, teaching when to be ashamed and when to stand firm (41:14-42:8). It commends honor of parents (3:1-16) as atonement for sins and a path to blessing. The praise of the fathers (chs. 44–50) recounts Israel's heroes from Enoch to the high priest Simon, celebrating their honor and modeling the veneration of the saints. This supports [[teaching/the-church]] and the communion of saints.

## Key Passages

> «እግዚአብሔርን መፍራት የጥበብ መጀመሪያ ነው፤ እርሷም ከታመኑት ጋር ከማኅፀን ጀምራ ተፈጠረች።»
> — ሲራክ 1:14

The beginning of wisdom — the fear of the Lord as the foundation of all moral and spiritual life.

> «እኔ ከልዑል አፍ ወጥቼአለሁ፤ እንደ ጭጋግም ምድርን ሸፈንሁ። ... እንግዲህ እነሆ የሕይወት ፈራጆቼን እሰማቸዋለሁ።»
> — ሲራክ 24:3, 24 (selections)

Wisdom's self-praise — the personification of Wisdom dwelling in Israel, read Mariologically by the Church's liturgy.

> «ልጄ ሆይ፥ በድህነት ዘመን ረዳቱን አትናቀው፤ ለድሆችም ስትሰጥ አታመንታ።»
> — ሲራክ 4:1-2

Care for the poor — Sirach's social ethics, foundational for Catholic social teaching on the preferential option for the poor.
