## Overview

ኦሪት ዘፍጥረት (Genesis, meaning "origin" or "beginning") is the first book of the Torah (Pentateuch) and the foundation of all Scripture. Traditionally attributed to Moses, it narrates the origins of the cosmos, humanity, sin, and the chosen people of Israel. The book divides into two major sections: the Primeval History (chs. 1–11), which tells of Creation, the Fall, the Flood, and the Tower of Babel — establishing the universal human condition under sin — and the Patriarchal History (chs. 12–50), which recounts God's call of Abraham and His covenant promises carried through Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph. Every major Catholic doctrine — creation, original sin, covenant, election, and the protoevangelium (first gospel) — finds its roots in Genesis. The book's opening words "In the beginning" (በመጀመሪያ) are deliberately echoed in John 1:1, binding creation and redemption in the single economy of the Word.

Genesis is not a scientific textbook but theological narrative: it answers the question of who created and why, not how in modern scientific terms. The Church reads Genesis in light of Christ — Adam is "a type of the one who was to come" (Romans 5:14), Eve prefigures Mary the new Eve, and the covenant with Abraham is fulfilled in the new and eternal covenant. The naming of the book in the Amharic tradition — ኦሪት ዘፍጥረት (the Law of Creation) — underscores its place as the first book of the Law and the interpretive key to the entire biblical canon.

## Key Themes

**Creation and the Goodness of Being.** Genesis 1–2 presents a world brought into existence by God's sovereign word, ordered and declared "good" (መልካም) at every stage. Creation is ex nihilo (out of nothing), the work of the Triune God — the Father creates through the Word (cf. John 1:3) and the Spirit hovers over the waters (1:2). Humanity is created in the divine image and likeness (መልክ / ምሳሌ, 1:26–27), male and female, endowed with dignity, freedom, and the vocation to steward creation. This theology of the imago Dei is the bedrock of [[teaching/god-the-father-creator]] and [[teaching/human-dignity]] — every human person possesses an inviolable dignity because they reflect the Creator. The Sabbath rest (2:2–3) sanctifies time itself and establishes the rhythm of work and worship.

**The Fall and the Protoevangelium.** Genesis 3 narrates the catastrophe of original sin: the rebellion of Adam and Eve against God's command, prompted by the serpent. The consequences are cosmic — alienation from God, each other, creation, and self (shame, blame, toil, and death). Yet in the very pronouncement of judgment, God gives the first gospel promise (the Protoevangelium): "I will put enmity between you and the woman, between your seed and her seed; he shall crush your head, and you shall strike his heel" (3:15). The Church reads this as the first promise of the Redeemer: Mary as the new Eve and Christ as the seed who definitively crushes the serpent. This passage fundamentally shapes [[teaching/man-and-the-fall]] and [[teaching/sin]], and is read Mariologically as the scriptural root of the Immaculate Conception.

**The Abrahamic Covenant and Election.** Genesis 12 marks a decisive turn from universal history to particular election. God calls Abram (later Abraham) out of his homeland with a sevenfold promise: land, nation, blessing, a great name, protection, a conduit of blessing to all nations (12:1–3). This covenant — sealed in chapter 15 through a mysterious theophany and in chapter 17 with the sign of circumcision — is unconditional and permanent. Abraham's faith, "reckoned to him as righteousness" (15:6), becomes the paradigm of justification by faith (cf. Romans 4, Galatians 3). The binding of Isaac (ch. 22) prefigures the sacrifice of Christ: the father offers his beloved son, who carries the wood of the sacrifice up the mountain. The covenant with Abraham grounds [[teaching/faith-and-revelation]] and the Catholic understanding of the Church as the new People of God.

## Key Passages

> «በመጀመሪያ እግዚአብሔር ሰማያትንና ምድርን ፈጠረ።»
> — ዘፍጥረት 1:1

The Bible's opening declaration — creation is the free, sovereign act of God, the starting point of salvation history and the foundation of the Creed.

> «እግዚአብሔርም ሰውን በመልኩ ፈጠረ፤ በእግዚአብሔር መልክ ፈጠረው፤ ወንድና ሴት አድርጎ ፈጠራቸው።»
> — ዘፍጥረት 1:27

The creation of humanity in the divine image — the foundation of human dignity, the equality of man and woman, and the call to communion.

> «በአንተና በሴቲቱ መካከል፥ በዘርህና በዘርዋም መካከል ጠላትነትን አደርጋለሁ፤ እርሱ ራስህን ይቀጠቅጣል፥ አንተም ሰኰናውን ትነድፋለህ።»
> — ዘፍጥረት 3:15

The Protoevangelium — the first announcement of the Redeemer, the enmity between the woman and the serpent, read Christologically and Mariologically.

> «እግዚአብሔርም አብራምን አለው፦ ከሀገርህ ከወገኖችህም ከአባትህም ቤት ተለይተህ እኔ ወደማሳይህ ምድር ሂድ። ... የምድር አሕዛብም ሁሉ በአንተ ይባረካሉ።»
> — ዘፍጥረት 12:1, 3

The call of Abraham — the election of a particular people for the sake of universal blessing, the foundation of salvation history and covenant theology.

> «አብራምም በእግዚአብሔር አመነ፤ ጽድቅም ሆኖ ተቈጠረለት።»
> — ዘፍጥረት 15:6

Abraham's faith reckoned as righteousness — the paradigm text for Paul's doctrine of justification by faith (Romans 4, Galatians 3).

## In the Compendium

Genesis is the foundational biblical text for the opening articles of the Creed and Catholic anthropology:

- **Creation (Genesis 1–2):** Primary scriptural source for [[teaching/god-the-father-creator]] (Q36–72) — God creates ex nihilo, through the Word, declaring all creation good.
- **Human Dignity and the Imago Dei (Genesis 1:26–27):** Anchors [[teaching/human-dignity]] — every person is created in God's image, establishing the foundation of Catholic social teaching.
- **The Fall and Original Sin (Genesis 3:1–24):** Essential for [[teaching/man-and-the-fall]] (Q73–83) and [[teaching/sin]] — the origin of sin, concupiscence, and death in human experience.
- **The Protoevangelium (Genesis 3:15):** The first promise of redemption, traditionally read in connection with [[teaching/mary]] and [[teaching/jesus-christ-incarnation]].
- **The Abrahamic Covenant (Genesis 12, 15, 17):** The paradigm of faith and divine election, grounding [[teaching/faith-and-revelation]] — faith is personal trust in God's promise.
- **The Testing of Abraham (Genesis 22):** A type of the Father's sacrifice of the Son, supporting [[teaching/passion-death-resurrection]] and the theology of redemptive suffering.

## Open questions

- The Amharic Emmaus rendering of "image and likeness" (መልክ / ምሳሌ) in Genesis 1:26 — does the translation distinguish the two terms (צֶלֶם / דְּמוּת) consistently, and does it preserve the patristic distinction that some Greek and Latin Fathers drew between image (reason/freedom) and likeness (sanctifying grace)?
- Genesis 3:15 — the translation of the Hebrew "he" (הוּא) vs. the Septuagint "he" (αὐτός) vs. the Vulgate "she" (ipsa). How does the Emmaus Amharic render the subject who crushes the serpent's head, and does this affect the Mariological reading?
- The relationship between Genesis 1–11 and ancient Near Eastern mythologies (Enuma Elish, Atrahasis, Gilgamesh) — a dedicated thematic page (e.g., "Genesis and Ancient Near Eastern Context") would be valuable for apologetics.
- The Joseph narrative (chs. 37–50) as a type of Christ: betrayed by his brothers, sold for silver, descends into the pit, rises to save the nations. This typology deserves its own synthesis page under [[themes/]].
