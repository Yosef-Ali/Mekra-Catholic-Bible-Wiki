## Overview
1 Peter, written from "Babylon" (a code name for Rome) by the apostle Peter through Silvanus as amanuensis (5:12), addresses Christians scattered across Asia Minor who are experiencing social ostracism and suffering for their faith. The letter is a theology of hope under persecution, reframing the believers' identity: they are "a chosen race, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, a people for God's own possession" (2:9) — language drawn from Exodus 19 and now applied to the baptized. Peter grounds the call to holiness in the Paschal Mystery: Christ suffered for us, leaving us an example. The letter also contains the New Testament's most developed reflection on Christ's descent to the spirits in prison (3:18–20), which undergirds the Catholic teaching on the harrowing of hell.

## Key Themes
**Living Stones and a Royal Priesthood.** Peter defines the Church with temple imagery: believers are "living stones" being built into a spiritual house, with Christ as the cornerstone (2:4–6). The whole community — not just office-holders — is "a royal priesthood" (βασίλειον ἱεράτευμα) offering spiritual sacrifices acceptable to God through Jesus Christ (2:5, 9). This common priesthood of the baptized (distinct from and complementary to the ministerial priesthood) is central to [[teaching/the-church]] and [[teaching/baptism]]. The temple language also echoes in the household code (2:13–3:7), where submission and mutual respect within the Christian household reflect the building of the spiritual house.

**Suffering with Christ — The Paschal Pattern.** Suffering is not a sign of divine abandonment but of participation in Christ's own passion. "Rejoice insofar as you share Christ's sufferings" (4:13); "if you are insulted for the name of Christ, you are blessed, because the Spirit of glory and of God rests upon you" (4:14). Peter insists that Christians must not suffer as evildoers but only as those who do good — their suffering must be unmerited, like Christ's. The letter also exhorts elders to shepherd the flock willingly, not for shameful gain but eagerly, with Christ the Chief Shepherd as the model and the "unfading crown of glory" as the reward (5:1–4). This passage grounds [[teaching/holy-orders]].
