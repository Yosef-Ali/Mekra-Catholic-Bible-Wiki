## Overview
Judith recounts the deliverance of the Israelite city of Bethulia from the Assyrian general Holofernes through the courage of Judith, a pious widow. She infiltrates the enemy camp, beheads Holofernes, and returns in triumph. The book is deuterocanonical — accepted by the Catholic and Orthodox Churches but not by Protestant or Jewish canons. In Catholic tradition, Judith is a type of Mary: a woman whose faith and purity defeat the enemy of God's people.

## Key Themes
**God's Victory Through the Weak.** Judith explicitly states that God "breaks war" and that victory comes "not by the spear but by the Lord's hand." A widow with no military training defeats the most powerful general of her age because God chooses the weak to shame the strong. This theology of divine reversal runs through Scripture — from David and Goliath to Mary's Magnificat — and grounds Catholic social teaching's preferential option for the poor.

**Courage, Chastity, and Piety.** Judith is beautiful, wealthy, and devout. She maintains strict observance of dietary laws even in the enemy camp, prays at the appointed hours, and remains chaste throughout her widowhood. Her beauty becomes a weapon for God, not for seduction but for deliverance. She models the Catholic understanding of virtue — not as repression but as directed toward God's purposes.

**Typology of Mary.** The Church's liturgy reads Judith as a Marian figure. "You are the glory of Jerusalem, the joy of Israel" (Judith 15:9) is applied to Mary in the Liturgy of the Hours. Both women are called "blessed among women," both crush the head of the enemy (Judith beheads Holofernes; Mary's seed crushes the serpent), and both intercede for God's people in a time of crisis.
