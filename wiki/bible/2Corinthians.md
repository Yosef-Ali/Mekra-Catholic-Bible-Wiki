## Overview
2 Corinthians is Paul's most personal letter, written in the heat of a crisis in his relationship with the Corinthian church. False "super-apostles" had undermined his authority, and the community had wavered in their loyalty. The letter oscillates between anguish, defense of his apostolic ministry, and soaring theological reflection on suffering, consolation, and the power of God made perfect in weakness. It is essential for understanding Catholic priesthood as a ministry of reconciliation and the theology of redemptive suffering.

## Key Themes
**The Ministry of Reconciliation.** Paul declares that "God was in Christ reconciling the world to himself" and has given the Church "the ministry of reconciliation" (5:18-19). This text is foundational for the Sacrament of Penance — priests act in persona Christi as ambassadors through whom God makes his appeal. The Compendium (Q295-312) grounds the sacrament of reconciliation in this Pauline theology.

**Strength in Weakness.** Paul's "thorn in the flesh" (12:7-10) leads to the revelation: "My grace is sufficient for you, for power is made perfect in weakness." This paradox — that God's power operates through human fragility — is central to Catholic spirituality. It validates the suffering of the sick, the humility of the confessor, and the Church's confidence not in its own strength but in Christ's.

**The Marks of Apostleship.** In chapters 11-12, Paul lists his credentials: imprisonments, beatings, shipwrecks, sleepless nights, hunger, anxiety for the churches. The true apostle is known not by eloquence or letters of recommendation but by suffering for the Gospel. This shapes Catholic understanding of apostolic succession — authority is authenticated by sacrificial service, not self-promotion. The collection for the Jerusalem poor (chapters 8-9) models Christian charity and solidarity among churches.
