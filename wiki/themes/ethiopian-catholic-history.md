## Overview

The Ethiopian Catholic Church is one of the **23 Eastern Catholic Churches** in full communion with Rome. Its existence today is the fruit of **two distinct movements**:

1. **Ancient apostolic Christianity** in Ethiopia (4th c. onward) — shared with the Tewahedo Church.
2. **Catholic missionary engagement** with Ethiopia (16th c. onward) — leading to the **Ge'ez Catholic Rite** in formal communion with Rome.

The Ethiopian Catholic Church preserves **the Ge'ez liturgy, ancient Ethiopian theological vocabulary, and Ethiopian Christian identity** — while being in full communion with the universal Catholic Church centered in Rome.

## I. The apostolic origins (1st–4th c.)

### The Ethiopian Eunuch

The first recorded contact between Ethiopia and the Gospel comes in the New Testament itself. Philip the Deacon (not Philip the Apostle) baptizes a high official of **Queen Candace** of Ethiopia:

> «የሐዋርያዊውም — የኢትዮጵያውያን ንግሥት — ካንዳክስ ጃንደረባ ነበር።
> በመመለስ — በሰረገላው ላይ ተቀምጦ — የነቢዩን የኢሳይያስን ቃል ያነብ ነበር።»
> — የሐዋ. ሥራ 8:27–28

This eunuch — a court official, possibly responsible for the queen's treasury — was reading **Isaiah 53** when Philip joined him. Philip explained the Suffering Servant prophecy as referring to Christ. The eunuch asked for baptism on the spot.

Tradition holds that this eunuch carried the Christian faith back to the kingdom of **Meroë** or **Aksum**. Whether or not the formal Christianization of Ethiopia followed his return, **Acts 8** ensures Ethiopian Christianity has the closest possible apostolic root.

### Frumentius — አባ ሰላማ ከሣቴ ብርሃን (4th c.)

The formal foundation of Ethiopian Christianity came in the 4th century through a Syrian Greek named **Frumentius**, known in Ethiopia as **አባ ሰላማ ከሣቴ ብርሃን** — *"Father of Peace, Revealer of Light."*

A young Christian shipwrecked on the Red Sea coast, he came to serve at the court of **King Ezana of Aksum** (c. 320–360). After helping convert the king and royal household, Frumentius traveled to Alexandria and was ordained the **first bishop of Aksum** by the great Saint **Athanasius the Apostolic** (c. 330 AD).

This single ordination established:

- **Ethiopian Christianity in apostolic succession** — through Athanasius back to Saint Mark the Evangelist.
- **The bond between the Ethiopian Church and the See of Alexandria** — for the next 1,600 years, the head bishop (*Abuna*) of Ethiopia was always sent from Egypt.
- **The Ge'ez liturgy and Bible** — translated in the early centuries from Greek and Syriac sources.

Ethiopian Christianity is therefore **not a Western missionary creation** — it is one of the oldest national Churches in the world, rooted in apostolic succession from the 4th century, predating the Christianization of most of Western Europe.

## II. The Council of Chalcedon and its consequences (5th c.)

In **451 AD**, the **Council of Chalcedon** defined that Christ has **two natures** (divine and human) in **one Person**. The Church of Alexandria — and with her, the daughter Church of Ethiopia — rejected the Chalcedonian formula, holding instead to the formula of Saint Cyril of Alexandria: **one incarnate nature of the Word of God**.

This is **Miaphysitism** (አንድ ባሕርይ — *"one nature"*) — the doctrine of the Ethiopian Tewahedo, Coptic, Syrian, Armenian, and Indian Orthodox Churches, also called the **Oriental Orthodox** family.

The Ethiopian Church became known as the **Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church** (*tewahedo* = "made one") — emphasizing the perfect unity of divinity and humanity in Christ, without confusion or separation.

This split from the See of Rome and Constantinople persisted for **over a thousand years**. The Ethiopian Church preserved unique liturgical, biblical, and devotional treasures during this time — but lost direct communion with the Western Church.

## III. The Jesuit mission (1555–1632)

The first sustained attempt to reunite Ethiopia with Rome came through the **Society of Jesus**.

### The Pioneers

- **Pedro Páez SJ (Spanish, 1564–1622)** — the great pioneer. Captured by pirates in 1589 and enslaved for six years before reaching Ethiopia in 1603. He mastered Ge'ez and Amharic, gained the trust of Emperor Susenyos, and converted him personally to Catholicism. Páez was also the **first European to confirm the source of the Blue Nile** — at Lake Tana — in 1618.
- **Afonso Mendes SJ (Portuguese, 1579–1656)** — appointed **Catholic Patriarch of Ethiopia** in 1622. His arrival in 1626 marked the high point of Catholic presence — and triggered its collapse.

### The brief Catholic kingdom (1622–1632)

Emperor **Susenyos I** publicly converted to Catholicism in 1622 and declared it the state religion. For ten years, the Ethiopian Church was in **formal communion with Rome** — Latin Rite, with the Ge'ez liturgy partially modified.

### The collapse

Mendes's reforms were too aggressive — he insisted on:
- Replacing the Ge'ez liturgy with the Latin Mass.
- Abolishing the Saturday Sabbath alongside Sunday (deeply Ethiopian).
- Re-baptizing the clergy.
- Introducing the Latin calendar.

The Ethiopian people **revolted**. Civil war broke out. In 1632, Susenyos abdicated in favor of his son **Fasilides**, who immediately:
- Restored the Tewahedo Church.
- Expelled the Jesuits in 1633.
- Closed Ethiopia to European missionaries — *"the gates of Ethiopia closed"* — for over 200 years.

The lesson the Catholic Church absorbed (slowly): **Ethiopian Catholicism cannot mean Latinization**. To win hearts, the Catholic faith must be preached in the Ge'ez idiom and in the Ethiopian liturgical tradition. This insight would not be implemented until the 19th–20th centuries.

## IV. The Capuchin and Lazarist missions (19th c.)

After 200 years of closed gates, Catholic missionaries returned cautiously:

- **Saint Justin de Jacobis CM (1800–1860)** — Lazarist missionary, known in Ethiopia as **አባ ያዕቆብ**. He understood the lesson the Jesuits had not: he respected the Ge'ez liturgy, ordained Ethiopian priests in the Ge'ez Rite, learned Ge'ez and Tigrinya, and was eventually consecrated bishop in Massawa. He is **the founder of the modern Ethiopian Catholic Church (Ge'ez Rite)** — beatified 1939, canonized 1975.
- **Cardinal Guglielmo Massaia OFMCap (1809–1889)** — Capuchin missionary to the Galla (Oromo) regions. Worked for 35 years in Ethiopia, mostly in clandestine conditions. Cardinal in 1884.

## V. The 20th century (1930–2000)

### The Italian occupation (1936–1941)

When Mussolini's Italy occupied Ethiopia, the Catholic Church — both Latin and Ge'ez Rite — found itself in a complicated position. **Abune Petros** (አቡነ ጴጥሮስ) — a Tewahedo bishop — was executed by the Italian occupiers for resisting the occupation; he is venerated as a martyr by all Ethiopian Christians. Italian Catholic chaplains served the troops; native Ethiopian Catholics maintained quiet fidelity to their Church under difficult circumstances.

### The Communist Derg era (1974–1991)

After the deposition of Emperor Haile Selassie in 1974, the **Derg** Marxist regime nationalized Church properties, restricted religious life, and banned religious instruction in schools. Ethiopian Catholic seminaries closed; many religious were imprisoned or killed. The Church survived through **catacomb-like fidelity** — small groups, secret catechesis, the daily Mass kept alive in homes.

### Post-1991 revival

Since 1991, the Ethiopian Catholic Church has experienced significant growth and consolidation. Today it includes:

**The Ethiopian Catholic Church (Ge'ez Rite) — Eastern Catholic, in full communion with Rome:**
- **Metropolitan See:** Addis Ababa
- **Eparchies (dioceses):** Adigrat, Bahir Dar–Dessie, Emdibir, Endibir, Hosanna, Ambo, Soddo
- **Approximately 700,000 faithful** (varies by estimate)
- Liturgical language: **Ge'ez** (with Amharic widely used in homily and hymnody)
- The Patriarchate is currently held by **Cardinal Berhaneyesus Demerew Souraphiel CM** (b. 1948, Cardinal 2015) — Archbishop of Addis Ababa, head of the Ethiopian Catholic Church, and a leading voice in African Catholicism.

**The Latin Rite Catholic communities in Ethiopia:**
- Apostolic Vicariates of Awasa, Gambella, Harar, Jimma–Bonga, Meki, Nekemte, Soddo (some have been transferred to Ge'ez Rite).
- Worship in the Roman/Latin Rite, often in Amharic.

## VI. The Ethiopian Catholic Church today

### Distinctives

- **Ge'ez liturgy** — the same Qiddase/Mass tradition shared with the Tewahedo Church, in full communion with Rome.
- **Multiple anaphoras** — including the *Anaphora of the Apostles*, *Our Lord*, *John Chrysostom*, *Mary*, and others. Far richer liturgical variety than the Roman Rite.
- **Wider canon of Scripture** — preserves the same biblical canon as the Tewahedo Church, including Enoch, Jubilees, etc., while affirming the Catholic deuterocanonical books at minimum.
- **Married priesthood** — like other Eastern Catholic Churches, married men may be ordained priests (though bishops are taken from celibate clergy).
- **Saturday Sabbath observance** continues alongside Sunday — uniquely Ethiopian.
- **Fasting tradition** of unmatched intensity — Lent, Advent, Apostles' Fast, Assumption Fast, Wednesdays, Fridays.
- **Marian devotion** (especially **ፍልሰታ ለማርያም**) — the deepest in the universal Catholic Church.

### Strengths

- **Deeply rooted Ethiopian identity.** Not a foreign import.
- **Rich liturgical and devotional life.** Centuries of contemplation distilled into prayer.
- **Bridge between Catholic and Tewahedo** — full communion with Rome while preserving the heritage shared with the Orthodox brethren.
- **Strong vocations.** Seminaries are full; African Catholicism is one of the fastest-growing in the world.

### Challenges

- **Numerical minority.** Ethiopian Catholics are a small fraction of Ethiopian Christians (Tewahedo Orthodox dominate).
- **Tensions** with the Tewahedo Church, sometimes amicable, sometimes not — especially regarding evangelization and the appearance of "stealing" Tewahedo faithful.
- **Persecution from secularism and Pentecostal pressure** — Pentecostal/evangelical movements have grown rapidly and view the Catholic Church and Tewahedo Church alike as un-evangelical.
- **Tigray War (2020–2022)** and ongoing instability — devastating Adigrat eparchy and the northern Catholic communities.

## VII. The continuing journey

The Ethiopian Catholic Church inherits both the **2000-year apostolic tradition** of Ethiopian Christianity and the **2000-year visible communion** of the universal Catholic Church. Her vocation is to be a **bridge**:

- Between Catholic and Tewahedo Christians — one in apostolic faith, separated visibly since Chalcedon.
- Between Africa and Rome.
- Between the ancient liturgical East and the ancient teaching authority of Peter.

The path forward is the path Saint Justin de Jacobis taught and the Second Vatican Council affirmed: **respect the heritage, preach Christ, work for unity in truth and charity.**

> «የሁሉም ቤተክርስቲያን — የቅዱስ ሐዋርያዊ ጅረቷ — በምሥጢራዊ አንድነት — ጸንታ ትኖራለች።»
