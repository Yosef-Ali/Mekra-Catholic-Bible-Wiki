# Sola Fide? — Faith Alone?

**Type:** apologetics
**Question:** Are we saved by faith alone, with works playing no role?
**Compendium Q:** 422–428
**CCC:** 1987–2029
**Sources:** 5
**Last updated:** 2026-05-09
**Related:** [[teaching/moral-law-and-grace]], [[teaching/faith-and-revelation]], [[concepts/ጸጋ]], [[concepts/እምነት]], [[concepts/ጽድቅ]]

## The challenge

> *"We are saved by faith alone (Sola Fide), not by works. Catholics teach a 'works-righteousness' that contradicts the Gospel."*

This was Luther's central rallying cry. It is also the one place where the Bible **explicitly** uses the phrase — to *deny* it.

## The single verse with "faith alone"

> «ሰው በሥራ ይጸድቃል — **በእምነት ብቻ አይደለም**።»
> *"You see that a man is justified by works **and not by faith alone**."*
> — ያዕቆብ 2:24

This is the **only** place in the New Testament where the phrase "faith alone" appears — and it is **directly negated**. Luther was so disturbed by this that he tried to remove the Letter of James from the canon, calling it "an epistle of straw."

The Catholic Church kept James. The Protestant Bibles that retain James have to wrestle with this verse — usually by redefining "justified" to mean something different from how Paul uses it. But James is not contradicting Paul; both are addressing different errors.

## Paul and James in harmony

**Paul** writes against Jewish Christians who thought the Mosaic Law (circumcision, kosher food, Sabbath) was necessary for salvation:

> «ሰው የሚጸድቀው — በሥርዓቱ ሥራ ሳይሆን — በኢየሱስ ክርስቶስ እምነት መሆኑን አወቅን።»
> — ገላትያ 2:16

Paul's "works" = **the works of the Mosaic Law** — circumcision, ritual. He is *not* saying that Christian moral life and obedience are irrelevant.

**James** writes against people who claim to have faith but live without love:

> «እምነት ሥራ ከሌለው — ራሱ ብቻውን — ሙት ነው።»
> — ያዕቆብ 2:17

James's "works" = **the fruit of charity** — caring for the poor, controlling the tongue, visiting the orphan and widow. He is saying: a faith that does not love is no real faith at all.

The Council of Trent (1547) and the Catholic Church teach exactly this: **we are saved by grace through a faith that works through love** (ገላ. 5:6).

## What Paul actually says about works

Read Paul carefully and the Catholic position is plain:

> «በፍቅር **የሚሠራ እምነት** ብቻ ዋጋ አለው።»
> — ገላትያ 5:6

> «ይህን ሁሉ የተሰጠ ጸጋ — በበጎ ሥራ የበዛ — እምነት — ተስፋ — ፍቅር — በውስጣችሁ እንዲኖሩ —»

> «የጻድቃን ሥራ — በፊቱ መልካም ነው።»
> *"For we are his workmanship, created in Christ Jesus for good works…"*
> — ኤፌሶን 2:10

Even in his most "Sola Fide" passage (Ephesians 2:8–9), the very next verse says God created us **for good works** which are necessary for the Christian.

## What Christ himself says

Jesus's teaching on the final judgment leaves no room for "faith alone":

> «… ጻድቃንን ለተኳቸው ለሆኑት እንዲህ ይላል —
> "ተራብሁ — አበላችሁኝ።
> ተጠማሁ — አጠጣችሁኝ።
> እንግዳ ነበርኩ — ተቀበላችሁኝ።
> ራቁቴን ነበርኩ — አለበሳችሁኝ።
> ታመምኩ — ጠየቃችሁኝ።
> ታሰርሁ — መጣችሁልኝ።"
> … ለነዚህ ለታናናሾች ወንድሞቼ ላደረጋችሁት — ለእኔ አደረጋችሁት።»
> — ማቴዎስ 25:35–40

The judgment is rendered on the basis of **works of mercy**. Not "did you say you believed?" but "did you feed the hungry, visit the sick, welcome the stranger?"

> «በደጅ የቆመ — ጌታ ጌታ የሚለኝ ሁሉ መንግሥተ ሰማያት አይገባም — የአባቴን ፈቃድ የሚያደርግ እንጂ።»
> — ማቴዎስ 7:21

## The Catholic teaching simply

1. **No one earns salvation.** Salvation is a free gift of God. We can do nothing to deserve God's love.
2. **Initial justification is by grace alone, through faith.** When God justifies the sinner — washing away sin in baptism — this is pure gift.
3. **The justified must persevere in love.** Faith without love is dead. Love expresses itself in works of mercy and obedience to the commandments.
4. **Mortal sin can destroy charity.** A baptized Christian who freely commits grave sin needs **repentance and the sacrament of confession** to be restored.
5. **Final salvation is the perseverance of grace-empowered love.** Not earning, but **cooperating** with grace.

This is exactly what St. Augustine summarized: *"He who created us without us will not save us without us."*

## A common Protestant question

> *"If works are necessary, then we earn our salvation."*

Catholics answer:

- **No.** The works themselves are the **fruit of grace** working in us. Even our cooperation is enabled by God's prior grace.
- **The works do not buy salvation.** They prove that grace is alive in the soul.
- **Without works, faith is a corpse** (James 2:26) — not real faith at all.

Saint Paul: *"I worked harder than any of them, though it was not I, but the grace of God that is with me"* (1 ቆሮ. 15:10). Work and grace are not enemies; they collaborate.

## The Reformation's overshoot

Luther was reacting to real abuses — particularly the sale of indulgences. The Catholic Church has acknowledged those abuses (Council of Trent reforms; Vatican II further). But Luther's *solution* — Sola Fide — overshot the truth.

The Catholic Church teaches:

- **Against Pelagianism** (humans save themselves by effort): salvation is grace.
- **Against Sola Fide** (faith without works): the saved must love.
- **For the apostolic balance**: grace empowers us to love, and love is the proof of faith.

## A note on the Joint Declaration

In 1999, the Catholic Church and the Lutheran World Federation signed a **Joint Declaration on the Doctrine of Justification**. Lutherans and Catholics agreed that:

- Justification is by grace alone, through faith.
- Good works necessarily follow as the fruit of justification.
- The Reformation-era condemnations no longer apply, given mutual clarification.

This is itself a sign that the apparent "either/or" was always a misreading of both sides.
