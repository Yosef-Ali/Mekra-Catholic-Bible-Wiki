# Why Infant Baptism?

**Type:** apologetics
**Question:** Why does the Catholic Church baptize infants who can't consciously believe?
**Compendium Q:** 257–262
**CCC:** 1250–1252
**Sources:** 5
**Last updated:** 2026-05-09
**Related:** [[teaching/baptism]], [[teaching/practical-baptism]], [[concepts/ጥምቀት]], [[concepts/ጸጋ]], [[apologetics/sola-fide]]

## The challenge

> *"Baptism requires personal faith. Babies can't believe, so infant baptism is invalid. Only believer's baptism is biblical."*

Many Protestant denominations (especially Baptists, Pentecostals, Evangelicals) reject infant baptism. The Catholic, Orthodox, Lutheran, Anglican, Methodist, Reformed, and ancient Eastern Christian traditions — including the Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church — **all** practice infant baptism. The custom is **older than the New Testament canon itself**.

## The biblical case

### 1. Whole households were baptized

Acts records multiple **household baptisms** with no exclusion of infants:

- **The household of Lydia** — Acts 16:15
- **The household of the Philippian jailer** — Acts 16:33
- **The household of Stephanas** — 1 Corinthians 1:16
- **The household of Cornelius** — Acts 10:48
- **The household of Crispus** — Acts 18:8

In a 1st-century Jewish or Greek household, "household" (οἶκος, ቤት) included children — including infants. The text never says "all the adults of the house"; it says **the household**.

### 2. Baptism replaces circumcision

> «በእርሱም — በእጅ ባልተገኘ መገረዝ — የሥጋ ኃጢአትን አስወገዳችሁ — ይኸውም **የክርስቶስ መገረዝ ነው።**
> ከእርሱ ጋር በጥምቀት ተቀበራችሁ።»
> — ቆላስይስ 2:11–12

Paul explicitly identifies **baptism as the new-covenant fulfillment of circumcision**. Circumcision was given to **infants on the eighth day** (ዘፍ. 17:12). If the new covenant is *better* and *more universal* than the old, would it shut out the children the old covenant included?

### 3. The Kingdom belongs to children

> «ሕፃናቱ ወደ እኔ ይምጡ — አትከልክሏቸው — የእግዚአብሔር መንግሥት የእንደነዚህ ናትና።»
> — ማርቆስ 10:14

If the Kingdom belongs to children, why would the **doorway** to the Kingdom — baptism (ዮሐ. 3:5) — be closed to them?

### 4. Peter's Pentecost promise

> «ንስሐ ግቡ — ሁሉም በኢየሱስ ክርስቶስ ስም ይጠመቅ — ኃጢአታችሁ ይሰረይ ዘንድ — የመንፈስ ቅዱስም ስጦታ ትቀበላላችሁ።
> **ተስፋው ለእናንተና ለልጆቻችሁ — ለሩቆችም ሁሉ ነው።**»
> — የሐዋ. ሥራ 2:38–39

Peter explicitly extends the baptismal promise to **"your children"** — not just adults capable of confession.

## The historical case

### The Apostolic Fathers (1st–2nd centuries)

- **Irenaeus of Lyons** (~AD 180): *"Christ came to save all through himself — all, I say, who through him are reborn unto God: infants, children, boys, youths, and old men."* (*Against Heresies* II.22.4)
- **Hippolytus of Rome** (~AD 215): *"Baptize first the children, and if they can speak for themselves, let them do so. Otherwise, let their parents or other relatives speak for them."* (*Apostolic Tradition* 21)
- **Origen** (~AD 244): *"The Church received from the apostles the tradition of giving baptism even to infants."*
- **Cyprian of Carthage** (AD 251): A council of 66 bishops debated whether to baptize on the **eighth day** (matching circumcision) or earlier; concluded earlier was permitted. **They never debated whether to baptize infants at all** — that was assumed.

For 1,500 years, infant baptism was the **universal Christian practice**. The Anabaptists ("re-baptizers") in the 16th century were the first significant group to reject it — and were condemned by Catholics, Lutherans, and Reformed alike.

## The theological case

### 1. Original sin needs cleansing

Every human is born with **original sin** inherited from Adam (ሮሜ 5:12, ኤፌ. 2:3). Baptism washes original sin away (የሐዋ. ሥራ 2:38, ቲቶስ 3:5). To withhold baptism from a child is to leave them in the wound of original sin until they can "decide" — gambling on their salvation.

### 2. Faith is given, not generated

Faith is itself a **gift** (ኤፌ. 2:8). It is not something the human soul produces. The infant's faith is the faith of the Church — the faith of parents, godparents, and the whole Christian family that surrounds the child. As the child grows, this received faith becomes personal.

This is exactly what happens in adult catechesis too: no adult comes to faith by generating it; they receive it from those who taught them.

### 3. Salvation is grace, not understanding

If faith required intellectual comprehension before grace can be given, then the mentally disabled, the comatose, and infants would all be excluded from salvation. That is not the Gospel. **God's grace works in souls who cannot yet articulate, just as he formed John the Baptist in his mother's womb (ሉቃ. 1:41).**

## Common objections answered

### "But Acts 2:38 says repent first, then be baptized."

That's addressed to **Jews on Pentecost being called to convert**. Of course adult converts must repent and believe first. The text doesn't address children of believers — but verse 39 does: *"the promise is for you and your children."*

### "Babies can't have faith."

Catholic theology distinguishes:
- **Habitus fidei** (the gift of faith infused at baptism) — given to the soul.
- **Actus fidei** (the act of believing) — required of those capable of it.

Infants receive the gift; as they mature, they grow into the act.

### "What if the child grows up and doesn't believe?"

Then they reject the grace they were given — but this is also possible for an adult who was baptized at 30 and later falls away. Apostasy is a human possibility at any age. Baptism is not magic; it requires lifelong cooperation.

### "Christ was baptized at 30, so we should be too."

Christ was baptized as an adult to **inaugurate his public ministry** and to fulfill all righteousness, not as a normative pattern for Christian baptism. The same logic would require us to be born in a stable and crucified at 33.

## The Ethiopian Catholic practice

Ethiopian Catholic infants are baptized following the ancient practice — usually on the **40th day for boys, the 80th day for girls** (echoing the Levitical purification of Mary, ሉቃ. 2:22) — though this timing is customary, not mandatory.

The Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church follows the same practice. **On infant baptism, Catholic and Ethiopian Orthodox tradition are united; only post-16th-century Protestant groups dissent.**
