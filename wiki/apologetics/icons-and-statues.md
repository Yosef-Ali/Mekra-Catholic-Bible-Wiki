# Are Icons and Statues Idolatry?

**Type:** apologetics
**Question:** Doesn't the Second Commandment forbid images of God and the saints?
**Compendium Q:** 240, 446
**CCC:** 2129–2132
**Sources:** 4
**Last updated:** 2026-05-09
**Related:** [[teaching/first-three-commandments]], [[apologetics/mary-veneration]], [[concepts/አምልኮ]], [[teaching/jesus-christ-incarnation]]

## The challenge

> *"The Bible says: 'You shall not make for yourself a graven image.' Yet Catholic and Orthodox churches are full of statues, icons, and images of Mary, the saints, and Christ. Isn't this idolatry?"*

This is one of the oldest accusations against historic Christianity (Catholic, Orthodox, Ethiopian Tewahedo, Coptic) — leveled by 8th-century iconoclasts, then by 16th-century Protestants, and today by some Pentecostal and Evangelical groups.

It rests on a **misreading of the commandment** and a misunderstanding of the **Incarnation**.

## What the commandment actually says

> «በሰማይ ካለ — በምድር ካለ — ከውሃ በታች ካለ —
> ለራስህ የተቀረጸ ምስል — የማንኛውንም ምስል —
> **አታድርግ።**
> **አታመልካቸው — አታገልግላቸውም።**»
> — ዘጸአት 20:4–5

The commandment is **two parts**:

1. **Do not make** carved images **for the purpose of worshipping them as gods**.
2. **Do not bow down to them or serve them.**

These two clauses must be read together. The prohibition is against **making idols and worshipping them** — not against making any image whatsoever. We know this because **God himself commanded the making of images** in the very next chapters of the Bible.

## God commands images

Within **chapters of giving the Ten Commandments**, God commands Moses to make images:

### Two cherubim of gold

> «ሁለት ኪሩቤልን ከወርቅ — በመዶሻ የተቀረጹ — ለመስዋዕት መሸፈኛ ሁለት ፅንፎች ላይ — አድርግ።»
> — ዘጸአት 25:18

Two **golden statues of angels** — placed on top of the Ark of the Covenant, in the Holy of Holies, the holiest space of Israel. God himself ordered them.

### The bronze serpent

> «ሙሴ — የናስ እባብ ሠራ — በዘንግ ላይ ሰቀለው።
> በእባቡ የተነከሰ ሁሉ — ወደ የናስ እባብ ቢመለከት — ሕያው ይሆናል።»
> — ዘኁልቁ 21:9

A **bronze serpent on a pole** — and looking at it brought healing. Christ himself draws the typological parallel to the cross (ዮሐ. 3:14).

### The temple of Solomon

The Temple was filled with images:

- **Two enormous cherubim** in the Holy of Holies (1 ነገ. 6:23–28)
- **Cherubim, palm trees, open flowers** carved on every wall (1 ነገ. 6:29)
- **Twelve bronze oxen** holding up the great basin (1 ነገ. 7:25)
- **Lions, oxen, cherubim** on the bronze stands (1 ነገ. 7:29)

If images per se were forbidden, **the Temple of Solomon was the most idolatrous building in the ancient world**. But it wasn't — because it had been **commanded by God**.

## So what is forbidden?

The commandment forbids:

- **Idol-making** — fashioning a "god" of wood, stone, or metal that is then worshipped as divine.
- **Worship of created things** — bowing down to creation in place of the Creator.
- **Magical use** — treating images as containing power apart from God.

It does not forbid:

- Making images of holy things.
- Honoring them as memorials and aids to prayer.
- Bowing toward them (as Israelites bowed before the Ark — 1 ሳሙ. 4:4–6).

The Hebrew word *avodah* (worship) refers to the **interior reality of adoration** — not the outward gesture. You can bow before a king without worshipping him.

## The Incarnation changes everything

This is the deepest theological point. The 8th-century Iconoclasts were answered definitively by **St. John of Damascus** and the **Second Council of Nicaea (787 AD)**:

> *"Of old, God the incorporeal and uncircumscribed was never depicted. But now, when God has been seen clothed in flesh, and conversed with men, I make an image of the God whom I see. I do not worship matter; I worship the God of matter, who became matter for my sake."*

Before the Incarnation, no image of God was possible — God was invisible spirit. **But now God has appeared in flesh.** The face of Jesus is the face of God. To say we cannot make an image of Christ is to deny that Christ truly took flesh — a Christological heresy.

This is why the Second Council of Nicaea declared image-making **doctrinally necessary**: to deny images is to deny that God really became man.

## The crucial distinction: *latria*, *dulia*, *hyperdulia*

The Catholic and Orthodox traditions carefully distinguish:

| Term | Meaning | Owed to |
|---|---|---|
| **Latria** (አምልኮ) | Adoration — owed only to God | God alone |
| **Dulia** (ክብር) | Veneration / honor — given to saints, angels | Holy creatures |
| **Hyperdulia** | Special honor (above other saints) | Mary alone, the Mother of God |

When a Catholic kisses an icon of Christ, **latria** is offered to Christ himself, of whom the icon is a representation. When the same person kisses an icon of Mary, **hyperdulia** is offered to her — honor as the Mother of God, never adoration.

This is the same distinction English uses when we say *"I love my mother"* and *"I love God"* — the same word, but everyone understands the difference.

## How icons function

In the Eastern Christian tradition (Orthodox, Ethiopian, Eastern Catholic), an icon is:

- **A window** — through which the gaze passes from the painted surface to the saint depicted.
- **A theology in colors** — teaching the faith without words to those who cannot read.
- **A presence** — not the saint himself, but a sign that draws the heart to the saint.
- **A reminder** — of the great cloud of witnesses (ዕብ. 12:1).

Saint John of Damascus: *"What the book is to the literate, the icon is to the illiterate. What speech is to hearing, the icon is to sight."*

## Common objections answered

### "But people kiss icons!"

So did Israelites kiss the Torah scroll. So does Pope Francis kiss the altar. So does a child kiss the photograph of a deceased parent. **A kiss is honor, not worship** — easily understood.

### "But people light candles!"

Candles are signs of prayer rising to heaven (ራዕይ 8:3–4). They mark the prayer offered, not the icon itself.

### "But what about miracles attributed to icons?"

When God works miracles through the bones of Elisha (2 ነገ. 13:21), the shadow of Peter (Acts 5:15), or handkerchiefs touched to Paul (Acts 19:12), these are **created things mediating God's grace** — exactly the principle of sacramentals. Miracles through icons are biblically consistent.

## The Ethiopian icon tradition

Ethiopian Christianity has produced a **distinctive iconographic tradition** for over 1,500 years — frescoes in rock-hewn churches, painted Bible scrolls, magic-defeating images of saints. These are part of Ethiopia's Christian heritage, **shared between Catholic and Orthodox** Ethiopian believers.

To reject icons is to reject not only Catholic teaching but the entire Ethiopian Christian artistic and spiritual patrimony.
