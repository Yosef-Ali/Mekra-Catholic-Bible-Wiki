# Apostolic Succession — Why It Matters

**Type:** apologetics

## What apostolic succession means

The doctrine that **Christ's authority and ministry pass through the apostles to their successors — the bishops** — by the **laying on of hands** in an unbroken line from the apostles to today.

Every bishop today can trace his ordination back through history to one of the original twelve apostles. This is not a metaphor. It is a **historical fact** — the lineage can be documented for many sees.

## The biblical foundation

### 1. Christ commissioned the apostles

> «… ሁሉን አሕዛብ ደቀ መዛሙርት አድርጓቸው።
> በአብ — በወልድ — በመንፈስ ቅዱስ — ስም አጥምቋቸው።
> ያዘዝኳችሁንም ሁሉ — እንዲጠብቁ አስተምሯቸው።
> እነሆ — እኔ ከእናንተ ጋር ነኝ — እስከ ዓለም ፍጻሜ ድረስ።»
> — ማቴዎስ 28:19–20

Christ's promise — *"I am with you always, to the end of the age"* — is given **to the apostles as office-bearers**, not just as individual men. The apostles all died within decades. The promise extends "to the end of the age" — therefore it extends to **their successors**.

### 2. The apostles ordained successors

The New Testament shows the apostles **immediately appointing successors**:

> «በቤተክርስቲያኑ ሽማግሌዎችን — እጅ በመጫን ሾመልኝ።»
> — የሐዋ. ሥራ 14:23

Paul and Barnabas appointed **presbyters (priests)** in every city.

> «ስለዚህ — በተሰቀለ ቦታ — ትቼ ነበርኩህ።
> በከተማ ሁሉ ሽማግሌዎችን ሹም — እኔ እንዳዘዝኩ።»
> — ቲቶስ 1:5

Paul leaves Titus in Crete with the explicit task of **ordaining bishops in every town**.

> «ቲሞቴዎስ — በእጄ መጫን — የተቀበልከውን — የእግዚአብሔርን ጸጋ — እንዳትቸልል — አሳስብሃለሁ።»
> — 2ኛ ጢሞ. 1:6

Timothy received his ministerial gift through **the laying on of Paul's hands**.

### 3. Replacing Judas — the pattern

The very first apostolic action after the Ascension was to **replace Judas**:

> «የእርሱም ስፍራ ሌላ ይውሰድ።»
> *"Let another take his office."*
> — የሐዋ. ሥራ 1:20

The apostles understood the **apostolic office (ἐπισκοπή — bishopric)** as something that must be **filled when vacated**. They did not say, "Judas is dead, the office is closed." They sought a successor — Matthias.

This same pattern — succession of office, not just personal authority — has continued for 2,000 years.

## The historical record

### The Apostolic Fathers

The first generation after the apostles testifies to apostolic succession explicitly:

**St. Clement of Rome** (~AD 96), writing to the Corinthians during the lifetime of the apostle John:

> *"Our Apostles… appointed [bishops] and afterwards added the codicil that, if these should fall asleep, other approved men should succeed to their ministry."*
> — *1 Clement* 44

**St. Ignatius of Antioch** (~AD 107), bishop ordained by John, on his way to martyrdom:

> *"Wherever the bishop appears, let the people be there. As wherever Christ Jesus is, there is the Catholic Church."*
> — *Letter to the Smyrneans* 8

**St. Irenaeus of Lyons** (~AD 180):

> *"It is necessary to obey those presbyters who are in the Church — those who possess the succession from the apostles… who have received the certain gift of truth according to the will of the Father."*
> — *Against Heresies* IV.26.2

Irenaeus actually **lists the bishops of Rome** from Peter to his own day to refute the Gnostics' claim of secret apostolic teaching.

### Episcopal lists — written down from the start

Every major see of the early Church kept and published its line of bishops back to an apostle:

- **Rome** — Peter → Linus → Cletus → Clement → … (continuous to today)
- **Alexandria** — Mark → Anianus → … (to the Coptic and Ethiopian patriarchates)
- **Antioch** — Peter → Evodius → Ignatius → … (continuous)
- **Jerusalem** — James the Less → Symeon → … (continuous)
- **Ethiopia** — Frumentius (አባ ሰላማ ከሣቴ ብርሃን), consecrated by St. Athanasius of Alexandria around 330 AD — and the line continues unbroken in the Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo and Ethiopian Catholic Churches today.

## Why apostolic succession matters

### 1. To guarantee the deposit of faith

The apostles received the truth from Christ. If anyone today wants to know what the apostles taught, they go to the **community founded by the apostles** — not to a self-appointed teacher of any age. Apostolic succession is the **historical chain of custody** of the Gospel.

> *"What is the use of telling people that they have grace if they aren't sure that the apostolic deposit has come down to them whole?"* — Irenaeus

### 2. To validate the sacraments

Some sacraments — especially the **Eucharist, Confession, Confirmation, Holy Orders** — require an ordained priest or bishop. **Without apostolic succession, the priest's words at the consecration are just words.**

This is the crucial point that distinguishes Catholic, Orthodox, and Ethiopian traditions from most Protestant denominations: we believe Christ's promise — *"This is my Body"* — is fulfilled only when the words are spoken by one **ordained in the apostolic line**.

### 3. To preserve unity

Without succession, every movement, every charismatic leader, every Bible reader can declare himself "called by God" — and the result is the 30,000+ Protestant denominations. Apostolic succession is the **structural unity** Christ prayed for in John 17.

## Common objections answered

### "Christ's true Church is invisible — there is no need for visible succession."

But Christ founded a **visible** Church (Mt. 5:14, 16:18). He gave it visible sacraments, a visible head (Peter), and visible structure. The "invisible Church" idea is a 16th-century rationalization for not being able to find one's church in the historical record.

### "What about apostolic teaching, not apostolic succession?"

The two cannot be separated. **Apostolic teaching has been preserved through apostolic succession.** The Bible itself was canonized by bishops in apostolic succession (Rome, Hippo, Carthage). Without succession, we have no way to know what is genuinely apostolic.

### "Some bishops have been corrupt."

True. But **the office is held not by personal merit but by ordination**. Even a bad bishop validly ordains; even a corrupt priest validly consecrates. This is what protects the Church from depending on the holiness of any individual minister.

### "Anglicans claim succession too — and they're not Catholic."

Pope Leo XIII in *Apostolicae Curae* (1896) declared Anglican orders invalid because of the changes made to the rite of ordination during the Reformation. The Catholic Church recognizes the validity of:
- All **Eastern Orthodox** orders (Greek, Russian, Antiochian, etc.).
- The **Oriental Orthodox** orders (Coptic, Syrian, Armenian, **Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo**).
- The **Old Catholic** churches.

These have unbroken apostolic succession even where they are not in full communion with Rome.

## The Ethiopian case

The **Ethiopian Catholic Church (Geez Rite)** maintains the apostolic succession of the ancient Ethiopian Christian tradition. Bishops of the Ethiopian Catholic Church can trace their ordination back through:

- The Ethiopian patriarchate, through
- The Coptic Orthodox patriarchate of Alexandria, through
- St. Mark the Evangelist, through
- St. Peter the Apostle, through
- Christ.

This is why an Ethiopian Catholic bishop's authority is not "imported from Rome" — it is **the same apostolic line** that Ethiopian Orthodox Christians revere, in full communion with the universal Catholic Church.

## A simple test

Ask any bishop: *"From whom did you receive the laying on of hands?"* He will name his consecrating bishop. Ask that bishop the same question. Continue back. Within 50–80 generations, you reach an apostle.

For a Catholic bishop, this list is documented. For most Protestant ministers, the list ends with someone in the 16th–20th century who founded the denomination — there is no further chain.

This is what apostolic succession means in practice: **the unbroken historical reception of Christ's commission from his own hands.**
