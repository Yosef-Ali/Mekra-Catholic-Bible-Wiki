# Why Do Catholics Believe in the Pope's Primacy?

## The Question

Why does the Catholic Church claim the Pope — the Bishop of Rome — has universal primacy and infallible teaching authority? Isn't Christ the only head of the Church? Didn't Peter make mistakes, even being rebuked by Paul (Gal 2:11-14)? Why should one bishop rule over all others?

## The Catholic Response

Catholic doctrine on the papacy rests on Christ's own institution. In Matthew 16:18-19, Jesus changes Simon's name to *Peter* (Rock) and declares: "On this rock I will build my Church." He gives Peter alone the "keys of the kingdom" — drawn from Isaiah 22, where the steward of the Davidic kingdom holds royal authority in the king's absence. Peter is established as Christ's prime minister, the visible head of the Church on earth.

After the Resurrection, Christ three times commissions Peter to "feed my sheep" (Jn 21:15-17), undoing Peter's threefold denial and establishing him as chief shepherd. The papacy is not a replacement for Christ — He remains the invisible Head — but a visible sign of unity. The Pope's infallibility (Vatican I, 1870) is not personal sinlessness. It is a *negative* protection: when the Pope speaks *ex cathedra* on faith and morals, the Holy Spirit prevents him from teaching error, guaranteeing the Church will never bind the faithful to heresy. The Church of Rome has been the ultimate court of appeal since the first century (1 Clement, St. Irenaeus, St. Cyprian).

## Scriptural Foundation

> እኔም እልሃለሁ፤ አንተ ጴጥሮስ ነህ፤ በዚህም ዓለት ላይ ቤተ ክርስቲያኔን እሠራለሁ፤ የገሃነም ደጆችም አያሸንፏትም። የመንግሥተ ሰማያትን መክፈቻዎች እሰጥሃለሁ፤ በምድር የምታስረው በሰማያት የታሰረ ይሆናል፤ በምድር የምትፈታው በሰማያት የተፈታ ይሆናል። (ማቴዎስ 16፡18-19)

Jesus gives Peter a new name and a unique office. "Keys" signify royal stewardship — governing the household in the king's name (cf. Isa 22:20-22). The binding/loosing authority is singularly given.

> ... ኢየሱስ ስምዖን ጴጥሮስን "የዮና ልጅ ስምዖን ሆይ! ከእነዚህ ይልቅ ትወደኛለህን?" አለው። ... ኢየሱስም "ግልገሎቼን አሰማራ፤" አለው። ... "ጠቦቶቼን ጠብቅ፤" ... "በጎቼን አሰማራ።" (ዮሐንስ 21፡15-17)

Three times Christ entrusts the entire flock — lambs and sheep, laity and pastors — to Peter alone. This universal pastoral commission is personal and singular.

## In the Compendium
