# Why Purgatory?

**Type:** apologetics
**Question:** Where is purgatory in the Bible? Isn't it a man-made doctrine?
**Compendium Q:** 210–211
**CCC:** 1030–1032
**Sources:** 5
**Last updated:** 2026-05-09
**Related:** [[teaching/forgiveness-resurrection-eternal-life]], [[concepts/መንጻት]], [[concepts/ምሕረት]], [[apologetics/praying-to-saints]]

## The challenge

> *"Purgatory isn't in the Bible. It's a Catholic invention to keep people afraid (and paying)."*

Purgatory **is** in the Bible — but the word itself is not, just as the words "Trinity" and "Bible" are not. The doctrine is biblical; the term comes from the **Latin *purgatorium*** ("place of purification").

## The biblical case

### 1. The clearest Old Testament verse — 2 Maccabees 12:46

> «ይህ ቅዱስና ጤናማ ሐሳብ ስለሆነ — ለሙታን ኃጢአት እንዲሰረይላቸው ጸሎት ማቅረብ መሥዋዕት ማቅረቡን ይገፋፋ ነበር።»
> — 2ኛ መቃብያን 12:46

This single verse establishes:

1. **There is a state of being where the dead are not yet finally judged** — they can still benefit from prayer.
2. **Praying for the dead is "holy and wholesome"** — pleasing to God.
3. **Sin can be forgiven after death.**

If the dead are simply in heaven (no need of prayer) or in hell (no possibility of help), prayer for them makes no sense. Yet Scripture commends it. Therefore **a third state exists**.

This is precisely why Luther wanted 2 Maccabees out of his Bible — and why the deuterocanon matters (see [[apologetics/deuterocanon]]).

### 2. Christ on forgiveness "in the age to come"

> «በመንፈስ ቅዱስ ላይ የሚሳደብ ቢሆን — በዚህም ዓለም ቢሆን — **በሚመጣው ዓለምም** — ይቅር አይባልም።»
> — ማቴዎስ 12:32

Christ implies that **some sins can be forgiven in the age to come** (otherwise the negative formulation would be meaningless). This is forgiveness *after* death — which is exactly what purgatory is.

St. Augustine, St. Gregory the Great, and other Fathers all recognized this implication.

### 3. Paul on testing by fire

> «የእያንዳንዱ ሰው ሥራ — ምን እንደ ሆነ — እሳት ይፈትነዋል። …
> ሥራው የሚቃጠል ቢሆን — እርሱ ይሸነፋል — እርሱ ራሱ ግን — በእሳት እንደሚድን ሰው — ይድናል።»
> — 1ኛ ቆሮንቶስ 3:13–15

Paul describes a state where:
- The person is **saved** (going to heaven, not hell).
- But they are **saved through fire** — purification.
- Their wrong works are **burned away**.

This is exactly purgatory — final purification of the saved.

### 4. Nothing unclean enters heaven

> «ምንም ርኩስ ነገር — ወደ እርስዋ አይገባም።»
> — ራዕይ 21:27

> «ቅድስናን ተከታተሉ — ያለ እርሱ ጌታን ሊያይ የሚችል የለምና።»
> — ዕብራውያን 12:14

Heaven admits **only the perfectly holy**. Yet most of us die imperfectly purified — venial sins forgiven but not yet perfectly purged from selfish attachments. Either:

- **God magically perfects us at the moment of death** (an arbitrary intervention not indicated in Scripture), or
- **God mercifully completes our purification** in a state between death and the full vision of heaven — purgatory.

The doctrine of purgatory is what makes sense of "nothing unclean enters heaven" without requiring that imperfect Christians go to hell.

### 5. Christ as the Just Judge

> «የመጨረሻ ሳንቲም እስካልከፈልክ ድረስ — ከዚያ አትወጣም።»
> — ማቴዎስ 5:26

Christ speaks of a debt **fully paid down**. Not eternal damnation, not free admission — **full satisfaction**. This pattern fits a temporary state of expiation perfectly.

## The historical case

The doctrine is found in early Christianity from the start:

- **Inscriptions in Roman catacombs** (1st–3rd centuries) ask passersby to **pray for the souls** of the deceased Christians buried there.
- **Tertullian** (~AD 200): records the Christian custom of celebrating Mass on the **anniversary of death** for the deceased.
- **St. Augustine** (~AD 400) prays for his mother **Monica** after her death (*Confessions* IX.13). He explicitly defends the practice.
- **St. John Chrysostom** (~AD 400): *"Let us help and commemorate them. If Job's sons were purified by their father's sacrifice, why would we doubt that our offerings for the dead bring them some consolation?"*
- **The Liturgy of St. James** (4th century, used in Ethiopia) — explicit prayers for the faithful departed.

For **1,500 years** every Christian Church prayed for the dead and believed in their purification. The Reformation broke that custom — but the Catholic, Orthodox, and Ethiopian Orthodox Churches have continued it unbroken to this day.

## What purgatory is **not**

Several Protestant caricatures should be set aside:

- ❌ **A "second chance" for the damned.** No — purgatory is for those who **die in friendship with God** but are still imperfect. The damned never reach purgatory.
- ❌ **A way to "earn" heaven.** No — heaven is given by Christ's grace; purgatory completes a process already won by him.
- ❌ **A medieval Catholic invention.** The roots are in 2 Maccabees, 1 Corinthians 3, the early Fathers, and the universal practice of the early Church.
- ❌ **A "place" with geography.** The Council of Trent deliberately did not define purgatory's *nature* — only that there is a **purifying state** between death and final glory.
- ❌ **About paying with money.** The 16th-century **abuse** of selling indulgences was real and was condemned by the Catholic Church itself at Trent. The doctrine of purgatory is older than and independent of that abuse.

## What purgatory is

Purgatory is the **final purification** of those who die in God's grace but with imperfections still in their souls. It is:

- **A grace** — not a punishment.
- **An act of mercy** — God making us fit for heaven.
- **Temporary** — terminating at the General Resurrection.
- **Helped by the prayers, Masses, and sacrifices of the living.**

## How we help the dead

The Catholic and Ethiopian Catholic tradition continues to:

- **Offer Masses** for the dead — particularly on the **3rd, 7th, 30th day** and the **anniversary**.
- **Pray for the faithful departed** — in the Litany, the Rosary, and personal prayer.
- **Give alms in their names**.
- **Gain indulgences** for them — applying the merits of Christ and the saints to assist their purification.

This is why every Catholic family has a discipline of **remembering the dead**. They are not gone; they are still part of the **communion of saints** (see [[concepts/ቅዱሳን-ሱታፌ]]).

## A pastoral note

Many Protestants who lose loved ones quietly find themselves wanting to pray for them — a sense planted deep in the human heart. Catholic doctrine names what they already feel: **love does not end at death.** The bond of charity reaches across the grave.

> «የሞቱ ምእመናን ነፍሶች — በእግዚአብሔር ምሕረት — በሰላም ያድሩ።»
> *"May the souls of the faithful departed, through the mercy of God, rest in peace."*
