# Why 73 books? — The Deuterocanon

**Type:** apologetics
**Question:** Why does the Catholic Bible have 73 books while the Protestant Bible has 66?
**Compendium Q:** 18–22
**CCC:** 120
**Sources:** 5
**Last updated:** 2026-05-09
**Related:** [[teaching/faith-and-revelation]], [[apologetics/sola-scriptura]], [[bible/Tobit]], [[bible/Judith]], [[bible/Wisdom]], [[bible/Sirach]], [[bible/Baruch]], [[bible/1Maccabees]], [[bible/2Maccabees]]

## The challenge

> *"The Catholic Bible has extra books that aren't really Scripture. The Protestant Bible has the original 66 books."*

This is one of the most common Protestant claims. It is also historically backwards.

## What are the disputed books?

Seven Old Testament books, plus additions to Esther and Daniel, are accepted by Catholics but rejected by Protestants:

1. **ጦቢት** (Tobit)
2. **ዩዲት** (Judith)
3. **ጥበበ ሰሎሞን** (Wisdom)
4. **ሲራክ / ኢያሱ ወልደ ሲራክ** (Sirach / Ecclesiasticus)
5. **ባሮክ** (Baruch)
6. **1ኛ መቃብያን** (1 Maccabees)
7. **2ኛ መቃብያን** (2 Maccabees)
8. Additions to **አስቴር** (Esther)
9. Additions to **ዳንኤል** (Daniel) — the Prayer of Azariah, Susanna, Bel and the Dragon

Catholics call these **deuterocanonical** ("second canon") — accepted into the canon later than the consensus on the protocanonical books, but no less inspired. Protestants call them **apocrypha** ("hidden") — rejected.

## The historical record

### 1. The Septuagint (LXX) — the Bible of the Apostles

By the time of Christ, **the Old Testament most widely read by Greek-speaking Jews was the Septuagint**, a Greek translation made in Alexandria from the 3rd century BC onward. The Septuagint **included the deuterocanonical books**.

When the New Testament writers quote the Old Testament — over **300 times** — they overwhelmingly quote the Septuagint, not a Hebrew text. This was the Bible they read.

### 2. The New Testament knows the deuterocanon

While the New Testament doesn't formally cite the deuterocanon as "Scripture says…" the way it cites Isaiah or the Psalms, it **alludes to and echoes the deuterocanon repeatedly**:

- **Hebrews 11:35** — "Women received their dead by resurrection. Some were tortured, refusing to accept release, that they might rise again to a better life." This is a direct reference to **2 Maccabees 7** — the only place in the OT where this happens.
- **Matthew 6:14** echoes **Sirach 28:2**.
- **James 1** parallels **Wisdom 2** and **Sirach 15** repeatedly.
- **Revelation's** imagery of angels presenting prayers (Rev 8:3–4) echoes **Tobit 12:12, 15**.

### 3. The Council of Rome (382), Hippo (393), Carthage (397, 419)

These early councils, attended by hundreds of bishops, formally listed the canon of Scripture. **All included the deuterocanonical books.** This canon was confirmed by:

- **Pope St. Damasus** (382) — commissioned St. Jerome to translate it (the Vulgate).
- **The Council of Florence** (1442).
- **The Council of Trent** (1546) — definitive Catholic teaching.

For **over 1,500 years**, Christians universally accepted the 73-book canon.

### 4. The Reformation removal

In the 16th century, **Martin Luther** removed the deuterocanonical books from his German translation, placing them in an appendix. He did this for several reasons:

- Some passages explicitly support Catholic doctrines he was rejecting (e.g., **2 Maccabees 12:46** on prayer for the dead, supporting purgatory).
- He preferred the **shorter Hebrew canon** that had been adopted by **Jewish rabbis at the Council of Jamnia (~AD 90)** — *after* Christianity had already split from Judaism.
- He privately admitted: *"This is the only book I would expel from my Bible."*

Subsequent Protestant Bibles dropped the appendix entirely. Today most Protestant editions of Scripture omit them.

## The Jamnia argument — and why it fails

Protestants who study the issue often appeal to a Jewish council at Jamnia (~AD 90) that fixed the shorter Hebrew canon.

The problem:

1. **The Christian canon was already fixed before Jamnia** — the apostles and the early Church were using the Septuagint.
2. **Jamnia was a Jewish council, not a Christian one** — and rabbinic Judaism had reasons to reject the deuterocanon precisely because Christians used it.
3. **Christians follow the apostolic tradition, not post-Christian rabbinic decisions.**

## A specific case: 2 Maccabees 12:46

> «ለሙታን ኃጢአት እንዲሰረይላቸው ጸሎት ማቅረብ ቅዱስና ጤናማ ሐሳብ ነው።»
> *"It is therefore a holy and wholesome thought to pray for the dead, that they may be loosed from their sins."*

This single verse is the reason many Protestants needed to remove 2 Maccabees: it directly supports the Catholic doctrine of **prayer for the dead** and, indirectly, **purgatory**. Removing the verse does not change the truth — it only hides the witness.

## What about Jerome's hesitation?

St. Jerome (translator of the Vulgate, late 4th century) initially expressed reservations about the deuterocanon, preferring the shorter Hebrew canon. Protestants often cite this. But:

- **Jerome submitted to the Church's judgment.** He included the deuterocanon in the Vulgate.
- **He cited the deuterocanon as Scripture** in his later writings.
- **The Church's authority — not Jerome's private opinion — fixes the canon.**

## In summary

| Claim | Reality |
|---|---|
| "Protestants use the original Bible" | The original Christian Bible (Septuagint + NT) is **closer to the Catholic** canon. |
| "Catholics added books at Trent" | Trent **defined dogmatically** what had been received from the apostles for **1,500 years**. |
| "Jews don't accept these books" | Modern rabbinic Judaism doesn't — but Hellenistic (Greek-speaking) Jews of Christ's time **did**, and Christ's apostles inherited that canon. |
| "These books contradict Scripture" | They don't. They confirm and extend it — including doctrines that the Reformers wished to drop. |

## A concluding note

The Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church accepts an **even broader canon** (81 books) — including, e.g., the **Book of Enoch**, quoted in the New Testament (Jude 14–15), and **Jubilees**. The Ethiopian tradition is therefore *more* expansive than the Catholic canon. Ethiopian Catholics use the standard 73-book Catholic canon.

The narrow 66-book canon is an anomaly in the history of the Church — a 16th-century innovation, not the apostolic deposit.
