## What they are

The **fourteen works of mercy** are the concrete shapes love takes when it meets human need. They are drawn directly from Christ's own teaching — most notably from the **Last Judgment scene** (ማቴ. 25:31–46), where the King separates the saved from the lost on the basis of *what they did for the least*.

There are **seven corporal** works (for bodily need) and **seven spiritual** works (for the need of the soul). Together they form the practical curriculum of Christian love.

## The Seven Corporal Works (ሥጋዊ የምሕረት ሥራዎች)

| # | Work | Amharic | Christ's words / Scripture |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Feed the hungry** | ለተራቡ ምግብ ስጥ | "I was hungry and you gave me food" (ማቴ. 25:35) |
| 2 | **Give drink to the thirsty** | ለተጠሙ ውሃ ስጥ | "I was thirsty and you gave me drink" (ማቴ. 25:35) |
| 3 | **Clothe the naked** | ራቁቱን አልብስ | "I was naked and you clothed me" (ማቴ. 25:36) |
| 4 | **Shelter the homeless** | መጠጊያ ለሌለው አስጠጋ | "I was a stranger and you welcomed me" (ማቴ. 25:35) |
| 5 | **Visit the sick** | ሕሙማንን ጠይቅ | "I was sick and you visited me" (ማቴ. 25:36) |
| 6 | **Visit the imprisoned** | የታሰሩትን ጠይቅ | "I was in prison and you came to me" (ማቴ. 25:36) |
| 7 | **Bury the dead** | ሙታንን ቅበር | The work of Tobit (ጦቢት 1:17), of Joseph of Arimathea (ማቴ. 27:57–60) |

## Why two kinds?

A human being is **body and soul**. Love that meets only bodily need is incomplete; love that meets only spiritual need is sentimental. The Catholic tradition holds both together. Saint Vincent de Paul fed the poor *and* taught them the faith. Mother Teresa nursed the dying *and* prepared them to meet God.

> «ሁሉን ለሁሉ ሆንኩ — ቢሆን አንዳንዶቹ ላድን።»
> — 1ኛ ቆሮ. 9:22

## Christ's identification

The most striking feature of Matthew 25 is that Christ does not say *"do this in my name"* or *"as if you were doing it to me."* He says:

> «እነዚህ የእግዚአብሔር ትዕዛዛት — ለመጨረሻዎቹ ስለ ፈጸማችሁ — ለእኔ ፈጸማችሁ።»

He **identifies himself** with the hungry, the imprisoned, the foreigner. This is not metaphor. The poor person at your door **is Christ**. The dying man you visit **is Christ**. The student you teach **is Christ**.

## The least vs the spectacular

Beware the temptation to do the *spectacular* work of mercy while ignoring the *small one at hand*. The poor in Calcutta are not your responsibility if you have a hungry neighbor. Mother Teresa's first rule: *"Do small things with great love."*

> «በውጭ የሚያስፈልጋቸውን ትኩረት ሰጥቶ — የራሱን ቤተሰብ የተወው — እምነት የለውም፤ ከካዳጅ የባሰ ነው።»
> — 1ኛ ጢሞ. 5:8

## A weekly rule

A simple rule a catechist can teach:

**Once a week, do at least one corporal work and one spiritual work.**

- **Corporal:** visit one sick person, bring food to one hungry family, sit with one elderly neighbor.
- **Spiritual:** pray a Rosary for the dead, write a letter to a discouraged friend, teach one child the Our Father, forgive one person you've held a grudge against.

A year of this is fifty-two acts of mercy. A decade is five hundred. A life is thousands. This is how saints are made.

## The Works of Mercy and the Last Judgment

The Catholic tradition takes Matthew 25 with full seriousness: **at the Last Judgment, we will be examined on these.** Not on our possessions, our successes, our prayers in private, our reputation. On these.

> «ጌታ ሆይ — መቼ ራቁትህን አይተን አለበስንህ?»
> *"Lord, when did we see you naked and clothe you?"*

The question is asked by both the saved and the lost. The saved did not realize they were serving Christ. The lost did not realize they were rejecting him. Mercy is not always done with awareness. **It is always done with consequences.**
