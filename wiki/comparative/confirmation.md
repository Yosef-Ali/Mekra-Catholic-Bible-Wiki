## Catholic teaching
Confirmation perfects baptismal grace, conferring the fullness of the Holy Spirit and an indelible spiritual seal (character). It strengthens the baptized for public witness and mission. In the Latin Rite, it is normally administered by a bishop to those who have reached the age of reason (CCC 1285–1321). The essential rite is anointing with chrism with the words "Be sealed with the gift of the Holy Spirit." Eastern Catholic churches administer it together with baptism and Eucharist (Chrismation). See [[teaching/confirmation]] (Q265–270).

## Eastern Orthodox position
The Orthodox administer Chrismation immediately after baptism by a priest (not only a bishop), using chrism consecrated by the bishop. This preserves the ancient unity of the three initiation sacraments. The theology is substantially identical to the Catholic understanding — the gift of the Holy Spirit, sealing, strengthening.

## Protestant positions
Most Protestant churches reject confirmation as a sacrament entirely. Some (Anglican, Lutheran, Methodist) retain a rite of confirmation as a public profession of faith and admission to communion, but without sacramental character or the conferral of an indelible seal. Evangelical churches practice "believer's baptism" and see no need for a post-baptismal rite.

## Where the differences come from
The separation of confirmation from baptism developed in the Latin West as bishops delegated baptism to priests while reserving chrismation to themselves. The Reformation rejected confirmation as a sacrament because it lacked explicit dominical institution. The EOTC preserves the ancient Eastern practice of unified initiation.
