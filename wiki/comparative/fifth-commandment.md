## Catholic teaching
The fifth commandment, "You shall not kill," requires respect for the sacredness of human life from conception to natural death (CCC 2258–2330). The Church upholds the right to legitimate self-defense while condemning abortion, euthanasia, and suicide as gravely contrary to the moral law. Just war doctrine permits defensive war under strict conditions. Capital punishment is admissible only if "this is the only possible way of effectively defending human lives against the unjust aggressor" (CCC 2267, revised by Pope Francis, 2018). See [[teaching/fifth-commandment]] (Q510–518).

## Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo (EOTC) position

## Eastern Orthodox position
The Orthodox share the same fundamental commitment to the sanctity of life. Abortion is considered murder. On war, Orthodox tradition is more reluctant to develop just-war theory — emphasizing instead the tragic nature of all killing, even in war, and sometimes requiring returning soldiers to abstain from communion for a period.

## Protestant positions
Evangelical Protestants are strongly pro-life, with abortion being a central political and moral concern. Mainline Protestants vary. On capital punishment, American evangelicals are more supportive than the Catholic position. Just war theory originated in Augustine but is now debated — some Protestants hold to pacifism (Mennonite, Quaker).

## Where the differences come from
The Catholic death penalty revision (2018) represents a development in doctrine. The EOTC's historic alliance with the Ethiopian state affected its stance on capital punishment. Orthodox penitential canons reflect the early Church's stricter discipline around killing.
