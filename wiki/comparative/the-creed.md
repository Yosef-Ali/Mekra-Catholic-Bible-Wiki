## Catholic teaching
The Catholic Church professes the Nicene-Constantinopolitan Creed (with the Filioque:
"who proceeds from the Father and the Son") at Sunday Mass and solemnities (CCC 184–197).
The Apostles' Creed, an ancient baptismal symbol of the Roman Church, is also authorized
particularly during Lent and Easter. The Creed summarizes the essential articles of faith
which the Magisterium guards and interprets. Vatican II (Dei Verbum) affirmed that the
Creed expresses the faith the Church has received from the Apostles.

## Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo (EOTC) position
የኢትዮጵያ ኦርቶዶክስ ተዋሕዶ ቤተክርስቲያን የኒቂያ-ቁስጥንጢንያ ሃይማኖተ አበው (Nicene Creed) ያለ ፊልዮክ
(Filioque) ትጠቀማለች — "መንፈስ ቅዱስ ከአብ የሚሰረጽ" ብቻ እንጂ "ከወልድም" አትልም።

የኢትዮጵያ ምእመናን በየቀኑ የሚያነቡት ጸሎተ ሃይማኖት (Prayer of Faith) የተባለ አጭር የእምነት መግለጫ
አለ። ይህ ለኢትዮጵያ ልዩ የሆነ ልማድ ነው። በተጨማሪም "የአበው ሃይማኖት" (Faith of the Fathers) የሚባሉ
ጥንታዊ የእምነት መግለጫዎች አሏት — እነዚህም ሐዋርያዊ ወግንና የሊቃውንትን ትምህርት ያካተቱ ናቸው።

## Eastern Orthodox position
Identical to EOTC on the Creed: recited without Filioque. The Orthodox view the Apostles'
Creed as a Western local baptismal symbol rather than an ecumenical standard. The Creed
is the central confession at every Divine Liturgy.

## Protestant positions
Most mainline Protestant churches accept both the Apostles' and Nicene Creeds as norms
of faith. Evangelical and free-church traditions sometimes reject creedal statements as
"man-made" ("no creed but the Bible"), though they typically affirm the doctrinal content
implicitly. Some Baptist and Pentecostal groups formally reject creedal subscription.

## Where the differences come from
The Filioque addition was unilateral — the Latin West added it without an ecumenical
council. The East viewed this as a violation of the conciliar principle that binds the
Church. Catholic-Orthodox dialogue has since acknowledged that the original Greek text
without Filioque is the normative ecumenical text. The Ethiopian ጸሎተ ሃይማኖት appears
to be an independent Ethiopian formulation with no direct Western equivalent.
