# The Lord's Prayer: Catholic vs EOTC
**Type:** comparative | **Pairs with:** [[teaching/the-lords-prayer]] | **Compendium Q:** 578-598
**Sources:** 3 | **Last updated:** 2026-05-06 | **Related:** [[concepts/ጸሎት]]

## Catholic teaching
The Lord's Prayer ("Our Father") is the summary of the whole Gospel and the most perfect
of prayers (CCC 2759-2865). It was taught by Christ Himself (Matthew 6:9-13; Luke
11:2-4). The Catholic Mass includes the Our Father after the Eucharistic Prayer, recited
or sung by the entire assembly. In the Latin Rite, the doxology ("For the kingdom, the
power, and the glory are yours...") is not part of the Our Father text but is added as
an embolism after the prayer.

## Eastern Orthodox position
The Orthodox include the full doxology with the Our Father in liturgical use, as the
EOTC does. The prayer is always recited by the entire congregation, never by the priest
alone, following the ancient practice that this is the prayer of the baptized faithful.

## Protestant positions
Protestants universally use the Lord's Prayer with the doxology included. The King
James Version's inclusion of the doxology in Matthew 6:13 (based on the Textus Receptus)
reinforced this practice. Liberal Protestants may use it as a model rather than a
prescribed formula.

## Where the differences come from
The Latin Vulgate and earliest Greek manuscripts do not include the doxology in Matthew
6:13 -- it appears to be a liturgical addition that entered the manuscript tradition of
the Byzantine text-type. The Catholic liturgical practice preserves the ancient Western
pattern (prayer followed by embolism), while the Ethiopian and Eastern traditions
integrated the doxology directly into the prayer text.
