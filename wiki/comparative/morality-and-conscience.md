# Morality and Conscience: Catholic vs EOTC

**Sources:** 3 | **Last updated:** 2026-05-06
**Related:** [[concepts/ኅሊና]], [[concepts/ነፃነት]]

## Catholic teaching
Conscience is "man's most secret core and his sanctuary" where he is alone with God
(Gaudium et Spes 16; CCC 1776-1802). The Catholic Church teaches that conscience must
be formed according to objective moral truth and that a person must follow the certain
judgment of conscience even when erroneous (if not culpably so). The dignity of
conscience does not mean autonomy from moral truth.

## Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo (EOTC) position
የኢትዮጵያ ኦርቶዶክስ ተዋሕዶ ቤተክርስቲያን ኅሊናን (conscience) በእግዚአብሔር የተሰጠ ውስጣዊ ምስክር (inner
witness) አድርጋ ትመለከተዋለች። ሰው በኅሊናው አማካይነት መልካሙንና ክፉውን ይለያል -- ይህ ከካቶሊክ
አስተምህሮ ጋር የሚስማማ ነው።

በኢትዮጵያ ወግ ኅሊና የማኅበረሰቡ አካል እንጂ ራሱን የቻለ አይደለም -- የሰው ኅሊና የሚመራው በቤተክርስቲያኒቱ
ትምህርትና በማኅበረሰቡ ሥነ ምግባር ነው። ይህ ከምዕራቡ የግለሰባዊ ኅሊና አጽንዖት (emphasis on individual
conscience) በተወሰነ መልኩ የተለየ ነው።

## Eastern Orthodox position
The Orthodox similarly teach conscience as an inner faculty. The discernment of logismoi
(thoughts) is central to Orthodox spiritual practice -- learning to identify which
thoughts come from God, which from the self, and which from demons.

## Protestant positions
Protestants emphasize individual conscience strongly (Luther at Worms: "My conscience
is captive to the Word of God"). This has led to both robust moral conviction and
fragmentation. Evangelical traditions often frame morality as biblical obedience rather
than conscience formation.

## Where the differences come from
The Western emphasis on individual conscience arose from medieval casuistry and the
Reformation's rejection of ecclesial mediation. The EOTC's communal framing of conscience
reflects patristic anthropology where the person is constituted in relationship.
