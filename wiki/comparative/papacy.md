## Catholic Teaching

The Catholic Church teaches that the Pope, the Bishop of Rome, holds universal primacy over the entire Church by divine institution. Christ established Peter as the rock on which He would build His Church, giving him alone the "keys of the kingdom of heaven" with the authority to bind and loose (Matt 16:18-19). After the Resurrection, Christ three times entrusted the entire flock — "feed my lambs... tend my sheep" (John 21:15-17) — to Peter alone. The Pope is Peter's successor, the perpetual and visible source of unity for all bishops and faithful (Compendium Q182).

The Pope's authority is supreme, full, immediate, and universal — he has direct jurisdiction over every diocese, every bishop, and every faithful Catholic worldwide, not merely a primacy of honor. When the Pope speaks *ex cathedra* (from the chair of Peter) to define a doctrine on faith or morals, he possesses infallibility — not by his own power, but by the protection of the Holy Spirit that prevents the Church from binding the faithful to error (Vatican I, 1870; CCC 891). This infallibility is a negative guarantee, not personal sinlessness or divine inspiration. The Pope exercises this authority in communion with the College of Bishops, who together with him form the Magisterium. Historically, the Church of Rome was already recognized as the ultimate court of appeal and the "presiding in love" by early witnesses such as St. Clement of Rome (1st century), St. Ignatius of Antioch, St. Irenaeus, and St. Cyprian.

## EOTC Teaching

The Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church is autocephalous (self-governing) and does not recognize the Pope's universal jurisdiction or infallibility. The EOTC traces its apostolic lineage through St. Frumentius (አባ ሰላማ ከሳቴ ብርሃን), who was consecrated as the first bishop of Aksum by St. Athanasius, Patriarch of Alexandria, in the 4th century. For most of its history, the Ethiopian Church received its metropolitan (Abun) from the Coptic Patriarchate of Alexandria — an Egyptian bishop appointed to lead the Ethiopian Church. This created a deep historical bond with the See of Alexandria, not Rome.

Only in 1959 did the Ethiopian Church gain full autocephaly with its own Patriarch (Patriarch of Ethiopia), though it remains in full communion with the Coptic Orthodox Church of Alexandria and the wider Oriental Orthodox family. The EOTC recognizes the Pope of Rome as a bishop with an honorific primacy — a "first among equals" — not a jurisdictional superior. It holds that all bishops are equal as successors of the apostles, and that Peter's "rock" in Matthew 16:18 refers to his confession of faith ("You are the Christ, the Son of the living God"), not to Peter's person. The EOTC, like all Oriental Orthodox, rejects the dogmas of Vatican I (1870) on papal infallibility and universal jurisdiction as later innovations unknown to the undivided Church of the first millennium. The Ethiopian Church governs itself through its Holy Synod under its Patriarch, with its own canon law (Fetha Negest) and liturgical tradition.

## Key Differences

- **Papal primacy:** Catholic — supreme, full, immediate, universal jurisdiction by divine right. EOTC — honorific primacy only; all bishops are equal successors of the apostles.
- **Infallibility:** Catholic — Pope is infallible when defining doctrine *ex cathedra* on faith/morals. EOTC — no single bishop possesses personal infallibility; Church as a whole (ecumenical councils) is the guardian of truth.
- **Matthew 16:18:** Catholic — Christ builds His Church on Peter the person (the Rock). EOTC — Christ builds on Peter's confession of faith, or on Christ Himself as the Rock.
- **Patriarchal authority:** Catholic — Pope has direct jurisdiction over all dioceses. EOTC — the Ethiopian Patriarch governs Ethiopia; Alexandria holds no jurisdictional authority since 1959.
- **Historical lineage:** Catholic — Pope succeeds Peter in an unbroken line at Rome. EOTC — Ethiopian Church founded from Alexandria by St. Frumentius; Rome is not the mother church.
- **Development of doctrine:** Catholic — papacy developed organically under Holy Spirit guidance, culminating in Vatican I. EOTC — Vatican I dogmas are innovations not received by the universal Church.

## Points of Agreement

- Both affirm the apostolic succession of bishops as successors of the apostles.
- Both hold that the Church is indefectible — the gates of hell shall not prevail against it (Matt 16:18).
- Both recognize the Bishop of Rome as historically significant and entitled to some form of primacy (Catholic: jurisdictional; EOTC: honorific).
- Both use Matthew 16:18-19 and John 21:15-17 as foundational texts for understanding Church authority.
- Both have a patriarchal structure (though the scope and nature of patriarchal authority differ).
- Both seek visible unity — the Catholic Church through communion with the Pope, the EOTC through communion of autocephalous churches.

## Open questions

- Could a "primacy of honor" without jurisdiction satisfy both Catholic ecclesiology and the EOTC's autocephalous tradition — what model of unity is possible?
- How does the EOTC interpret the role of St. Peter in the early Church as evidenced by Acts (Peter leading the apostles, presiding at Pentecost, making the decisive speech at the Council of Jerusalem)?
- What would an Ethiopian Catholic Patriarchate in full communion with Rome look like under the Eastern Catholic model, and how does the existing Ethiopian Catholic Church (a *sui iuris* church) address this?
