## Catholic Teaching

Baptism (ጥምቀት) is the foundation of the entire Christian life, the gateway to life in the Spirit, and the door to the other sacraments (CCC 1213). Through baptism all sins are washed away — original sin and any personal sins — and the baptized person is incorporated into Christ's death and resurrection (Rom 6:3-4), becoming a member of the Body of Christ, the Church. Baptism confers an indelible spiritual seal or character (ማህተም) that can never be repeated; once validly baptized, a person is permanently configured to Christ and can never be rebaptized, even after grave sin or apostasy (CCC 1272).

The Catholic Church baptizes with the Trinitarian formula commanded by Christ: "in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit" (Matt 28:19). The ordinary matter is water, applied by immersion or pouring (affusion). Both infants and adults receive baptism; for infants, the faith of the parents and godparents supplies what the child cannot yet profess. In danger of death, anyone — even a non-Christian — can validly baptize with the right intention. Baptism is understood as the sacrament of faith: the beginning of the Christian journey, not its completion, which is why Confirmation and Eucharist complete Christian initiation.

## EOTC Teaching

The Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church (EOTC) holds baptism as the essential mystery (ምሥጢር) of entry into the Church. Baptism is administered by triple immersion in the name of the Holy Trinity, following the ancient practice of the undivided Church. The EOTC celebrates the Feast of Timket (ጥምቀት) — the Baptism of the Lord at Epiphany — as one of its most important annual celebrations, with elaborate processions, blessing of water, and renewal of baptismal vows that is central to Ethiopian Christian identity.

A distinctive EOTC practice is the rebaptism of those who apostatize from the faith and later return. While the Catholic Church teaches that baptism's indelible character prevents any repetition, many EOTC authorities hold that those who denied Christ (especially under duress, such as during periods of Islamic pressure in Ethiopian history) must be rebaptized upon returning, on the grounds that apostasy severs the baptismal grace entirely. The EOTC also requires strict triple immersion (not pouring) and typically baptizes infants on the 40th day (for boys) or 80th day (for girls), following Old Testament purification customs. Male circumcision is sometimes observed alongside baptism as a cultural practice, though it is not considered a requirement for salvation.

## Key Differences

- **Rebaptism:** Catholic — never, under any circumstance, due to the indelible seal. EOTC — practiced for apostates who return; grace is considered lost through apostasy.
- **Mode:** Catholic — immersion or pouring both valid. EOTC — triple immersion required; pouring is not accepted as sufficient.
- **Timing for infants:** Catholic — encouraged soon after birth without fixed day. EOTC — 40 days (boys) / 80 days (girls), tied to purification rites.
- **Minister:** Catholic — any person (even unbaptized) can baptize validly in emergencies. EOTC — only an ordained priest may baptize, even in emergencies.
- **Circumcision:** Catholic — not required, superseded by baptism (Col 2:11-12). EOTC — often practiced culturally alongside baptism, with some theological significance.
- **Christmation (Confirmation):** Catholic — separated from baptism for infants, conferred later by the bishop. EOTC — conferred immediately after baptism (as in all Oriental Orthodox) by the priest using myron (ሜሮን) blessed by the Patriarch.

## Points of Agreement

- Both affirm baptism as necessary for salvation (John 3:5).
- Both use the Trinitarian formula ("in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit").
- Both baptize infants and recognize the role of sponsors/godparents.
- Both teach the remission of original sin through baptism.
- Both link baptism to Christ's death and resurrection.
- Both recognize each other's baptism as valid (the Catholic Church explicitly recognizes EOTC baptism; EOTC generally recognizes Catholic baptism, though some require conditional baptism in practice).

## Open questions

- How did the rebaptism-of-apostates practice develop in Ethiopian history, and which EOTC councils or texts authorize it?
- Does the EOTC's chrismation-at-baptism practice affect how it views the Catholic separation of Baptism and Confirmation?
- Can the EOTC's *de facto* recognition of Catholic baptism (without rebaptism) be reconciled with its theological principle that apostasy severs baptismal grace?
