## Catholic teaching
The first three commandments concern humanity's direct relationship with God: exclusive
worship (1st), reverence for God's name (2nd), and sanctification of the Lord's Day
(3rd). The Catholic Church interprets the prohibition of graven images (Exodus 20:4)
as forbidding idolatry, not sacred art -- Nicaea II (787) affirmed the veneration of
icons, distinguishing latria (worship due to God alone) from proskynesis (veneration).
Sunday replaces the Sabbath as the day of Resurrection.

## Eastern Orthodox position
Substantially identical on icons: Nicaea II is received by both Eastern Orthodox and
Catholic churches. The Orthodox distinguish between worship (latreia) and veneration
(proskynesis). The Tabot tradition is uniquely Ethiopian, with no Eastern parallel.

## Protestant positions
Protestants divide sharply. Lutherans permit art but reject veneration. Reformed
Calvinists, following a strict reading of Exodus 20:4, historically practiced
iconoclasm -- removing statues, paintings, and stained glass. Evangelicals often avoid
religious imagery in worship (bare cross rather than crucifix). Seventh-day Adventists
observe Saturday Sabbath rather than Sunday.

## Where the differences come from
The icon controversy (8th-9th c.) was resolved at Nicaea II (787), which the EOTC
received. The Reformation's rejection of images stemmed from concern about superstition
and strict biblical interpretation. The Tabot tradition has pre-Christian roots baptized
into Ethiopian liturgy -- possibly the world's most distinctive Christian practice.
