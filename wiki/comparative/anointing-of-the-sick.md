## Catholic teaching
The Anointing of the Sick is one of the seven sacraments, instituted by Christ and attested in James 5:14-15. It confers a special grace of the Holy Spirit: union with Christ's passion, ecclesial strengthening, forgiveness of sins, and, if God wills, physical healing (CCC 1511-1516). Unlike Last Rites (which includes anointing, confession, and Viaticum), the Anointing is for all gravely ill, not only the dying. It may be repeated if the sick person recovers and falls ill again. Only a priest administers it using blessed oil. See [[teaching/anointing-of-the-sick]] (Q313-320).

## Eastern Orthodox position
Eastern Orthodox practice closely mirrors the Catholic understanding but emphasizes the communal dimension more strongly. The service involves seven priests ideally, olive oil blessed by the bishop, and prayers for both spiritual healing and bodily recovery. The Orthodox share the Catholic conviction that this is a sacrament of healing, not only of the dying, and it may be repeated.

## Protestant positions
Most Protestant churches do not recognize the Anointing as a sacrament. Some (Lutheran, Anglican) retain it as a rite based on James 5 but without sacramental efficacy. Evangelical and Pentecostal traditions practice "anointing with oil" as a prayer for healing, often accompanied by laying on of hands, but reject any notion of the rite conferring grace ex opere operato or being restricted to priests.

## Where the differences come from
The divergence traces to the Reformation's reduction of sacraments to two (Baptism, Eucharist) based on explicit dominical institution. James 5:14-15 was deemed insufficient to establish a sacrament, though the Council of Trent (Session XIV) defined the Anointing as one of the seven instituted by Christ and promulgated by James. The EOTC retains the sacrament but developed its own pastoral discipline not codified by Trent.
