## Catholic Teaching

The Eucharist (ቅዱስ ቁርባን) is the "source and summit of the Christian life" (CCC 1324). The Catholic Church teaches that at the consecration during Mass, by the power of Christ's words and the Holy Spirit, a complete change of substance occurs — transubstantiation (መለወጥ). The entire substance of bread becomes the Body of Christ, and the entire substance of wine becomes His Blood, while the outward appearances (species) of bread and wine remain unchanged (CCC 1376). Christ is then truly, really, and substantially present — Body, Blood, soul, and divinity — under each Eucharistic species (Compendium Q282-283).

The Eucharist is not merely a memorial meal but the re-presentation (not repetition) of Christ's one eternal sacrifice on Calvary. The Mass makes sacramentally present the same sacrifice, with Christ as both priest and victim. The Catholic Church requires the faithful to attend Mass on Sundays and holy days of obligation, and to receive communion at least once a year during the Easter season (CCC 1389). To receive, one must be in a state of grace — free from mortal sin — and observe the Eucharistic fast (one hour before communion in the Latin rite). The Roman rite uses unleavened bread (following the Passover context of the Last Supper), though Eastern Catholic rites use leavened bread. Communion may be received under one or both species; Christ is fully present in either.

## EOTC Teaching

The Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church also holds the Eucharist in the highest reverence as the true Body and Blood of Christ. Like all Oriental Orthodox churches, the EOTC affirms the real presence of Christ in the Eucharist without using the scholastic term "transubstantiation." The consecration is understood as a divine mystery (ምሥጢር) wrought by the Holy Spirit through the priest's epiclesis (invocation) — the change is real but not defined through Aristotelian categories of substance and accidents. The EOTC liturgy (ቅዳሴ) is celebrated exclusively with leavened bread and wine, typically prepared by the faithful and consecrated during the Divine Liturgy.

EOTC Eucharistic discipline is notably strict. The faithful must fast completely from food and water from midnight before receiving (a more rigorous fast than the Catholic one-hour requirement). Communion must be received under both species; the priest administers the consecrated Bread dipped in the consecrated Wine (intinction) directly into the mouth of each communicant. Only those in good standing who have recently confessed and are not under penance may approach. Married couples observe periods of marital abstinence before communion. Unlike the Catholic practice of daily Mass, EOTC parishes typically celebrate the Divine Liturgy (ቅዳሴ) only on Sundays, major feasts, and fasting-day vigils — though the liturgy itself is a lengthy, elaborate celebration lasting several hours with extensive chanting, incense, and ritual processions that closely resemble ancient temple worship.

## Key Differences

- **Nature of change:** Catholic — transubstantiation (substance/accidents philosophy). EOTC — real mystical change, but no dogmatic definition using scholastic categories.
- **Bread:** Catholic (Latin rite) — unleavened bread. EOTC — always leavened bread.
- **Eucharistic fast:** Catholic — one hour before communion. EOTC — total fast from midnight, including water.
- **Frequency:** Catholic — daily Mass available; encouraged weekly at minimum. EOTC — typically only Sundays and major feasts; lengthy liturgy limits daily celebration.
- **Reception:** Catholic — host on tongue or hand; chalice optional. EOTC — intinction only, directly into mouth by priest, both species required.
- **Minister:** Catholic — bishop or priest; deacons may distribute. EOTC — only a bishop or priest may consecrate and administer.
- **Confession before communion:** Catholic — required only if in mortal sin. EOTC — recent confession expected as a general discipline before receiving.
- **Epiclesis:** Catholic — consecration occurs at the Words of Institution. EOTC — strong emphasis on epiclesis (invocation of the Holy Spirit) as effecting the consecration, together with the Words of Institution.

## Points of Agreement

- Both confess the real, true, and substantial presence of Christ in the Eucharist.
- Both hold the Eucharist as a sacrifice — the making-present of Christ's one sacrifice on Calvary.
- Both require proper preparation and a state of grace to receive.
- Both reserve the consecrated elements for the sick and for adoration.
- Both see the Eucharist as the central act of Christian worship, the "mystery of mysteries."
- Both trace the Eucharist to Christ's institution at the Last Supper.
- The Catholic Church permits EOTC faithful to receive communion from Catholic ministers under certain conditions, recognizing the validity of EOTC sacraments (Compendium Q293).

## Open questions

- Does the EOTC's use of leavened bread carry a theological argument about the Risen Christ versus the Passover connection of unleavened bread?
