## Catholic teaching
The Council of Chalcedon (451 AD) defined that Christ is one divine Person with two natures —
divine and human — "without confusion, change, division, or separation" (CCC 464–469).
The human nature is consubstantial with us; the divine nature consubstantial with the Father.
Mary is Theotokos — Mother of God — because she bore the Person of the Word, not merely
a human nature. The Compendium Q73–97 covers the Incarnation, the two natures, the
virginal conception, and Christ's hidden life at Nazareth. The Catholic Church affirms
that the Chalcedonian formula is the normative expression of apostolic faith.

## Eastern Orthodox position
The Eastern Orthodox accept Chalcedon's "two natures" but emphasize that the hypostatic
union is a mystery beyond full rational articulation. The Filioque controversy and papal
primacy are the primary remaining barriers to reunion, not Christology — Orthodox and
Catholics have been in substantial Christological agreement since Chalcedon.

## Protestant positions
Protestant churches universally affirm Chalcedonian Christology — Christ is fully God
and fully man. Liberal Protestantism sometimes downplays the virginal conception, but
creedal Protestants (Lutheran, Reformed, Anglican) maintain the classical definitions.
The Reformation added no new Christological controversies to the ecumenical landscape.

## Where the differences come from
The EOTC-Catholic divide originates at Chalcedon (451). The linguistic and political
dimensions are as important as the theological: Cyril's Miaphysite formula ("one nature")
was interpreted differently by Greek-speaking and Syriac/Coptic-speaking churches. Both
sides confess that Christ is fully divine and fully human. The 20th-century bilateral
dialogues concluded the disagreement is substantially verbal. The EOTC's continuing
separate identity reflects 1500 years of institutional and liturgical independence.
