## Catholic teaching
The eighth commandment requires truthfulness and forbids false witness, lying, rash judgment, detraction, and calumny (CCC 2464–2513). Truth is owed to all, though not all truth must be shared with everyone — discretion and charity govern disclosure. The seal of confession is absolute and inviolable. The Compendium (Q538–543) links this commandment to the virtue of truthfulness and the duty of reparation for harm caused by false speech. See [[teaching/eighth-commandment]].

## Eastern Orthodox position
The Orthodox understanding of the eighth commandment is traditionally merged with the ninth (coveting) in their decalogue numbering. Truth-telling is a fundamental virtue. The sacrament of confession requires absolute secrecy. Monastic literature (e.g., John Climacus) treats falsehood as spiritually destructive.

## Protestant positions
Protestant ethics emphasize truth-telling but make distinctions: "necessary lies" (e.g., to protect life, as with Rahab or Corrie ten Boom) are debated. Reformed confessions stress the duty of truth in courts, contracts, and neighborly conversation. The Westminster Larger Catechism (Q143–145) expands the eighth commandment (9th in Protestant numbering) to include "preserving and promoting of truth."

## Where the differences come from
The numbering difference (Catholic/Lutheran 8th = Protestant 9th) goes back to Augustine vs Origen. The Catholic Church follows Augustine's numbering adopted at Trent. The substantive ethics of truthfulness have minimal confessional divergence.
