## Catholic teaching
Divine Revelation is God's self-communication, fully given in Jesus Christ and transmitted through Scripture and Tradition, which "flow from the same divine wellspring" (Dei Verbum 9). The Magisterium serves Revelation, not above it. Faith is a personal act — the free assent of intellect and will to God revealing — and also an ecclesial act: "I believe" is the faith of the Church (CCC 166). Scripture and Tradition form one sacred deposit of faith. See [[teaching/faith-and-revelation]] (Q1–25).

## Eastern Orthodox position
The Orthodox share the Catholic conviction that Scripture and Tradition form one deposit, but reject the Magisterium as a distinct teaching office. For the Orthodox, the Church as a whole — bishops, clergy, and laity together — is the guardian of Tradition, expressed conciliarly. The Orthodox canon is slightly narrower than the EOTC canon (no Enoch or Jubilees).

## Protestant positions
Protestantism holds to sola scriptura — Scripture alone is the supreme authority. Tradition is respected but not infallible. The canon is restricted to 66 books (39 OT / 27 NT), rejecting the deuterocanon/apocrypha. Faith is individual trust in Christ, though Reformed and Lutheran traditions also emphasize the communal, covenantal character of faith.

## Where the differences come from
The Reformation principle of sola scriptura was a direct response to perceived abuses of ecclesial authority. The EOTC canon reflects its historical isolation and use of Ge'ez literature. The Catholic position was clarified at Trent (1546) and Vatican II (Dei Verbum, 1965).
