## Catholic teaching
Holy Orders is the sacrament through which the mission entrusted by Christ to His apostles
continues in the Church until the end of time (CCC 1536). It has three degrees: bishop
(fullness of orders), priest (co-worker of the bishop), and deacon (service). In the
Latin Rite, priests and bishops are ordinarily required to be celibate — a discipline,
not a dogma. The Church holds that "the Church has no authority whatsoever to confer
priestly ordination on women" (Ordinatio Sacerdotalis, 1994). Eastern Catholic Churches
permit married men to be ordained priests, following the ancient tradition of the East.

See [[teaching/holy-orders]] (Q321–335).

## Eastern Orthodox position
Similar to EOTC: married men may be ordained priests (but not bishops — bishops must be
celibate or widowed monastics). Female priesthood is rejected. Apostolic succession
through bishops is strictly maintained. The Orthodox recognize Catholic orders as valid
in principle (unlike Protestant orders) but do not concelebrate due to schism.

## Protestant positions
Most Protestant traditions reject a sacrificial priesthood. Ordination is for ministry
of Word and sacrament, not an ontological change in the person. Many denominations
ordain women (Anglican, Lutheran, Methodist, Presbyterian). Anglicans claim apostolic
succession, but Rome judged Anglican orders "absolutely null and utterly void"
(Apostolicae Curae, 1896). Lutherans and Methodists retain modified episcopal structures.

## Where the differences come from
The celibacy requirement in the Latin Rite developed gradually (mandated universally by
the First Lateran Council, 1123, and reinforced at Trent). The EOTC and Eastern churches
preserved the earlier discipline of married priesthood rooted in 1 Timothy 3:2. The
EOTC practice of episcopal celibacy mirrors the broader Eastern tradition.
