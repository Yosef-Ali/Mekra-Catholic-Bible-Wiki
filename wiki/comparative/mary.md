## Catholic Teaching

The Catholic Church honors the Blessed Virgin Mary with the highest veneration (hyperdulia), distinct from the worship (latria) due to God alone. As defined by the Council of Ephesus (431 AD), Mary is *Theotokos* — the Mother of God (ወላዲተ አምላክ) — because she gave birth to Jesus Christ, one divine Person with two natures, truly God and truly man. This title protects Christological orthodoxy: denying Mary as Mother of God divides Christ's person.

Four Marian dogmas define Catholic teaching. First, Mary's **perpetual virginity**: she was a virgin before, during, and after the birth of Christ — she had no other children. Second, the **Immaculate Conception** (defined 1854): Mary, by a singular grace, was conceived without original sin from the first moment of her existence, by the merits of Christ her Son. Third, Mary as **Mother of God** (Theotokos). Fourth, the **Assumption** (defined 1950): at the end of her earthly life, Mary was taken up body and soul into heavenly glory; the Church leaves open whether she died before this, but affirms she did not undergo corruption. Mary is Mother of the Church — given to the beloved disciple at the cross (John 19:26-27) — and from heaven she intercedes for the faithful as Mediatrix and Advocate. All her privileges point to Christ and flow from His merits, not her own.

## EOTC Teaching

The Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church shares an extraordinarily deep devotion to the Virgin Mary that is arguably even more prominent in daily piety than in Catholic practice. The EOTC calls Mary *Waladite Amlak* (ወላዲተ አምላክ, Mother of God) and holds her as the highest of all saints, the Queen of Heaven, and the supreme intercessor. Ethiopian liturgical poetry (such as the *Weddase Maryam* — Praises of Mary) and the *Anqetsa Birhan* (Gates of Light) are dedicated to her, and every Wednesday is kept as a day of special Marian commemoration. No Ethiopian Orthodox church is complete without an icon of Mary holding the Christ child, prominently displayed.

However, the EOTC does **not** accept the Immaculate Conception as defined by the Catholic Church in 1854. In EOTC teaching, Mary was born with the same fallen human nature as all descendants of Adam. She was purified and sanctified at the Annunciation — when the Holy Spirit overshadowed her (Luke 1:35) at the moment of Christ's conception. She is sinless by grace and free consent, but not conceived free from original sin. The EOTC also does not dogmatically define the Assumption in Catholic terms. Instead, it holds to the **Dormition** (falling asleep, ዕርገት) of Mary: she died a natural death, and her body was later taken to heaven by her Son, typically on the third day after her burial (a tradition found in the *Liber Requiei* and other early texts). Whether her body experienced corruption before translation is a matter of theological opinion, not dogma. The EOTC affirms Mary's perpetual virginity wholeheartedly and explicitly rejects the "brothers of Jesus" as children of Joseph from a prior marriage.

## Key Differences

- **Immaculate Conception:** Catholic — Mary conceived without original sin from the first moment (dogma, 1854). EOTC — Mary born with ordinary human nature; purified at the Annunciation; the 1854 definition is a later innovation.
- **Assumption vs Dormition:** Catholic — Mary assumed body and soul; whether she died before is an open question. EOTC — Mary died (Dormition), was buried, and her body was taken to heaven later; this is celebrated on August 15 (Nehasse 9 in Ethiopian calendar).
- **Dogmatization:** Catholic — Immaculate Conception and Assumption are infallible dogmas binding on all faithful. EOTC — these are pious beliefs, not binding dogmas; the Church never defined them ecumenically.
- **Mode of sinlessness:** Catholic — Mary preserved from original sin from conception (passive). EOTC — Mary cleansed at the Annunciation through the Holy Spirit (active purification at a moment in time).
- **Joseph's prior marriage:** EOTC — explicitly teaches Joseph was a widower with children; this explains the "brothers" passages. Catholic — some hold the same view, but the Church has no dogmatic position; "brothers" are often understood as cousins or close relatives.
- **Liturgical expression:** EOTC — devotion to Mary so extensive that some outside observers see it as approaching worship. Catholic — hyperdulia is carefully distinguished from latria; post-Vatican II, some felt Marian devotion needed rebalancing toward Christocentrism.

## Points of Agreement

- Both confess Mary as *Theotokos* / *Waladite Amlak* — Mother of God.
- Both affirm Mary's perpetual virginity — before, during, and after the birth of Christ.
- Both honor Mary as the greatest of saints and the supreme model of faith, obedience, and discipleship.
- Both hold that Mary intercedes for the faithful from heaven.
- Both celebrate major Marian feasts: the Annunciation, the Nativity of Mary, and the Dormition/Assumption (same date — August 15 / Nehasse 9).
- Both see Luke 1:48 ("all generations will call me blessed") as prophetic warrant for Marian veneration.
- Both see Mary as the New Eve and the Ark of the New Covenant, fulfilling Old Testament types.
- Both reject any worship (*latria*) of Mary; all honor is relative to Christ.

## Open questions

- Could the EOTC accept the Immaculate Conception if it were expressed in their own theological categories (e.g., Mary sanctified from the womb rather than "from the first moment of conception")?
- How close is the EOTC's Dormition tradition to the Catholic Assumption — is the difference merely one of timing (immediate vs. third-day assumption) or are there deeper theological divergences?
- Given the EOTC's intense Marian devotion, what would be the pastoral impact in Ethiopia if reunion with Rome required accepting the Immaculate Conception as defined in 1854?
