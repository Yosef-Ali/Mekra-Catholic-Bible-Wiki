## Catholic teaching
Prayer is a battle -- against self, against distraction, against the Tempter (CCC 2725).
The great spiritual masters (John of the Cross, Teresa of Avila, Ignatius of Loyola)
describe the "dark night" of purification and the disciplines needed to persevere in
prayer. The Church identifies three major obstacles: erroneous notions of prayer, a
mentality that reduces prayer to technique, and discouragement in dryness. The Compendium
(Q569-577) addresses the vigilance, humility, and filial trust that sustain prayer.
See [[teaching/prayer-combat]].

## Eastern Orthodox position
The Orthodox hesychast tradition (from hesychia, stillness) developed by Gregory Palamas
(14th c.) teaches that through ceaseless prayer, the mind descends into the heart and
the pray-er experiences the uncreated light of Tabor. The Philokalia is the classic
collection on spiritual combat. The Jesus Prayer ("Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have
mercy on me, a sinner") is the cornerstone of Orthodox spiritual warfare.

## Protestant positions
Protestant spirituality emphasizes "quiet time," Bible reading, and petitionary prayer
with less emphasis on ascetical combat. Puritan traditions (John Bunyan's Pilgrim's
Progress) frame the Christian life as warfare against sin. Pentecostals emphasize
"spiritual warfare" against demonic forces through binding prayers and deliverance.

## Where the differences come from
The Desert tradition (Antony, Pachomius, Evagrius) influenced both Egyptian Coptic and
Ethiopian monasticism before Chalcedon. Eastern hesychasm developed in Byzantium.
Western mysticism (John of the Cross, 16th c.) developed independently. The Reformation
moved away from monastic-ascetical paradigms toward domestic spirituality.
